[5] However, d’Entrecasteaux did not honour his chief engineer hydrographer, Charles Beautemps-Beaupré, by bestowing his name on any natural feature in this channel, albeit ‘he could not praise enough the zeal and intelligence of Beautemps-Beaupré’. This may be due to his official grade of ‘engineer’, rated before the revolutionary period below those of officers and botanists. Some other officers, such as Alexis-Ignace de Crestin (also spelt Cretin), who had directed several successful missions in Tasmania were not acknowledged with the award of a Tasmanian place name, possibly because Crestin was responsible (at least according to Labillardière’s narrative) for the tearing of the main topsail when he was master on the 2d April 1792. But the contributions of both Beautemps-Beaupré and Cretin were recognised later in the voyage with place names in New Caledonia and in New Guinea.

[6] Most of them honoured its commanders and officers: Cap Bruny*, île Bruny, Canal d’Entrecasteaux (D’Entrecasteaux Channel), île Huon*, Rivière Huon, Pointe Le Grand*, Pointe Rossel*, Cap Trobriand*, Pointe Gicquel*, Pointe Jurien*, Pointe Laignel* , Cap Deslacs* and Rochers d’Hippolite*, île Willaumez*, île St Aignant*, Pointe Renard*, Pointe Joannet*, Cap Raoul*.
Four toponyms referred to the savants on board, whether royalist or revolutionary: The botanist Labillardière on la Recherche and his colleague naturalist Riche on l’Espérance, as well as the gardener who worked with Labillardiere, Felix Lahaie / Lahaye: Pointe Labillardière*, Pointe Riche*, Cap Riche, île Lahaye*,
Two peaks closed to each other in Tasmania honoured two priests, the naturalist Ventenat and the astronomer Pierson: Pointe Ventenat* and Pointe Pierson).
