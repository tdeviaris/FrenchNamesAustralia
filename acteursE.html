Key Actors

The explorers and scientists of the d'Entrecasteaux and Baudin expeditions

These pages present the main actors of the two great French expeditions that mapped and named many places along the Australian coasts.

  • Nicolas Baudin
  • Charles-François Beautemps-Beaupré
  • Antoine Bruni d'Entrecasteaux
  • Louis de Freycinet
  • Charles-Alexandre Lesueur
  • François Péron

Nicolas Baudin

Nicolas Baudin, born in 1754 in Saint-Martin-de-Ré, was a distinguished French mariner and explorer. After several expeditions to the Indian Ocean and, notably, to the West Indies - the latter voyage aboard La Belle Angélique proving decisive in his selection - he was appointed by Bonaparte, in agreement with the scholars of the Institut national des sciences et des arts, to lead a major voyage of discovery to the Southern Lands. The expedition departed from Le Havre in October 1800 aboard two corvettes: Le Géographe, commanded by Baudin himself, and Le Naturaliste, under the command of Hamelin.

Between 1801 and 1803, the expedition collected over 100,000 specimens of natural history, produced more than 1,500 sketches and paintings, and mapped extensive portions of the southern and western coasts of New Holland (Australia), as well as parts of the eastern and northern coasts of Van Diemen's Land (Tasmania). During the voyage, Baudin met the British navigator Matthew Flinders in southern Australia, as both were exploring the continent's southern coastline.

Baudin died of tuberculosis in September 1803 on the island of Mauritius, on his return journey. His expedition left a significant scientific legacy, and many places in Australia still bear the names assigned at the conclusion of the voyage.

Charles-François Beautemps-Beaupré

Charles-François Beautemps-Beaupré, born on 6 August 1766 in La Neuville-au-Pont in Champagne and died on 16 March 1854 in Paris, is considered the father of modern hydrography. He was first employed as an unpaid student under his cousin Jean-Nicolas Buache, chief hydrographic engineer of the Marine Depot in 1783.

In 1791, he embarked on La Recherche under d'Entrecasteaux's command as the first hydrographic engineer to search for La Pérouse, who had been missing since 1788. From 1791 to 1796, he took advantage of the expedition to conduct surveys of the coasts of the countries visited, experimenting with new methods, particularly Jean-Charles de Borda's reflecting circle.

He established the foundations that made hydrography a true science, soon adopted by all navies. The Appendix to d'Entrecasteaux's voyage, containing all of Beautemps-Beaupré's work from this period and published in 1808, had considerable impact as it "revolutionized the hydrographic methods" used previously.

After returning to France in 1796, he was appointed deputy curator of the Depot of Marine Maps and Plans in 1799. He then proceeded to survey the French Empire's coastline, work that earned him entry into the Academy of Sciences in 1810. His contributions revolutionized marine cartography and precision navigation.

Antoine Bruni d'Entrecasteaux

Antoine Raymond Joseph de Bruni, Chevalier d'Entrecasteaux (8 November 1737 – 21 July 1793) was a French naval officer, explorer and colonial administrator. Educated at a Jesuit school, he was enrolled in the French Navy in 1754 by his father who opposed his religious vocation.

He is best known for leading the 1791-1794 expedition in search of La Pérouse, who had been missing for three years. Promoted to rear-admiral upon departure from Brest on 28 September 1791, he commanded two ships, La Recherche and L'Espérance.

D'Entrecasteaux was fortunate to have excellent officers and scientists, notably the hydrographic engineer Charles-François Beautemps-Beaupré, now regarded as the father of modern French hydrography. The expedition mapped in detail the coasts of Tasmania and Western Australia in 1792-1793.

D'Entrecasteaux died of scurvy on 21 July 1793. Although he did not find La Pérouse, his voyage produced important surveys of several Pacific islands, as well as reports on their vegetation, weather patterns, languages and cultures.

Louis de Freycinet

Louis Claude de Saulces de Freycinet, born in 1779 in Montélimar, was a French geographer and explorer of the 19th century. He participated in the scientific expedition to the Southern Lands (1800-1803) led by Nicolas Baudin, first as second officer to Captain Hamelin on Le Naturaliste, then commander of the schooner Casuarina.

During this expedition, he contributed to mapping the Australian coasts, producing "the first published map of the coasts of New Holland (Australia)". After Baudin's death, he worked with François Péron on writing the expedition narrative and publishing maps and atlases.

In 1817-1820, he led a circumnavigation expedition aboard the Uranie, accompanied by his wife Rose, disguised as a man. His scientific work was remarkable, collecting geographical, ethnographic and botanical data in different regions of the world.

Elected to the Academy of Sciences in 1825, he participated in the creation of the Geographical Society and published several important works on his voyages, thus contributing to the influence of French science and knowledge of the southern lands.

Charles-Alexandre Lesueur

Charles-Alexandre Lesueur (1778–1846) was a French naturalist, artist, and explorer born in Le Havre on January 1, 1778. Upon learning of preparations for the great expedition to the Southern Lands, he enlisted in 1800 as an assistant gunner, though Baudin quickly employed him to illustrate his logbook. Lesueur proved to be an exceptional draftsman.

After the desertion of artists due to difficult conditions at sea, he became one of the official draftsmen of the campaign, specializing in animal representation under the direction of zoologist François Péron, who taught him the fundamentals of his discipline. He executed some 960 drawings, not counting numerous sketches and color notes. A prolific natural-history collector, he gathered many type specimens in Australia, Southeast Asia, and North America.

After the expedition, Lesueur spent 22 years in the United States (1816-1837), where he contributed significantly to the scientific exploration of the American frontier and participated in Robert Owen's utopian community at New Harmony. He brought back a vast quantity of writings, drawings, and paintings.

Returning to France in 1837, he was appointed curator of the Natural History Museum of Le Havre in March 1846, but died suddenly nine months later on December 12, 1846, leaving a considerable artistic and scientific legacy.

François Péron

François Péron (1775–1810) was a French naturalist and explorer born in Cérilly. After an initial military career cut short by injury, he turned to the study of medicine and natural history in Paris. In 1800, he joined the Baudin expedition to the Southern Lands, embarking aboard the vessels Le Géographe and Le Naturaliste. Initially appointed as assistant zoologist on Le Géographe, Péron became the expedition's principal zoologist and anthropologist following the departure of several naturalists at Île de France (Mauritius) in 1801. He worked closely with the expedition's artist, Charles-Alexandre Lesueur (1778–1846), who produced drawings and watercolors of the specimens collected by Péron.

Following the death of Nicolas Baudin, Péron was entrusted by the Ministry of the Interior with preparing the official account of the expedition. Elected a corresponding member of the Institut in 1805, he published several scientific memoirs based on the collections brought back from the Southern Lands, contributing significantly to the development of foundational concepts in biology.

Péron died prematurely of tuberculosis in 1810, at the age of thirty-five. His work and research left a substantial scientific legacy, greatly enriching European knowledge of Australia and its natural environment.
