Péron gave a rather dull description of the cape and its location: ‘Cape Bernoulli which forms its southernmost point [of Lacepede Bay] is protected by very dangerous reefs, near which the depth fell to 6 fathoms, rocky bottom, and which we had much difficulty in doubling. To describe the nature of this new part of the south-west coast, it would be necessary to darken further (if that were possible) the miserable picture of sterility and monotony that New-Holland had unceasingly presented thus far. Nevertheless, the human species would seem to be fairly numerous on this coast- at least, that may be presumed from the great number of fires that we saw at the head of the Bay Lacépède. Countless flocks of Cormorants were settled on some islets near Cape Bernoulli and seemed to us be the sole occupants of those fearsome rocks.’

Further information: Baudin named the cape Cap Barré to indicate to navigator that access was barred by rocks [the Margaret Brock Reef].
