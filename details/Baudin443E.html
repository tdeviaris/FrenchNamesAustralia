Cap Levillain - Cape Levillain

Stanislas Levillain [spelled Le Vilain by Hamelin and Le Villain by Baudin] had already accompanied Baudin on an earlier expedition to the West Indies, aboard La Belle Angélique. He later embarked on Le Géographe as an assistant zoologist, before being transferred to Le Naturaliste, whose crew no longer included any zoological scientist after the voluntary disembarkation of Bory de Saint-Vincent and Désiré Dumont at Isle de France in early 1801.

Levillain had been deeply affected by the events that took place in Geographe Bay between 19 and 23 Prairial, Year IX (8-12 June 1801), marked by the disappearance of the sailor Vasse and the loss of his dog Kismy. In his journal, he reveals a very strong emotional response to the account of the wreck of Le Géographe's longboat and of the sailor's death, swept away by the surf after having already been rescued twice. He writes that his "heart is heavy" and that he cannot hold back his tears.

To this emotion at Vasse's death was added an even more intimate grief: the loss of Kismy, his dog, whom he had lent as a favour to Milius. Frightened by the surf, the animal could not be taken back on board and had to be left on the shore. Levillain describes him as his "most faithful friend", a gentle and affectionate animal loved by the whole crew. This loss was not only emotional; it also directly affected his work as a zoologist. Levillain stresses that a dog was indispensable for obtaining quadrupeds, marsh game, or aquatic birds shot down into the water. This page of his journal thus reveals a particularly personal sensitivity, in which grief for a sailor, attachment to an animal companion, and the practical needs of zoological collecting are closely intertwined.

It was therefore a Levillain already tested by death, loss and the practical difficulties of naturalist work in an unknown land who set out again, a few days later, on a new reconnaissance. On 28 Prairial, Year IX, Hamelin "dispatched the longboat under Midshipman Milius, accompanied by the zoologist Le Vilain and Midshipman Giraud, to reconnoitre the island [Garden Island? Carnac Island?] remaining to us in the southern part, and the extent of the reefs around it and between it and Rottnest Island." Hamelin specifies: "I had it supplied with provisions for two days, with orders to return this evening or tomorrow."

Levillain's mission report, together with the pages of his journal devoted to this reconnaissance, combines zoological observation, description of the natural environment, coastal survey and survival narrative. Having set out with the officer Milius, Levillain observed abundant wildlife, dominated by the "ours marins", that is, fur seals or sea lions, which the men saw, killed and consumed in large numbers. He also recorded the presence of crows larger than those of Europe, a buzzard, wild ducks, and a beautiful black oystercatcher, with red beak, eyes and feet, killed by Milius.

His observations also concern vegetation and terrain: fragrant woods, a kind of large tree-heath, abundant moss inside the forests, and sandy soil. Later, on the shore, he notes the discovery of a large piece of succin or amber, found near the remains of a large fish that he supposed to be a whale. These remarks reveal the eye of a zoologist attentive not only to the species encountered, but also to the material and natural traces left by the coastal environment.

The report, however, quickly becomes a narrative of extreme danger. After leaving a note in a bottle attesting to their passage on the island, the men put back to sea at about five o'clock in the evening. To avoid a vast chain of reefs, the longboat made for the mainland, but a storm arose during the night. The boat nearly capsized and filled repeatedly, while the exhausted boatmen remained asleep. Milius had the grapnel cast near a large sand cliff; the following day, the mainmast broke and, at about nine o'clock, the longboat was wrecked.

All the men managed to save themselves, but their situation soon became critical. The castaways had to find fire, water and food, and to survive for several days in an improvised camp, exposed to cold, hunger and fear of animals or of the inhabitants of the country. Milius's zeal nearly cost him his life when he tried to save the longboat, at a moment when the men no longer obeyed him. The weakened group searched among the plants for edible seeds, but nearly poisoned themselves with a seed which, when cooked, had the smell and taste of chestnut. Levillain was the most seriously affected: he suffered violent vomiting, risked death, and long afterwards continued to experience severe colic.

This ordeal takes on a particular intensity in his account because it awakened in Levillain the intimate and painful memory of a family shipwreck: his father and brother had perished at sea. The disaster experienced on this Australian coast was therefore not merely a maritime incident; it revived a personal memory of grief and gave the report a deeply human tone.

After several days of waiting, help finally arrived from the ship. The ship's workers repaired the longboat, while the castaways organized their camp, gathered shellfish, searched for water, kept fires burning, and in turn left in a bottle a notice recounting their shipwreck on this coast. When the longboat was finally refloated, after several hours of effort, the return to the ship was intensely emotional: the men came back on board with tears of joy, embracing their comrades, who had believed them lost. Through this report, Levillain appears at once as a zoologist attentive to the species encountered, an observer of the natural environment, and a direct witness to an experience of shipwreck that transformed a scientific mission into a survival narrative.

A few months later, Levillain died of dysentery. Baudin wrote in his journal that, on 23 December 1801, Hamelin informed him that Citizen Le Villain, who had embarked as a naturalist's assistant, had died after being struck by dysentery during his stay in Timor. Baudin described him as a "gentle, affable and good-natured" man.

On 5 February 1802, Baudin also referred to the spoliation of Levillain's collections by members of the crew. According to Péron's narrative, this episode enabled British officers, during Le Géographe's stopover at Port Jackson, to recover some of the rare specimens collected by the zoologist and send them to British museums, much to the displeasure of Péron, the Freycinet brothers and Ransonnet, and despite Baudin's request to Governor King for their return. However, among the 1,021 bird skins brought back by the Baudin expedition, Louis Dufresne, a taxidermist at the Muséum national d'Histoire naturelle, mounted several cormorants as well as many other Australian birds collected by Levillain and his colleagues Maugé and Lesueur.
