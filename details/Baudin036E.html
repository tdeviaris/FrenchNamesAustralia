Before the French Revolution, with the support of Louis XVI’s minister Anne Robert Jacques Turgot, Dombey was appointed to join the Spanish botanical expedition to Peru and Chile led by Hipólito Ruiz and José Pavón. Leaving Cadiz in 1777, Dombey travelled through the Andes, studied the local flora and assembled major botanical collections. He helped introduce European scholars to several South American plants, including araucarias, and gathered valuable information on the cinchona tree, whose bark, rich in quinine, was used in the treatment of malarial fevers.

His return to Europe was marked by misfortune. Part of his collection was captured at sea by the British and sent to the British Museum, while another major part was retained by the Spanish authorities and left to deteriorate in customs warehouses.

Dombey was also connected with one of the major scientific and political projects of the French Revolution: the creation and international diffusion of the new metric system. In 1794, he was sent to the United States to meet Thomas Jefferson and present the American authorities with the new French standards of measurement: a one-metre rod and a standard of mass then known as a “grave”, soon to be replaced by the kilogram. The mission was intended to promote a rational, decimal and universal system of measurement, based on natural units rather than on older local or customary measures. But Dombey’s ship was driven off course by a storm and then intercepted by British privateers. Taken prisoner on Montserrat, in the Caribbean, he died there in 1794, before he could complete his mission.

The link with François Péron is indirect but significant. In the Voyage de découvertes aux Terres australes, Péron names Dombey among the travellers and naturalists whom he regarded as especially reliable observers, alongside Cook, La Pérouse, Banks, La Billardière and Vancouver. This places Dombey within the scientific tradition to which Péron himself appealed: that of the travelling naturalist trained in close observation, systematic collecting and rigorous description of the natural world.

Dombey’s name therefore evokes the importance attached by the Baudin expedition to late eighteenth-century travelling scientists, but also to a new scientific culture shaped by the ambition to measure, classify and compare the world according to universal principles. Through Dombey, natural history, exploration, scientific collections and the revolutionary reform of weights and measures all come together.
