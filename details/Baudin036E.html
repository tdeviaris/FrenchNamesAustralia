Before the French Revolution and his mission to the United States, Joseph Dombey had also been entrusted with a mission by Louis XVI’s minister, Anne Robert Jacques Turgot, to take part in a Spanish expedition which left Cadiz for Peru and Chile in 1779. There he studied and collected botanical specimens and discovered species unknown to Europeans, such as the Araucarias (evergreen coniferous trees). He gathered valuable information on the cinchona tree, whose bark yields quinine used in the treatment of malaria. An important part of his collection was captured at sea by the British and sent to the British Museum, and another major part was seized by the Spanish government and left to rot in the customs houses. Péron expressed esteem for Dombey, and quoted him in a footnote that lists ‘the most rigorous observers – those least susceptible to enthusiasm or exaggeration’ including the names of Cook, La Pérouse, La Billardière, Vancouver, Banks etc…’

Further information: Baudin did not name this cape. Cape Dombey in the Northern Territory is also named after the naturalist.
