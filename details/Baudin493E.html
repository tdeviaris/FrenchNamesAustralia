Pierre Bouguer (1698-1758) belongs to that category of savants whose names, in the nomenclature of the Baudin expedition, remind us that the voyage of discovery was not merely an enterprise of coastal reconnaissance, but also the heir to a scientific culture closely linked to navigation, astronomy, geodesy, and naval construction. By giving his name both to a maritime entrance in Western Australia and to a cape exposed to the reefs and breakers of the southern coast of Kangaroo Island, the expedition’s cartographers associated the Australian coastline with the history of the sciences that had made such voyages possible.

Bouguer had distinguished himself in one of the great scientific enterprises of the eighteenth century. Together with Charles-Marie de La Condamine, also present in the expedition’s nomenclature, he was sent to Peru in 1735 to measure an arc of the meridian near the equator, between the regions of Quito and Cuenca. This expedition was intended to help determine the figure of the Earth and to settle the question of its flattening at the poles. Bouguer published his results in La Figure de la Terre, which appeared in 1749 and was based on the observations carried out with La Condamine in the vicinity of the equator.

Bouguer’s importance for a voyage such as Baudin’s also lay in his directly maritime work. His Traité du navire, de sa construction et de ses mouvemens (1746) offered a mathematical approach to the construction and stability of ships. In it, he notably developed the use of the metacentre, a theoretical point used to assess a vessel’s stability, a concept that became central to modern naval architecture. His studies of manoeuvres, hull forms, and the equilibrium of ships formed part of the scientific knowledge that the training of naval officers had to take into account.

From 1745 onwards, Bouguer was also called upon by the Academy of Sciences and by successive ministers of the Navy, notably Maurepas and Rouillé, to examine memoirs and projects concerning the calculation of longitude. He succeeded Maupertuis as the officer responsible for the improvement of the Navy. On this point, however, his authority had an ambivalent effect. Convinced that no clock was then accurate enough to determine longitude at sea, Bouguer considered projects based on marine watches and timekeepers to be doomed to failure. This position helped to slow the development of marine chronometry in France during the 1750s, at a time when Britain was making rapid progress in this field.

The presence of Bouguer’s name in Baudin’s nomenclature therefore encapsulates a characteristic tension within eighteenth-century nautical science. Bouguer embodies both the new power of mathematics applied to the sea, the measurement of the Earth, the theory of the ship, and the stability of vessels, and the limits of a learned expertise that could sometimes be reluctant to embrace technical innovation. For the officers and hydrographers of the expedition, however, his name evoked a foundational moment: the point at which navigation became a privileged field for the application of physics, geometry, and calculation.
