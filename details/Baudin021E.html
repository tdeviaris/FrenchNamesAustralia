In the chapter “Of Cannibals” in the Essays, Montaigne recounts the encounter in Rouen between the young King Charles IX [11 years old] and three men from Brazil. The king spoke with them at length, while they were shown “our ways, our pomp, the form of a fine city”. When asked what had most struck them, the visitors first expressed surprise that grown men, strong and armed, should accept being ruled by a child rather than choosing one of their own number to command. They were also struck by the deep social inequalities they observed in France: some men lived in abundance, while others, hungry and destitute, begged at their doors.

Through this episode, Montaigne reverses the usual gaze cast upon so-called “savage” peoples. It is no longer the Amerindians who appear barbarous, but the Europeans themselves, unable to recognise the violence, injustice and irrationality of their own society.
