Ventenat was a native of Limoges in central-western France. He became a Catholic priest with an interest in natural history. Backed probably by his brother, Etienne, who was an active member of the Societé d’Histoire Naturelle in Paris, he joined the expedition as a chaplain and collaborated with Labillardière in the collection of plants. Besides his botanical activities, Ventenat played also the flute, and Labillardière reported that during the expedition’s second visit to Van Diemen Land, aboriginals enjoyed listening to him playing music on his instrument. This was not the case with Saint-Aignan* when he played violin. In his journal, Ventenat described also ‘the pretty women’ of Van Diemen. During his return voyage to France, Ventenat contracted a high fever and died on the Isle de France *. He has been honoured by having a species of a tropical plant, Peperomia ventenatii, named after him.
