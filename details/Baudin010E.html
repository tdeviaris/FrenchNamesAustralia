The names Cape Marengo, Cape Desaix, Marengo Island, and Desaix Island form part of a single commemorative ensemble, directly linked to the Battle of Marengo. During that battle, in 1800, the Austrians initially gained the advantage and seemed close to victory. The arrival of General Louis Charles Antoine Desaix (1768–1800), with reinforcements and a decisive counter-attack, nevertheless reversed the course of the battle in favour of the French. His intervention helped secure one of the most celebrated victories of the Consulate, although he himself was killed there. It was in this context that the cape neighbouring Cape Marengo received the name Desaix.

The victory of Marengo, won in extremis and often regarded as one of Napoléon’s victories that came closest to defeat, was elevated in public memory to the rank of a brilliant triumph. It enabled Napoléon Bonaparte to consolidate his position after the coup d’état that had brought him to power as First Consul the previous year. The heroic interpretation of the battle formed part of a broader propaganda strategy, even though it simplified a much more uncertain military reality. Berthier, who commanded the Army of Reserve and whose name was also inscribed in the nomenclature of this sector (see the Berthier Islands in South Australia, of which Marengo Island forms part), played an active role in shaping this propaganda narrative by reinterpreting, in the Relation de la bataille de Marengo published in 1804, the retreat of the French army as a calculated manoeuvre intended to deceive the enemy, whereas this was not in fact the case.

Desaix’s death on the battlefield was likewise amplified in order to construct the image of the Napoleonic hero sacrificing his life for the nation. According to later accounts, his body, concealed by the smoke of battle, was not found until nightfall, stripped by looters and identified only by his long hair. The combined glorification of Marengo, Desaix, and the commanders associated with them thus contributed to the elaboration of a political language of heroism and sacrifice, intended to strengthen Bonaparte’s image and to help reconcile the French after the divisions of the Revolutionary period. These names accordingly became enduring symbols, inscribed in the nomenclature of the voyage. Cape Marengo in particular stands out among Baudin’s place names for having been retained by Péron and Freycinet, which suggests the lasting force of its symbolic and unifying significance.
On Baudin’s sketches and coastal profiles, the cape is spelled Maringo.
