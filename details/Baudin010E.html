Cape Desaix is now within the boundaries of the Great Otway National Park, home to a diverse range of wildlife habitats and 43 endemic species. Cape Marengo sits just at the edge of the park.

Desaix’s death on the battlefield was amplified to create the image of a Napoleonic hero who had sacrificed himself for his nation. Likewise, the French victory at Marengo—snatched from the brink of defeat—was celebrated as a brilliant triumph, reinforcing Bonaparte’s authority. This carefully crafted narrative of heroism and victory helped reconcile and unite the French after the turbulent years of the Revolution. Both Desaix and Marengo became enduring symbols, reflected in the voyage’s nomenclature. Cap Marengo, in particular, stands out among Baudin’s toponyms for having been retained by Péron and Freycinet, highlighting its lasting unifying and symbolic significance.On Baudin’s sketches and coastal profiles, the cape is spelled Maringo. On a lighter note: According to legend, after the Battle of Marengo, Bonaparte was overcome by hunger. He asked his personal chef, François Claude Guignet, known as Dunan, to prepare him a meal. However, after the battle, the chef's kitchens had been damaged. Dunan had to make do with what he had. He found: a chicken olive oil tomatoes garlic fried eggs crayfish bread croutons He fried everything together and served this improvised dish to Napoleon, who loved it. He often asked for more, calling it poulet marengo, before the recipe was modified to replace the chicken with veal, which was considered more refined.

An island in South Australia also bears the name of Marengo.
