Île Colbert – Colbert Island

The name Colbert belongs to a family of state nobility. One of its ancestors, Jean-Baptiste Colbert (1619–1683), became Louis XIV’s principal minister. Extending the policy of Richelieu*, Colbert helped equip France with a powerful royal navy and merchant fleet, notably through monopoly trading companies. He became known as “the great Colbert.”

At the time when Péron and Freycinet were working on their nomenclature, Auguste François-Marie de Colbert-Chabanais (1777–1809), a descendant of this lineage, had distinguished himself as an officer during the Italian and Egyptian campaigns before rising to the rank of general in the Grande Armée. Awarded the Legion of Honour*, he stood out in several major Napoleonic battles, including Marengo*, Saint-Jean-d’Acre*—where he was very seriously wounded—Austerlitz*, and Jena*. His name was later engraved on the Arc de Triomphe. In Napoleonic propaganda, Auguste Colbert came to embody the ideal of bravery and sacrifice associated with the officers of the Grande Armée. Before departing for Egypt, he wrote to his mother: “Amid my hopes and my joy, eager to fulfil the great promise of my youth, I send to France—and to you, my dear mother—a patriotic remembrance. This is my moral testament...”

Auguste François-Marie de Colbert-Chabanais had two older brothers, both of whom also chose military careers. All three Colberts rose to the rank of general.

It remains difficult to know with certainty which member of the Colbert family Péron and Freycinet had specifically in mind. However, insofar as Bonaparte rewarded Auguste François-Marie and, after his death at the Battle of Cacabelos on 3 January 1809, regarded him as a model of bravery for the nation, it is reasonable to assume that he is the figure whom Péron and Freycinet chiefly intended to honour. Today, however, it is above all Jean-Baptiste Colbert who remains familiar from history textbooks.
