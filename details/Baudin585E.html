Baie Voltaire - Blanche Harbour

François-Marie Arouet, known as Voltaire (1694–1778), took his pen name from a Latinized anagram of “Arouet l(e) j(eune)” — “Arouet the Younger” — at a time when the letters U and V, as well as I and J, were still typographically interchangeable. He was a writer and philosopher, and one of the leading figures of the French Enlightenment. He wrote tragedies, tales, and historical and philosophical works.

His writings were later collected in the multi-volume Œuvres complètes de Voltaire. Voltaire was also an outspoken defender of individual rights against the arbitrariness and abuses of royal and religious authority. On several occasions, the critical and satirical views expressed in his works led to his imprisonment in the Bastille or to periods of exile.

In Napoleonic France, Voltaire remained a powerful symbol of French intellectual prestige. Napoleon Bonaparte held in high regard Voltaire’s historical biography of the Swedish king Charles XII, Histoire de Charles XII (1731), a work noted for its concern with historical accuracy and the use of testimony from people who had known or encountered Charles XII. It is said that, as a student, Bonaparte secretly read extracts from the book during his German lessons, and that he later selected it for his camp library during the Egyptian campaign.

Napoleon was also drawn to some of Voltaire’s most frequently performed plays, including Zaïre, Œdipe, La Mort de César, and Mahomet, works associated with themes of Antiquity, power, religion, and the Orient. These plays formed part of the repertoire of the Comédie-Française during the First Empire and could acquire a political or diplomatic resonance. For example, during the meeting between Napoleon and Tsar Alexander I at Erfurt, held from 27 September to 14 October 1808, Voltaire’s Œdipe was reportedly chosen by the Emperor as part of a cultural programme intended to display, before Europe, the friendship between France and Russia. When the actor Talma declaimed Voltaire’s celebrated line, “L’amitié d’un grand homme est un bienfait des dieux” — “The friendship of a great man is a gift from the gods” — it was understood as being addressed above all to Tsar Alexander I, who was present in the audience.

In this context, the use of the name Voltaire for a coastal feature in Australia can be understood as part of the wider French national and intellectual culture of the Napoleonic period. The toponym did not merely commemorate an Enlightenment writer: it also evoked a figure who, by the early nineteenth century, had come to embody French philosophical independence and the prestige of French literature abroad.
