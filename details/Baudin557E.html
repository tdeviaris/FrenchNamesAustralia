Île Tournefort – Tournefort Island

Cap Tournefort – Cape Tournefort

In his Élémens de botanique ou méthode pour connaître les plantes, Tournefort developed a classification based chiefly on the configuration of flowers, arranging 10,146 species into 698 genera. He classified plants according to their different parts — leaves, roots, stems, corolla forms, fruits, and scent — and encouraged collectors “to gather together plants that resemble one another, and to separate them from those that do not.” This method, which left a lasting mark on the history of botany, helped establish him as one of the major learned authorities of the Jardin du Roi.

In his section “On the Habitat of Seals,” Péron cites Tournefort in relation to contemporary naturalist debates on the evolution of their distribution. He first refers to a scholar who held that “the Mediterranean, the Black Sea, and the Caspian once formed a single and the same sea, populated by these animals…”. He then adds: “Other naturalists, on the contrary, with Tournefort at their head, think that the Mediterranean, whose level was formerly higher than we see it today, broke through the mountains of the Bosphorus and formed the Caspian by rushing over Asia like a dreadful torrent. Thus the Ocean once overthrew its barriers between Calpe and Abyla in order to form the Mediterranean itself” (vol. II, p. 353). This reference shows that, for Péron, Tournefort remained an important authority not only in botany, but also in learned debates concerning the physical history of the globe and the distribution of species.

Tournefort died accidentally and was succeeded by Bernard de Jussieu (1699–1777).
