Pointe Coulomb - Coulomb Point

Baie Coulomb - Coulomb Bay

Charles-Augustin de Coulomb's work significantly advanced the understanding of electricity, magnetism, and mechanics, particularly in relation to friction and torsion. His contribution to physics was later summarised by the physicist Jean-Baptiste Biot, who wrote:

"It is to Borda and Coulomb that we owe the birth of true physics in France - not a verbose and hypothetical physics, but an ingenious and exact science that observes and compares everything with rigour."

His research on compass needles and their magnetisation, as well as on friction in rope systems, was of particular importance to nautical science. He established the quantitative laws of electrostatic and magnetic attraction, now known as Coulomb's laws.

In 1802, Napoléon Bonaparte appointed Coulomb to a commission tasked with reorganising the French educational system, and he also held an administrative role within the ministry of Antoine François de Fourcroy.

Today, the coulomb is the SI unit of electric charge, defined as one ampere per second.
