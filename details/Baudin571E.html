Île Condillac – Condillac Island

Condillac gave a central place to questions of pedagogy and method in the formation of the mind. His Cours d’étude pour l’instruction du prince de Parme served in particular as a point of reference in the reflections that accompanied the establishment of the new educational system after the French Revolution. The work had been designed to educate the young Ferdinand of Bourbon, the future Duke of Parma, in the spirit of an enlightened ruler of the eighteenth century: a prince able to govern with reason and method, to understand law, economy, and society, and to avoid the errors of a regime founded solely on tradition. Ferdinand, however, proved little receptive to his tutor’s methods.

Among his other major works are the Traité des animaux (1755), in which he argued that the actions of animals are linked to their sensations, their needs, and the association of their ideas, and the Traité des sensations, in which he imagines a statue gradually endowed with the sense of smell and then with other human faculties such as memory and sensibility.
