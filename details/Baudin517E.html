Lagrange corresponded with Euler (see Cape Euler) and developed the “calculus of variations.” He applied his method to mechanics and published the Mécanique analytique. He was also in contact with the mathematician Laplace and, when his treatise on analytical mechanics was submitted to the Académie des sciences, Laplace was a member of the committee responsible for approving its publication.

For twenty years, Lagrange worked at the Berlin Academy of Sciences and repeatedly won the annual prize awarded by the Académie des sciences in Paris for his work on the motion and stability of the planets and the solar system.

In 1787, after the death of the Prussian king Frederick II, Lagrange settled in France. During the French Revolution, he was appointed president of the Commission of Weights and Measures. In this capacity, he is regarded as one of the fathers of the metric system and of the adoption of the decimal division of measures.

The execution of the scientist and chemist Lavoisier deeply distressed Lagrange and his colleagues. He is said to have exclaimed:

“It took them only an instant to cut off that head, and a hundred years may not produce another like it.”

Lagrange also contributed to the development of the principles of differential calculus, showed that algebraic analysis could be used to solve equations, and introduced the mathematical term “derived function,” from the Latin derivare, to describe a function derived from another.
