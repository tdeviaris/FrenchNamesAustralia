Baie Geoffroy - Geoffroy Bay

In his Note sur les animaux vivans venus à bord du Géographe, Étienne Geoffroy Saint-Hilaire does not offer a detailed zoological study. Rather, he provides an inventory of the living animals landed from the corvette. The list consists mainly of mammals and birds, to which are added thirty-two "freshwater and land tortoises". Only at the end of the inventory does Geoffroy make an important clarification: most of the seventy-two living animals that arrived aboard the Géographe had not come directly from New Holland. They had either been given to the French government by Jansens, Governor-General of the Cape, or acquired at Île-de-France, present-day Mauritius, through the efforts of Captain-General Decaen.

Geoffroy states that, "with the exception of the Kanguroos, the Casoars émiou[s] and the Perroquets", all the other animals were of African or Mauritian origin. In other words, the animals that can be most directly connected with New Holland, that is, Australia, were the kangaroos, the "casoars émiou[s]" and the parrots.

In the inventory, Geoffroy distinguishes two kangaroos. The first is a "Kanguroo géant", or giant kangaroo, which he describes as measuring more than one and a half metres. He adds that this animal had not survived "the fatigues of the journey" and that Georges Cuvier had studied its internal anatomy. The second is named the "Kanguroo de King". Geoffroy explains that this species, so named by François Péron, had not yet been described and had been found on King Island, at the entrance to Bass Strait.

This "Kanguroo de King" should, however, be interpreted with caution. It does not necessarily correspond to a species now recognised as being unique to King Island. The name seems instead to reflect the place of collection indicated by Péron. Unlike the King Island emu, a now extinct island form that is clearly identified today, no kangaroo or wallaby strictly endemic to King Island is generally recognised in current taxonomy. The "Kanguroo de King" should therefore probably be understood as a historical designation, linked to the early European classification of Australian macropods.

Geoffroy also mentions two "Casoars émiou[s]". He specifies that these were the "helmetless cassowary of New Holland" and refers to Commodore Phillip, who had first made the bird known to Europeans. This expression does not refer to a cassowary in the modern sense, but to the Australian emu (Dromaius novaehollandiae). The terminology reflects the still uncertain state of zoological nomenclature in the early nineteenth century: European naturalists then associated the emu with cassowaries, both being large flightless running birds, while noting that the emu was "helmetless", unlike the true cassowary.

Thus, despite the composite character of the living cargo carried by the Géographe, Geoffroy's inventory allows us to identify several animals directly associated with Australian fauna: the "Kanguroo géant", the "Kanguroo de King", the two "Casoars émiou[s]", that is, emus, and the parrots of New Holland.
