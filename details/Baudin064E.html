Nemours was also the name of Pierre Samuel du Pont de Nemours (1739-1817), who left France in 1799 and settled in the United States where his son, Eleuthère Irénée du Pont, established a black powder manufactory in Delaware, the start of an industrial empire. During the Napoleonic period Pierre Samuel du Pont returned to France where he served in several diplomatic positions and was a member of the Institut. He was involved in the negotiations which led to the Louisiana Purchase. He was also known for his transcription of the nightingale’s song into verses (this bird and its song symbolized love and the renewal of life in springtime). When Napoléon left the island of Elba, he returned to the United States. However, it is unlikely that the place was named after him, as the other names of golfe Joséphine were all related to women.

Further information: As for the other French names in the Gulf, the name Cap Nemours was not retained by the English colonisers. The current designation, Marino Rocks, may derive from one of two sources: either from two landmarks familiar to Charles Kingston, an Irish immigrant who arrived in South Australia aboard the Cygnet in 1849. Kingston was born in Bandon, County Cork, near Marino Point, a notable promontory in Ireland; or from the Kaurna word ‘marana’, meaning ‘place of the hand,’ which is linked to the Tjilbruke Dreaming story. The site also served as a traditional resting place for the Kaurna people.
