Volume 2 of the published account of the voyage pays tribute to Pierre-François Bernier, the expedition’s astronomer, who died prematurely during the return journey. The text emphasises both his scientific abilities and his personal qualities:

“Not only did everyone see in M. Bernier a learned and hardworking astronomer; but those who had lived in close intimacy with him regretted above all his character, full of gentleness and modesty, and his amiable and obliging manners. Born in La Rochelle on 19 November 1779, Pierre-François Bernier became, from the age of sixteen, the pupil and friend of an able astronomer, M. Duc-la-Chapelle of Montauban, who inspired in him a taste for astronomy and gave him his first instruction in it. He was scarcely seventeen when he made observations worthy of being printed, and these were indeed promptly inserted in the Connaissance des temps. Jérôme Lalande brought him to Paris and, knowing that Bernier had little fortune, lodged him in his own house and, with a disinterested generosity that does honour at once to master and pupil, provided him with every means of perfecting his education. On 5 August 1800, the commission of the Institut selected him as one of the two astronomers appointed to join the expedition to the Southern Lands; and, although his health was not very robust, he eagerly accepted a post that required great zeal and diligence, but promised the observer an abundant harvest of work and glory. I shall not recount all that he did during the voyage, whether for astronomy, geography or physics: it is to the work itself, in which all these materials have been brought together, that one should turn, as to the numerous and incontestable titles Bernier earned to the esteem of scholars and the gratitude of navigators. He died at the age of twenty-three years, seven months and seventeen days...”

During the voyage, Bernier contributed not only to astronomical observations, but also to work in geography and physics. The geographical operations preserved in the archives, such as those carried out at King George Harbour, give an idea of the measurements he performed in the field: determining positions, taking angular readings, calculating latitude and longitude, and providing scientific support for hydrographic surveys.

It was no doubt both the quality of his work and the generosity of his collaboration with the other members of the expedition that explain why his name was given to several places visited or charted by it: Bernier Island, Cape Bernier in Tasmania, and Cape Bernier in Western Australia.
