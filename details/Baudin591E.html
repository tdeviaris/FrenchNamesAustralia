Pierre-François Bernier was one of the two astronomers initially appointed to the Baudin expedition, alongside Frédéric de Bissy. Bissy, however, gave up the voyage at the Isle de France, before the survey of the Australian coasts had truly begun. Initially assigned to the Naturaliste, Bernier was later transferred to the Géographe. His presence on board reflects the growing importance of astronomy in late eighteenth-century voyages of reconnaissance. Astronomers were not only expected to observe the heavens, but also to determine longitudes, check the performance of chronometers, fix geographical positions with precision, and contribute directly to the making of maps.

Born in La Rochelle into a family affected by financial misfortune, Bernier grew up in Montauban, where his father made considerable sacrifices to provide him with a solid education. He showed an early interest in science. His vocation as an astronomer began with his meeting with Duc-Lachapelle, a highly skilled amateur astronomer who was himself connected with Lalande. Under his guidance, Bernier acquired both theoretical and practical training: he observed Mercury, learned how to reduce observations, and became familiar with astronomical calculations. His qualities as an observer and calculator impressed Lalande, who had some of his observations published in the Connaissance des Temps.

Bernier arrived in Paris in early 1800 to complete his training under Lalande, but he spent only a few months at the observatory of the Collège de France. This short stay nevertheless allowed him to learn nautical astronomy and the use of navigational instruments. He therefore embarked on the Naturaliste with genuine competence, but also with fragile health. Lalande had in fact tried to dissuade him from joining the expedition, believing that he would be more useful to astronomy in less physically demanding work. Bernier nevertheless insisted on taking part, probably motivated by the desire to learn, to distinguish himself, and to make a name for himself.

His work during the voyage was essential, even if it did not always take the form of spectacular discoveries. At sea, Bernier checked the regularity of the chronometers and measured lunar distances in order to determine longitude. On land, whenever circumstances allowed, he set up a temporary observatory, sometimes no more than a simple tent, to carry out operations of astronomical geography. His observations were therefore not only part of “pure” astronomy: they served navigation, hydrography, and cartography directly.

The long stay at Port Jackson, from June to November 1802, was especially productive. The colony already had a tradition of astronomical observation, notably through the observatory established by William Dawes. Bernier set up his own observatory on the eastern side of Sydney Cove, between today’s Opera House and the Botanic Garden, a few hundred metres from Dawes’s former observatory. In these more stable and favourable conditions, he was able to carry out important observations, including the transit of Mercury across the Sun on 9 November 1802.

Bernier also observed the solar eclipse of 4 March 1802, which was also observed by Matthew Flinders, as well as a lunar eclipse on 19 March 1802, useful for determining local longitude. He also gathered valuable observations on aurorae australes, tides, and the local components of the magnetic field. Although his contribution to theoretical astronomy was limited by the conditions of the voyage, his observations were crucial to the geographical success of the expedition, particularly in fixing more accurately certain points on the western coast of Australia and in Van Diemen’s Land.

Bernier’s death, during the night of 5–6 June 1803, gives his career a tragic dimension. Exhausted by privations and weakened by illness, he died at sea before reaching the age of twenty-four. Appreciated by both scientists and officers, he was mourned throughout the expedition. The second volume of the voyage narrative paid him a warm tribute, noting that “everyone saw in M. Bernier not only a learned and hardworking astronomer, but those who had lived in close contact with him especially regretted his gentle and modest character, and his kind and obliging manners…”

His name remains attached to several places today, including Bernier Island, at the entrance to Shark Bay, Cape Bernier in Western Australia, and another Cape Bernier on the eastern coast of Tasmania. These place names recall the discreet but decisive role of a young astronomer whose observations helped provide a scientific foundation for the maps produced from the Baudin voyage.
