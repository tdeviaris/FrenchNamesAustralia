Cap Duhamel - Cape Duhamel

Duhamel created a school to train future shipbuilders. The syllabus of the school drew on the publications of Bézout’s textbook (see Île Bézout, in WA) and Bouguer’s Traité du Navire (1746) (see cap Bouguer, in SA), that emphasised the acquisition of scientific expertise which the school would deliver.

The candidates were selected by examination. Every two years they had to pass further exams to advance to higher classes. The training extended over a period of six years, with the last two taught in “l’École de Paris” (now the ENSTA, the oldest French school for engineers).

Duhamel himself published a fundamental and substantial treatise for students that he planned to be “purely practical and even elementary”: Éléments de l'architecture navale, ou Traité pratique de la construction des vaisseaux. He also wrote a treatise on the means of maintaining the good health of crews and improve the hygiene in the hospitals where the sick members of the crews were treated. He suggests technics to improve the circulation of air and recipes to make healthy meals (based on rice or spelt, ground semolina, sauerkraut mixed with a ragout of vegetables and fish stock, etc.) and beneficial spirituous drinks.
