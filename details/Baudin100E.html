Turenne’s memory was long transmitted through heroic military narratives, funeral orations and song. The piece known as Monsieur de Turenne, with its martial rhythm accompanied by drums, fifes and trumpets, belongs to this tradition. It is often associated with an air attributed to Jean-Baptiste Lully, in the context of Louis XIV’s military policy, particularly in Alsace. Under the Empire, Napoleon’s soldiers sang these couplets and refrains during their long marches, celebrating military movement, victory and the pride of conquering armies.

This glorious memory must nevertheless be set against the more violent realities of seventeenth-century warfare. Turenne’s campaigns, especially in Alsace and around Turckheim, involved destruction, forced requisitions and violence against civilian populations. Such practices were not unique to the French armies: from the Thirty Years’ War to the wars of Louis XIV, European powers commonly resorted to scorched-earth tactics, pillage, intimidation and collective punishment. This does not lessen the brutality of these acts, but it helps place the violence attributed to Turenne within a broader European military system in which civilians were often among the principal victims.

Fléchier, who delivered the funeral oration for Turenne, declared that “the whole kingdom mourns the death of its defender.” This formulation helped establish the image of a national hero, while leaving aside the violence committed or tolerated during his campaigns. The contrast between the funeral praise, the military song and the realities of war illustrates how Turenne’s memory was constructed, selected and embellished.

Freycinet retained the name Turenne from his earliest draft, whereas several other names proposed by him or by Péron for this sector were later changed. In the final nomenclature, a bay, a cape and an island in South Australia were named after Turenne. This repeated use reflects Bonaparte’s admiration for the commander, whose remains he had transferred to Les Invalides, a site dedicated to the glories of the French military.
