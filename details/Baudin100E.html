Turenne Island - Franklin Islands (western island)

The memory of Turenne was long transmitted through heroic military narratives, funeral orations and song. The musical piece known as Monsieur de Turenne, sung to a martial rhythm accompanied by drums, fifes and trumpets, belongs to this tradition. It is often associated with a tune attributed to Jean-Baptiste Lully, in the context of Louis XIV's military policy, particularly in Alsace. Under the Empire, it is quite possible that these couplets and refrains, celebrating marches, victories and the pride of armies, still circulated among Napoleon's soldiers.

This glorious memory must nevertheless be placed within the broader history of wartime violence against civilian populations. Turenne's campaigns, particularly in Alsace and around Turckheim, were accompanied by destruction, forced requisitions and violence against local inhabitants. Such practices were not specific to the French armies, nor to the seventeenth century alone: in many conflicts, belligerent powers resorted, to varying degrees, to devastation, pillage, intimidation, reprisals and collective punishment.

In the Europe of the Thirty Years' War and the wars of Louis XIV, these forms of violence belonged to a military system in which armies largely lived off the territories they crossed or occupied, placing a considerable share of the cost of war upon civilians. These practices did not disappear at the turn of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. The Revolutionary and Napoleonic campaigns, notably in Italy and Egypt, were also marked by requisitions, imposed contributions, confiscations and, in some cases, by pillage and violence against local populations.

This does not in any way lessen the brutality of the violence associated with Turenne's campaigns, but it helps to place it within a long history of war, in which civilians were very often the first victims of military operations, logistical necessities and policies of occupation or domination.

The official funeral oration delivered by Flechier - see Cape Flechier - also contributed to this construction of memory, declaring that "the whole kingdom mourns the death of its defender." Such formulas helped to fix the image of a national hero, while leaving unspoken the violence committed or tolerated during his campaigns. The contrast between funeral praise, military song and the realities of war thus illustrates the way in which the memory of Turenne was constructed, selected and embellished.

Freycinet retained the name Turenne from his earliest drafts, whereas several other names proposed by himself or by Peron for this sector were subsequently altered. In the end, a bay, a cape and an island in South Australia received the name Turenne. This insistence reflects Bonaparte's admiration for this military commander, whose remains he had transferred to Les Invalides, a site dedicated to the military glories of France.
