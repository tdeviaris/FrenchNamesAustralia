Cap Caffarelli - No known English name (Coorong National Park)

Baie Caffarelli - No known English name (Nuyts Archipelago)

In this letter, Louis Marie Maximilien de Caffarelli du Falga addressed a request for leave to the Minister of War in order to rejoin his family and recover from the wound in which he lost his left leg at the crossing of the Nahe, after being struck by a cannonball on 17 December 1795. This injury, which left him one-legged, was followed by a long period of confinement to bed. At the time, Caffarelli was serving as battalion commander and deputy director of fortifications with the Army of Sambre-et-Meuse, under the orders of Jean-Baptiste Kleber and later Francois Severin Marceau.

Promoted brigadier general, he nevertheless continued to serve with a wooden leg. He enjoyed the particular esteem of Napoleon Bonaparte, who admired both his courage and his abilities. During the siege of Saint-Jean-d'Acre (see Cape Mont-Tabor), he lost his right arm and died of his wounds on 27 April 1799.

He was also an associate of the Institut National des Sciences et des Arts.

Baudin named the cap Cap Basse Terre. A bay in South Australia is also dedicated to Caffarelli.
