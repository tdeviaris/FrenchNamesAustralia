The medical men of the Baudin expedition

The doctors and surgeons who sailed with the Baudin expedition played an essential, though often discreet, role in the success of the voyage. The two corvettes carried several men trained in medicine or naval surgery: François-Étienne L’Haridon de Créménec, chief medical officer aboard the Géographe; Hubert-Jules Taillefer, a young surgeon also assigned to the Géographe; and Jérôme Jean-Claude Bellefin, medical officer aboard the Naturaliste. François Péron, officially appointed as a student zoologist, also had medical training and sometimes acted in an unofficial medical capacity. Despite the frequent separation of the ships, these men were consulted collectively in serious cases.

Their presence on board belongs to a period of profound transformation in French medicine. Bellefin and L’Haridon had been trained before or at the beginning of the Revolution, in the tradition of naval medical and surgical schools. Taillefer and Péron belonged more clearly to the younger generation shaped by revolutionary medical reforms and by the rise of clinical teaching in Paris, Strasbourg and Montpellier. These differences in training partly explain the variety of their observations and of their interpretations of the illnesses encountered during the voyage.

At the beginning of the expedition, their duties were not exclusively medical. Like many practitioners embarked on voyages of discovery, they also took part in exploration, plant collecting, environmental observation and, at times, natural history work. Their role, however, soon became crucial when the crews were struck by the major diseases of long-distance seafaring: scurvy, dysentery, tropical fevers, and conditions linked to exhaustion, privation and climate. The period at Timor and the navigation along the Australian coasts were particularly trying. The stay at Port Jackson, by contrast, allowed the crews to recover through medical care, rest and the return to a fresher diet.

Bellefin occupies a special place in this group. As medical officer of the Naturaliste, he seems to have enjoyed Hamelin’s confidence; Hamelin described him as an “excellent surgeon” and requested his promotion. Bellefin’s relations with the other practitioners also appear to have been good: he and L’Haridon consulted each other on several occasions and acknowledged one another’s competence. For several reasons, the health record of the Naturaliste was better than that of the Géographe, and Baudin himself seems to have wished to secure Bellefin’s services for the continuation of the voyage from Port Jackson.

During the Naturaliste’s stay at the Baie des Chiens-Marins, now Shark Bay, Bellefin had to deal with severe diarrhoea among the men who remained ashore to repair the ship’s boat. Péron reports that, despite tents and woollen blankets, most of them suffered from “abundant diarrhoea”, which Bellefin attributed to the extreme contrasts in temperature between day and night. This explanation reflects the medical ideas of the time, which placed great importance on the effects of climate, humidity, atmospheric variations and sudden environmental changes on European bodies.

Aboard the Géographe, the situation was often more critical. L’Haridon had to treat severe episodes of scurvy and dysentery, but his relations with Baudin were difficult. The commander was at times highly critical of him, even though L’Haridon gave him constant care when Baudin was gravely ill. The expedition seems to have left deep marks on him: after his return to France, he defended a thesis on scurvy, then died in tragic circumstances in 1807, with a contemporary letter referring to suicide and linking his melancholy state to the voyage around the world under Baudin.

Taillefer, much younger, represents another figure of the expedition’s medical world. Trained in the intellectual climate of the new Parisian clinical medicine, he distinguished himself by the precision of his observations on dysentery. In his thesis published after the voyage, he gave detailed descriptions of symptoms and even mentioned autopsy findings, evidence of a more clinical and anatomopathological approach. Southwood and Simpson particularly emphasise his careful examination of patients and his relative reluctance to resort to bleeding, still a common practice at the time.

Péron, although not an official ship’s doctor, occupies a more ambivalent position. His medical training, combined with his skills as a zoologist and anatomist, enabled him to take part in some consultations. After the expedition, he also published texts on naval hygiene and tropical dysentery. His interpretations, however, remained shaped by the climatic theories of his time: he stressed humidity, heat, excessive perspiration and certain dietary habits, while showing strong distrust of fruit and acidic drinks in tropical regions.

These practitioners did not, of course, possess modern knowledge of the causes of scurvy, dysentery or malaria. The role of vitamin C in scurvy, the microbial causes of dysentery and the causes of malaria were unknown to them. Their explanations may therefore appear inaccurate or insufficient today. Yet it would be unfair to judge them only by present knowledge. In extreme conditions — long passages at sea, deteriorating food, tropical heat, humidity, fatigue, isolation and high mortality — they tried to observe, compare, treat and understand. Their post-expedition writings show an effort to turn the painful experience of the voyage into medical knowledge.

This medical dimension is also visible in the expedition’s toponymy: Baie Lharidon and Isthme Taillefer, on the Péron Peninsula, and Cape Bellefin, in the southern part of Shark Bay. Within the geography of Shark Bay itself, these names preserve the memory of the role played by the surgeons in the survival of the crews.

The absence of François-Nicolas-Auguste Collas from this nomenclature is revealing. As the expedition’s pharmacist, he performed a function essential to the health of the crews: preserving remedies, preparing treatments and managing medical substances. If he was not honoured with a place name, this was probably due to his lower rank within the naval and scientific hierarchy. In this sense, the nomenclature reproduces, at least in part, the hierarchy of titles: it preserves the names of the most visible figures while leaving in the shadows essential actors who occupied lower positions in the institutional order.
