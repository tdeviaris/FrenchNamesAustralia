Golfe Joséphine - Gulf St Vincent

Îles Joséphine - part of the Nuyts Archipelago

Born Marie-Josèphe Rose Tascher de La Pagerie (1763-1814), Joséphine is chiefly known by the name given to her by her second husband, Napoléon Bonaparte. She was also known as Joséphine de Beauharnais, after her first husband, Alexandre de Beauharnais, who was guillotined during the Terror in 1794. She was Empress of the French from Napoléon’s coronation in 1804 until their divorce in 1809.

A woman of considerable influence, Joséphine welcomed savants and travellers to La Malmaison. They supplied her with exotic plants and animals, which could be admired by her visitors, including foreign sovereigns. Her collections expressed French interest in newly explored lands, the natural world, and distant societies. She enlisted the botanist Étienne-Pierre Ventenat - brother of the naturalist Louis Ventenat - to care for the plants and seeds, to document them, and to commission illustrations from Pierre-Joseph Redouté.

In his Jardin de La Malmaison, Ventenat described plants from New Holland brought back by the Baudin expedition and cultivated in the greenhouses at La Malmaison, including Josephinia imperatricis. He noted that it was “a herbaceous plant […] raised from seeds from New Holland brought back by Captain Hamelin, and flowering in midsummer”. In a footnote, he explained that the honour of dedicating a genus to the august Empress of the French had required the authorisation of the founder of the garden at La Malmaison. This modest tribute was intended to attest to the enlightened protection that Her Majesty extended to the sciences, and to the distinction she gave them.

Other notable arrivals from New Holland at La Malmaison included a kangaroo, Kanguroo kingiensis, taken aboard by Baudin at King Island - two other specimens had died during the voyage - and two emus, one from King Island and the other from Kangaroo Island, belonging to two species now extinct. Black swans were also introduced and bred in France; Joséphine was especially fond of them. Her first lady’s maid, Mlle d’Avrillion, recalled that, as the allied armies approached in 1814, the Empress was forced to leave La Malmaison and feared that her black swans, which had just produced young, would be killed by foreign soldiers. They were ultimately spared.

Further information. Baudin initially named the gulf Golfe de la Mauvaise (“Nasty Gulf”), because of the fatigue it had caused the whole crew. When the first charts of this coast were drafted, however, he renamed it Golfe de la Misanthropie - perhaps in allusion to Molière’s comedy The Misanthrope.
The present name, Gulf St Vincent, was chosen by Matthew Flinders on 30 March 1802 in honour of the Earl of St Vincent, who had succeeded Lord Spencer as First Lord of the Admiralty.
