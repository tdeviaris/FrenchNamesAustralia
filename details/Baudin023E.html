René Descartes is famous for his philosophical statement “I think, therefore I am”, first expressed in the Discourse on the Method (1637). The phrase became emblematic of a mode of thought that sought to ground knowledge on a first certainty, reached through doubt and reason.

Descartes was at once philosopher, mathematician and natural philosopher. Alongside Pierre de Fermat, he contributed to the rise of analytic geometry, and his work left a lasting mark on European intellectual history. Although his physics and cosmology were later largely displaced by Newtonian mechanics, his name continued to stand for the ideal of a science founded on method.

During the Napoleonic period, Descartes still appeared as a tutelary figure of reason, method and French science. His prestige resonated with the intellectual world of the Institut national des sciences et des arts, to which several members of the expedition were connected.

The choice of Descartes's name for a bay, a cove and an island thus belongs to a learned geography in which the southern lands also became a site for honoring the great figures of European thought.
