René Descartes is famous for his philosophical statement “I think, therefore I am”, first expressed in French as “Je pense, donc je suis” in the Discourse on the Method, published in 1637. Written in French rather than Latin, this work was a landmark in the history of European thought. It argued that the search for truth should be grounded in the disciplined use of reason, doubt and reflection.

Descartes was not only a philosopher, but also a mathematician and scientist. Together with Pierre de Fermat*, he played a decisive role in the development of analytic geometry, which connected algebra and geometry. His scientific cosmology, especially his theory of vortices and subtle matter, was later superseded by Newtonian physics. By the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, French scientific culture was dominated by Newtonian celestial mechanics, above all through figures such as Laplace, Lagrange, Monge, Fourier and others.

Yet during the Napoleonic period, Descartes remained a tutelary figure of method, reason and French scientific thought. Phrases such as “I think, therefore I am”, “It is not enough to have a good mind; the main thing is to use it well”, and the ambition to become “as masters and possessors of nature” could resonate strongly with the values of the Institut: clarity of analysis, rational organisation of knowledge, the public usefulness of science, and the ambition to place the exploration of the world within an intelligible order.

The choice of Descartes's name for a bay, a cove and an island therefore belongs to a learned geography that associated the exploration of the southern lands with major figures of thought, science and reason.
