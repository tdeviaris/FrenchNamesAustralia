Pierre André de Suffren de Saint-Tropez, bailli de Suffren (1729–1788), was a French admiral renowned for his campaigns against the British in the Indian Ocean, where his audacity and energy earned him the nickname “Admiral Satan” among his enemies.

Napoléon held Suffren in very high esteem and is reported to have said to Las Cases, the French naval officer who joined him in exile on Saint Helena: “Why did this man not live for me? I would have made him our Nelson, and affairs would have taken another turn.” Suffren understood at every stage the importance of coordinating naval and land operations and of acting with speed and decision in war.

Las Cases, however, offered a more ambivalent portrait of the admiral. He wrote that “M. de Suffren, very harsh, very eccentric, extremely selfish, and bad-tempered, a poor friend, was loved by no one, but esteemed and admired by all. He was a man with whom one could not live; above all, he was very difficult to command, obeyed little, criticised everything, continually railed, for example, against the uselessness of tactics, yet, when necessary, proved himself the best of tacticians.” Many naval officers also disliked Suffren and nicknamed him le gros calfat, a mocking reference both to his physical appearance and to what they regarded as his coarse manners.
