Cap Frézier - Cape Frezier

Assigned to Saint-Malo under the chief engineer Jean Siméon Garangeau, Frézier specialized in coastal fortifications and naval engineering, while also distinguishing himself through a number of technical innovations. Between 1712 and 1714 he was sent on a reconnaissance mission to the Spanish ports of Chile and Peru. He visited Concepción, Valparaíso, and Callao, charted Cape Horn and the Strait of Magellan, and gathered strategic intelligence on colonial defenses, trade networks, and natural resources.

At the same time, in the manner of the Enlightenment savant, he pursued observations in botany, zoology, ethnology, mineralogy, and physics. He notably introduced to France Chilean strawberry plants later acclimatized in Brittany.

Appointed chief engineer in Saint-Domingue (1719-1725), he drew the plan of the town of Saint-Louis, now Saint-Louis-du-Sud in Haiti, designed several forts, and produced a general map of the colony and its anchorages. Later named chief engineer at Landau in the Rhineland Palatinate, he strengthened Vauban's fortifications and designed a military hospital, while also supervising works at Philippsburg.

In 1739 he became chief engineer of the fortifications of Brittany, overseeing major urban and defensive works in Nantes, Brest, Port-Louis, Concarneau, Morlaix, and Saint-Malo. Among his most notable achievements was the completion, between 1741 and 1745, of the Château du Taureau in the Bay of Morlaix. Promoted lieutenant-colonel in 1756, he retired in 1764, leaving behind a career that exemplified the Enlightenment synthesis of scientific culture, global observation, cartographic precision, and military engineering.
