Cap Legendre - Cap Legendre

Legendre’s Éléments de géométrie, first published in 1794, was one of the fundamental works studied by officers, engineers and geographers of the period. Widely used in higher scientific education, the treatise provided an essential foundation for the practices of measurement, navigation and cartography. The second edition, published in 1799 and enlarged with a section devoted to trigonometry, was especially well suited to the training of men responsible for surveying coasts, calculating positions and producing maps. Trigonometry and triangulation did indeed lie at the heart of hydrographic practice: from a few accurately determined reference points and the precise measurement of angles, they made it possible to establish the positions of capes, islands, bays and hazards in relation to one another, and then to plot them accurately on the chart.

Legendre also served as an examiner in mathematics at the École polytechnique. In that capacity, he assessed young candidates destined for the scientific and technical branches of the state; it is possible that he examined Charles-Pierre Boullanger during the 1795 entrance examinations.

A member of the Institut, in the mathematics section, Legendre was later elected, in 1813, to the Bureau des longitudes, a central institution in the development of French astronomy, navigation and geodesy. His name, given by the Baudin expedition to an island in the Dampier Archipelago and to a cape in Spencer Gulf, therefore evokes a mathematical culture that directly supported the work of hydrographers: measuring angles, establishing triangulations, calculating distances and transforming the observation of the coastline into cartographic representation.
