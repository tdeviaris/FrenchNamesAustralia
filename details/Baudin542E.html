The Freycinet brothers, Henri Freycinet (1777-1840), known as “the elder”, and Louis-Claude de Saulces de Freycinet (1779-1842), known as “the younger”, embarked with the rank of sub-lieutenant, the former aboard the Géographe and the latter aboard the Naturaliste. Both were promoted to lieutenant in 1803.

Louis-Claude de Saulces de Freycinet (1779-1842) began the voyage at the age of twenty-one, with the relatively modest rank of sub-lieutenant, aboard the Naturaliste. During the expedition, he was promoted to acting commander of the schooner Casuarina, purchased by Baudin at Port Jackson in order to allow the geographers to approach the coasts more closely. Before joining the Baudin expedition, Louis Freycinet had entered the French Navy in 1794 and received military training. He saw action in the Mediterranean when he took part in Admiral Bruix’s campaign in 1795. It was, however, during the voyage to Australia that he truly developed his hydrographical and cartographical skills, learning from the geographers Faure and Boullanger. These qualifications led to his being entrusted with editing the historical and geographical atlases of the expedition, which in turn enabled him to promote his own scientific circumnavigation aboard the Uranie (1817-1820). During this later voyage, Freycinet revisited several parts of the Australian coast, notably Shark Bay and Port Jackson/Sydney. This second voyage nevertheless proved difficult for its commander, being marked, among other hardships, by two shipwrecks.

Louis Henri de Saulces de Freycinet, known as Henri Freycinet, the elder brother of Louis Freycinet (1777-1840), also embarked as a sub-lieutenant aboard the Géographe. He was promoted acting lieutenant at Timor on 20 October 1801, and lieutenant on 5 March 1803. After Baudin’s death in September 1803, Péron expressed the opinion of the officers and savants when he wrote that “it was expected that the command of the Géographe would remain with M. Henri Freycinet, first lieutenant of the corvette. His work throughout the voyage, in both geography and astronomical observation, seemed further to strengthen the claims he already possessed as a naval officer. However, Admiral Linois considered himself obliged to appoint Captain Milius… his seniority in rank assured him of the command” (vol. II, pp. 251-252). Henri Freycinet later reached the rank of rear admiral in 1828 and served as governor of Île Bourbon (Réunion) from 1821 to 1826, then of French Guiana (1827-1829) and Martinique (1829-1830).

The Freycinet brothers belonged to a Protestant family, which may help explain the relatively high number of place names referring to Protestant figures in the nomenclature.
