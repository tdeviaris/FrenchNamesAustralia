Cap Faujas - Point Hillier

The work of Barthelemy Faujas de Saint-Fond belongs to a formative moment in the history of the Earth sciences. In the late eighteenth century, geology was only beginning to emerge as an autonomous discipline, out of mineralogy, natural history and field observation. It was then shaped by a major debate between Neptunists and Plutonists. The former, associated above all with Abraham Gottlob Werner, explained most rocks as deposits formed in the waters of an ancient universal ocean; within this framework, basalt could be interpreted as a sedimentary rock. The latter, by contrast, emphasised the role of internal heat, molten matter and volcanic activity in the formation of rocks.

Faujas's research on the extinct volcanoes of the Vivarais, the Velay and the Massif Central helped strengthen this second interpretation. By showing that the basalts of these regions were linked to ancient eruptive phenomena, he contributed to the recognition of volcanism as a major geological force. His conclusions were confirmed by his contemporary Deodat de Dolomieu, a French geologist also present in the expedition's nomenclature, who had studied volcanoes in Italy and Sicily and regarded basalts as products of volcanic activity.

It is possible that Freycinet or Boullanger recognised, or at least rightly suspected, the volcanic nature of the hill they designated Piton Faujas. The term piton could suggest an outcrop or summit of volcanic appearance. The toponym would thus honour one of the pioneers of French volcanic geology.

In 1793, Faujas held the first chair of geology at the Museum national d'Histoire naturelle. His writings and teaching thus mark the transition from a science still largely descriptive and rooted in mineralogy to a field-based geology concerned with the physical history of the globe, its relief, strata, rocks and the processes that had formed them.

Faujas was also, like many savants of his time, associated with the political and cultural transformations of the Revolutionary period. When the French armies occupied the Netherlands in 1794, Andre Thouin and Barthelemy Faujas de Saint-Fond took part in the transfer of Dutch natural history collections to Paris.
