Jean-Guillaume Bruguière occupies a significant place in late eighteenth-century natural history through his work on invertebrates, molluscs, and fossil shells, which provided an important intellectual framework for later observers such as François Péron. Trained initially in medicine, Bruguière devoted himself to the study of living and fossil invertebrates, combining extensive field experience with theoretical reflection.

In his contributions to the Encyclopédie méthodique and the Journal d'Histoire naturelle, he articulated a coherent vision of Earth history grounded in the horizontality of strata, the continuity of geological processes, and the comparison between fossil and extant species. This approach places him among the earliest thinkers who may be associated with uniformitarianism, that is, a reading of Earth history based on the prolonged action of observable natural phenomena.

Bruguière defended the fixity of species while insisting on the constancy through time of the anatomical features that made them identifiable. He therefore considered that fossil shells could be reliably compared with living forms. His attention to even subtle morphological differences led him to reject the idea of excessive variability within a single species. He nonetheless recognised a central difficulty: the differing geographical distributions of fossil and living species that nevertheless appeared analogous. This question, which he left unresolved, seemed to him crucial to any understanding of Earth's past.

This comparative method profoundly informed Péron's interpretation of fossil shells encountered on the Australian coasts, including large tridacnas and zoophytes observed at elevations well above present sea level. Like Bruguière, Péron read these remains as evidence of former marine conditions and long-term geological continuity rather than catastrophic upheaval. Bruguière's emphasis on invertebrates as privileged witnesses to Earth's history thus provided Péron with both a methodological model and a conceptual reference for his geological and palaeontological reasoning in the southern hemisphere.
