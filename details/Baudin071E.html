Pointe Dorothée - Port Clinton

Émilie du Châtelet took a particular interest in the work of Leibniz and Newton, whose competing theories formed one of the major scientific debates of her time. In her Institutions de physique, dedicated to her son as both an intellectual legacy and a pedagogical work, she set out Leibnizian physics with precision. Recognising the importance of Newton’s work, she later undertook the substantial task of translating his Principia Mathematica into French. This translation remains, to this day, the only complete French version of the work. Voltaire was her companion and intellectual collaborator for around ten years.

The name Dorothée may also refer to one of the heroines in La Femme auteur, a short story by Madame de Genlis published in 1806, which evokes the difficulties faced by women seeking to pursue intellectual ambitions.

Another possibility is that the point was named after Catharina of Württemberg (1783-1835), born Friederike Catharina Sophia Dorothea of Württemberg, daughter of King Frederick I of Württemberg. Through her marriage to Jérôme Bonaparte (see Dorothée Island), she became Napoleon’s sister-in-law and Queen Consort of Westphalia.

Further information: When Freycinet and his crew reached the head of Gulf St Vincent, they hoped to find the mouth of a major river. Instead, they encountered low, waterlogged shores with no substantial estuary. In his notebook, Boullanger, the geographer of the Baudin expedition, recorded on 15 and 16 January 1803: “The head of this first gulf ends in such shallow ground that, even in only two or three fathoms of water, we could see on the shores nothing but drowned trees [in fact, Avicennia marina].”
Neither Boullanger nor Freycinet could have foreseen that these apparently uniform, waterlogged margins were in fact a mangrove environment of considerable ecological richness, whose scientific significance remained unknown to European observers in the early nineteenth century.
Today, this mangrove, within the Upper Gulf St Vincent Marine Park, forms with the Light River Delta one of South Australia’s most ecologically intact mangrove and saltmarsh systems, although it is now vulnerable to algal blooms and various forms of pollution.
