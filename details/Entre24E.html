The isthmus which has given the bay its name, is a narrow strip of dune sand about five km in length and a little over a hundred meters wide at its narrowest point. It connects the northern and southern parts of Bruny Island and separates d’Entrecasteaux Channel from Adventure Bay and the Tasman Sea. The bay was first discovered by lieutenant de Cretin and his party, in May 1792. His report prompted d’Entrecasteaux to send lieutenant Saint-Aignan accompanied by the hydrographer Beautemps-Beaupré to survey it and explore its surroundings. Saint-Aignan described it as ‘a vast basin’ (p. 90) and confirmed the existence of the isthmus. He notes in his report: ‘We were, however, in the vast haven discovered by Mr Cretin … I could not resist the desire to find out if I could discover the sea on the other side … the boat grounded at a great distance from the shore on a sandbank … Despite the severity of the weather, I did not hesitate to undress and jump out of the boat; holding a gun in one hand and a compass in the other, I wadded to the shore. As I reached it, I could hear the surf breaking violently on the other side. Crossing a strip of land, a cable length in width, I found myself on the coast of Adventure Bay’. D’Entrecasteaux records that ‘Isthmus Bay offered the boat’s crew a large quantity of fish and shellfish, such as rays, small mullets, cod, mussels, oysters etc., but the ample supply of swans made them despise/disregard this kind of nourishment’.
