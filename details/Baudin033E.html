Îles de Rivoli - Rivoli Islands

In the commemorative nomenclature composed by François Péron in honour of Napoleon Bonaparte, the name Rivoli naturally imposed itself. It is thus found in three Australian toponyms: a bay, a cape, and a group of islands.

The Battle of Rivoli, won on 14 and 15 January 1797 — 25 and 26 Nivôse Year V — under the command of General-in-Chief Bonaparte, was regarded as one of the most brilliant victories of the first Italian campaign. It brought the Austrian offensive in northern Italy to an end and helped open the road towards Austria for the Republican armies. The memory of this victory was rapidly disseminated through prints and commemorative imagery, as shown by the 1797 engraving entitled Bataille de Rivoli les 25 et 26 nivôse an 5, held by the Bibliothèque nationale de France.

Rivoli thus occupied a central place in the making of Bonaparte’s military glory. Masséna’s intervention, whose division reached the battlefield after a forced march, contributed decisively to the French success; in 1808, Napoleon granted him the title Duke of Rivoli in memory of his role in the victory. The name was also given to the great Parisian thoroughfare opened under the Consulate along the Tuileries and the Louvre: the Rue de Rivoli, so named in 1804 in memory of Bonaparte’s victory over the Austrians.

Péron’s choice therefore did not merely identify a coastal feature, a headland, or an island group. By substituting Rivoli for the names proposed by Baudin, he inscribed the Australian coast within a French national narrative shaped by the Revolutionary Wars, military victory, and the emergence of the cult of Bonaparte. The bay thus became a site of Napoleonic memory, transported into the geography of the southern hemisphere.

This logic contrasted sharply with that of Nicolas Baudin. Baudin had, for example, named the bay Baie de Quiberon, after the Breton peninsula, and had given a reef within the bay the name La Chaussée. These names followed a more geographical and nautical logic: Quiberon evoked a familiar coastal form in the French landscape, while La Chaussée described a dangerous shoal, a kind of rocky pavement. According to Péron, this reef was sighted at the very last moment, when Le Géographe was at risk of running aground, with consequences that might have been disastrous for the crew. The outer part of this reef, together with other rocky outcrops, now forms part of Ringwood Reef.

Rivoli’s place in Napoleonic memory was further confirmed in 1806, when Napoleon had a commemorative column about eighty feet high erected on the battlefield. The monument was destroyed by the Austrians in 1814. This official commemoration helps explain why the name Rivoli found such a natural place in Péron’s list of names chosen to honour Bonaparte.
