After surveying the lower reaches of the Derwent River and Ralphs Bay, the party led by Lieutenant Willaumez explored the south-eastern shores of the Derwent Estuary where the river sheds its waters into Storm Bay. In their open boat they followed the coast of the peninsula which encloses the southern half of Ralphs Bay. It is puzzling why they named it Presqu’île du Nord ( ‘peninsula of the North’), given that an opposing arm of land separates the Derwent Estuary from the northern part of the bay. Their naming may relate to the location of the peninsula close to the northern end of the d’Entrecasteaux channel. Today the southern peninsula (Presqu’île du Nord) is known simply as South Arm and its counterpart, across the narrow entrance to Ralphs Bay, as North Arm. A small island to the southeast of South Arm was named in honor of one of the officer of the d’Entrecasteaux expedition, Jean-Baptiste Philibert Willaumez (1763-1845). He was born on a small French island, Belle- île-en-Mer, off the coast of Britany, in western France. Île Willaumez has since been renamed Betsey Island. Willaumez records in his logbook that he put to sea on 14 February 1793. On the 19th, the weather remained overcast with rain until two in the afternoon, and the sea, heavy and confused, pressed hard upon their small craft. Forced to tack and shorten sail, they sought shelter near the island now called Betsey, before standing on again. By reefing their sails and pulling hard at the oars, they succeeded in clearing the rocks, rounding two capes that d’Entrecasteaux would later name Cap des contrariétiés and Cap de la Direction, and made for the strait, where Huon received them aboard the Espérance. Thence they rejoined La Recherche, to the great relief of d’Entrecasteaux, after five days absent. ANF/MAR/5JJ/5/B piece 422 Willaumez had already sailed as pilot under d’Entrecasteaux’s command on Le Patriote in 1790-91 and, with the rank of Enseigne / sub-lieutenant, had joined d’Entrecasteaux on his expedition in search of La Pérouse. During the voyage, d’Entrecasteaux promoted him to lieutenant de vaisseau and gave him responsibility for the maintenance of the ships’ timekeepers. Willaumez, who was republican, was consensual, and seemed to have also good relationships with his colleagues on all sides. He became with Rossel, who was royalist, one of the expedition officers who were familiar with astronomical angles and positions. To take the distances between the sun and the moon, or the sun at its highest elevation and measure latitudes from the elevation of the sun, he had a sextant in copper made by the instrument maker Dollon. However, according to Labillardière, its use was challenging as the measurement had to be done in a very uncomfortable position. It was also partly inaccurate as Willaumez ‘discovered a cause of error in the calculation, that one would have hardly suspected.:The radii of this instrument, though very heavy, were still too slender and bent when the instrument was pressed with any degree of force against the breath, thereby causing disruption in the parallelism of the mirrors’. Hence, to measure angles, Willaumez chose to make more and more use of the reflective circle where cross observations allow errors to be rectified (see Pointe Pierson*). He probably used the one he had received from Louis XVI as a reward for his astronomical skills on l’Astrée in 1786. He worked collaboratively and complementaritaly with Beautemps-Beaupré* who was also an adept of the reflexive circle method.
