René Louiche Desfontaines (1750–1833) was, like his colleague Jussieu*, one of the influential naturalists of his time. Both worked at the Jardin du Roi, renamed after the Revolution as the Jardin des Plantes and incorporated into the Muséum national d’Histoire naturelle. Before the Revolution, Desfontaines travelled in Africa for two years, from which he brought back significant botanical collections and an important herbarium.

During the Revolution, he took part in the establishment of the Muséum and in the unification of botanical knowledge. He organised the collections brought back from various voyages into a single general herbarium, classifying specimens according to Jussieu’s system of plant families. When Le Naturaliste in 1803, and Le Géographe in 1804, brought back part of the herbarium collected during the Baudin voyage, these collections were deposited at the Muséum. Desfontaines, then Professor of Botany, incorporated them into the general herbarium and formally described several Australian species, including eucalypts, shrubs such as Diplolaena grandiflora, and other plants.

In his Histoire des arbres et arbrisseaux qui peuvent être cultivés en pleine terre sur le sol de la France, Desfontaines strongly recommended the acclimatisation, preferably in the south of France, of eucalypts from New Holland and Van Diemen’s Land.
