Cap Malouet - Cape Malouet (Western Australia)

Pierre-Victor Malouet (1740-1814) was a French colonial administrator, politician and senior official of the Navy whose career underwent a significant revival under Napoleon. A former monarchien deputy to the Estates-General and an émigré during the Revolution, he was struck off the list of émigrés and recalled to service by Bonaparte. From 1803, he was sent to Antwerp as commissary-general of the Navy, and then as maritime prefect, responsible for supervising defensive works, organising the port and building up a fleet.

This position placed Malouet at the heart of Napoleonic naval policy. Antwerp was intended to become one of the great arsenals of the Empire, capable of supporting the French maritime effort against British power. Malouet represented there a form of administrative expertise inherited from the Ancien Régime, redeployed by the imperial regime to rebuild and strengthen the French naval apparatus. His career thus illustrates the way in which Napoleon could recall experienced administrators to his service, even when they came from politically moderate or monarchien circles, provided that their skills could serve the state.

The link with the Freycinet brothers gives Malouet's presence in the voyage's nomenclature a particular resonance. In 1804-1805, Henri de Freycinet was at Antwerp, where he received several instructions signed by Malouet. A letter from Malouet to Henri de Freycinet, sent from Antwerp on 19 August 1805, even mentions Louis de Freycinet's poor health and the need to grant him one month's rest. It is therefore possible that Louis de Freycinet met Malouet at Antwerp, or at least that he was known to him within the services of the Navy. This hypothesis must remain cautious, but it provides a concrete institutional context for the appearance of the name Malouet in the nomenclature.

The fact that Malouet later fell into disgrace should not obscure the significance of his name at the time when Baudin's nomenclature was being prepared. Around 1806-1807, Malouet was still a useful and important figure in the imperial naval administration, especially through his functions at Antwerp. His later disgrace in 1812, linked to his political independence and suspected royalist sympathies, reveals the ambiguity of his position: he was both a servant of the Napoleonic state and a man whose monarchien background made him politically suspect. The presence of his name in the nomenclature therefore refers less to a stable ideological allegiance than to the pragmatic world of imperial administration, in which experienced men of the Ancien Régime and the Revolution could be temporarily reintegrated into the service of the Empire.

Malouet was also a man of letters. He published his poems, memoirs and several works of political and historical reflection, among them the Considérations historiques sur l'empire de la mer chez les anciens et les modernes, published in Antwerp in 1810. This work, devoted to maritime power in history, further reinforces the relevance of his name in the nomenclature of a voyage of discovery: Malouet appears not only as a naval administrator, but also as an author attentive to the relationships between empire, commerce, the navy and command of the seas.
