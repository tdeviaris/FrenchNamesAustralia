Île La Caille - Ile La Caille

In November 1750, the Académie royale des sciences sent Lacaille to southern Africa to undertake an extensive programme of astronomical and geodetic observations. After reaching the Cape of Good Hope in April 1751, he established a small observatory on the shores of Table Bay. Using a modest refracting telescope, he systematically recorded the positions of nearly 10,000 southern stars and also compiled a catalogue of 42 nebulae and star clusters. This work provided astronomers and navigators with a far more accurate representation of the southern sky than had previously been available.

His Planisphère contenant les constellations célestes comprises entre le Pôle austral et le Tropique du Capricorne, published in 1756, gave cartographic form to these observations. Together with his astronomical tables and his work on the lunar-distance method for determining longitude at sea, this chart belonged to the body of knowledge used by astronomers and naval officers on scientific voyages. Such resources helped them to determine a ship's position more accurately and to plot the capes, bays, islands and other coastal features encountered during exploration.

Lacaille's work also formed part of the gradual adoption of Newtonian celestial mechanics by eighteenth-century French astronomers. Newton's theory explained the motion of celestial bodies through universal gravitation and progressively displaced the Cartesian theory of vortices. This scientific transition provided part of the theoretical background to the astronomy practised by the scientists and navigators of the Baudin expedition (see Île d'Alembert).
