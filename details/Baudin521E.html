Cap Gantheaume - Cape Gantheaume

Honoré Joseph Antoine Ganteaume (1755-1818), whose name was spelled with an "h" - Gantheaume - by Péron and Freycinet, was one of the most prominent French naval officers of the Revolutionary and Napoleonic period. He served during the American War of Independence and later in the Indian Ocean under Suffren.

His career was subsequently closely linked to the Egyptian campaign. He was aboard the flagship L'Orient at the Battle of the Nile in 1798 and narrowly escaped death when the ship exploded. He also took part in the siege of Saint-Jean-d'Acre, the combat of Gaza and the attack on the fort of Aboukir.

In 1801, Bonaparte entrusted him with a strategic mission: to lead a French squadron from Brest to the eastern Mediterranean in order to assist the French army in Egypt. The expedition, however, was interrupted by damage to the ships, shortages of replacement equipment and the presence of British forces, especially those of Admiral Warren. In a letter dated 9 Ventôse Year IX - 28 February 1801 - Bonaparte, while maintaining a diplomatic tone, clearly showed his displeasure. He implicitly reproached Ganteaume for having approached Mahon too closely, thereby risking alerting Warren, and urged him to repair the situation, regain the initiative and fulfil the mission entrusted to him by the government.

“Find the remedy.”

Translation of the letter:

Appendix no. X

9 Ventôse, Year IX
[28 February 1801]

To Rear-Admiral Ganteaume

You will find enclosed, Citizen-General, a copy of the treaty that has just been concluded with the King of Naples. You may communicate its contents, but you must not have it printed.

I have already informed you that I would have been displeased had you come too close to Mahon and thereby alerted Admiral Warren, whose apparent mission was to guard Mahon. But at present the only question is to find the remedy.

I am impatiently awaiting the return of the courier I sent you, so as to learn your position and the strength of the enemies who are blockading you.

I do not doubt that you are convinced that the major interest of the Republic and the glory of your squadron lie in fulfilling the mission entrusted to you by the Government. My confidence in you remains unchanged.

Let me know the definitive course of action that circumstances lead you to take.

Bonaparte

Archives nationales, AF IV - 862.

Ganteaume was appointed vice-admiral in 1804 and Count of the Empire in 1810. That same year, in the context of the rebuilding of the Imperial Navy after the disaster of Trafalgar, he submitted to Napoleon a memorandum on the recruitment and training of naval officers and crews. It included a proposal for a flotilla of 300 vessels to protect the French coasts.

Yet Napoleon retained a harsh opinion of him. At Saint Helena, he later delivered a severe assessment that contrasts sharply with the honours the Empire had bestowed upon him.

“Ganteaume was nothing but an incapable and resourceless sailor.”

Freycinet also invoked Ganteaume in a letter to Decrès dated 8 June 1812 in order to justify the nomenclature of Kangaroo Island, then named Île Decrès. He explained that the island's principal bays and capes had been used to commemorate men who had contributed either to the progress of nautical science or to the administration of the Navy. In that framework, Ganteaume appeared, after the Minister himself, as the most prominent naval officer whose name deserved to be retained.

Translation of Page 3 of Freycinet's letter to Decrès, Paris, 8 June 1812:

As for the map of Île Decrès, you must dedicate it to yourself: the purpose of the particular nomenclature of this island was to bring together, on the principal bays or the most prominent capes, the names of men who, through their work, have contributed most to the progress of nautical science, and of men who have distinguished themselves in the Ministry of our Navy. The name of Gantheaume also seemed to deserve a place there as that of the most prominent general officer after that of the Minister who directs all branches of this part of the public force.

I believe it necessary to observe to Your Excellency that the admiral's name has also been applied to an island ten leagues in circumference, located on the northern coast of New Holland, at about 120° longitude. Now, Sir, if it seems appropriate to you to remove the name Cape Gantheaume from the nomenclature of Île Decrès, it would be all the easier to substitute another for it, since Mr Péron, in the first volume of the historical account of the Voyage, made no mention of it.

I could replace it with the name Cape Jean Bart.

Changes of the kind I am proposing to Your Excellency could, strictly speaking, be made to a large number of other points; but if, independently of the serious disadvantages that would arise from direct opposition to the work already published by Mr Péron, Your Excellency wishes to consider the expenses and the...

Marginal note: Since Gantheaume has an island, I am quite willing to keep him in mine.
