The career of Lazare Nicolas Marguerite Carnot illustrates the close interweaving of the scientific and political spheres at the end of the eighteenth century. The coup d’État of 18 Fructidor Year V (1797), which forced him into exile because of his political positions, led to the vacancy of his seat at the Institut, which was promptly filled by Napoléon Bonaparte. Re-elected in 1800 under the Consulate, and excluded again in 1816 during the reorganisation of the Institut, Carnot thus embodies a figure whose scientific trajectory remained inseparable from the institutional and political reconfigurations of his time.

Carnot’s scientific work is particularly noteworthy. A student of Gaspard Monge, he drew inspiration from the works of Jean le Rond d’Alembert and Pierre Louis Moreau de Maupertuis. From 1783, in his Essay on Machines in General, he sought to develop a general theory of machines by taking as his starting point the principle of the conservation of “living forces,” which he considered a constant quantity in nature, capable of transformation but neither created nor destroyed.

During his political exile in Germany, he wrote Réflexions sur la métaphysique du calcul infinitésimal (1797), in which he sought to clarify the principles of infinitesimal analysis. He examined the manner in which differential and integral calculus had been constituted as an operational tool, while comparing it with other mathematical approaches, such as the methods of exhaustion, indivisibles, and indeterminate quantities. He writes:

“I seek to determine what constitutes the true spirit of infinitesimal calculus. The reflections I propose on this subject are divided into three chapters: in the first, I set out the general principles of infinitesimal analysis; in the second, I examine how this analysis has been reduced to an algorithm through the invention of differential and integral calculus; in the third, I compare this analysis with other methods that may substitute for it, such as the method of exhaustion, that of indivisibles, and that of indeterminate quantities, etc.”

A few years later, his work Geometry of Position for the Use of Those Who Intend to Measure Land (1803) contributed to the renewal of projective geometry. Carnot considered that all quantities could be represented within the framework of calculation. He demonstrated that measurement operations could be based solely on relations of position between objects — alignments, intersections, and ratios — without systematically resorting to direct measurement.

Such an approach proved particularly valuable for engineer-geographers and naval officers responsible for surveying, especially in exploratory contexts where material conditions made measurements difficult or imprecise. It made it possible to reconstruct distances, forms, and configurations from partial observations, according to a logic underlying the practices of triangulation and cartography implemented during maritime expeditions, such as those conducted by Louis-Claude de Saulces de Freycinet.
