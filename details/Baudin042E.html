Portrait of Gaspard Monge sketched when teaching at the Polytechnic School by Atthalin, student from 1802 to 1804. ©Bibliothèque centrale de l’Ecole polytechnique (Fonds Gaspard Monge, III 3B (1802-1803) Above the portrait and in the second document, notes taken by this student during the lectures on partial differential equations and devil's curves (‘equations du Diable’, known also as Cramer curves)

Monge took part in 1798 in Bonaparte Egyptian expedition* to supervise with Berthollet* the scientific and archaeological undertakings. He also administered the Institute of Egypt (Institut d’Egypte), drew maps of the country, studied its monuments, and found the scientific explanation of mirages. He was Napoléon Bonaparte’s great friend but defended his students’ republicanism and hostility against the emperor after the establishment of the imperial regime as shown in this exchange between the two men: ‘Well Monge, your students are about all rebelling against me, they claim to be my enemies’-‘Sire, it was not easy for us to encourage them to become republicans; give them time to become imperialist. And let me say that you took a rather precipitous shortcut’.

Further information: Baudin named cape Monge Pointe du Papillon (butterfly). An island in Western Australia was also dedicated to Monge.
