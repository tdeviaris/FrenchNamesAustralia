As admiring and laudatory as Péron is towards Bass, he becomes noticeably more defensive when it comes to asserting the merits of the French contribution to discovery and cartography. Drawing on information gathered from the expedition’s journals, Péron explains that the British “Chart of Bass’s Strait between New South Wales and Van Diemen’s Land: surveyed by Lieut. Flinders of His Majesty’s ship Reliance, by order of His Excellency Governor Hunter, 1798–9” is “defective”, containing “errors” and omitting several geographical features or “particular names.” On his side, Baudin remarked that ‘…This bay seemed to us to form a much larger indentation than the English chart appears to indicate. Moreover, there is an infinite number of more or less sizeable coves inside it, formed by jutting points which, from their situation, could merit the term of cape…’. These critical comments may have served to justify the surveying work by the French on the coasts of a strait discovered by a British officer.
