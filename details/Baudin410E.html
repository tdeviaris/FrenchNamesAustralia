Riviere Vasse - Vasse River

In his historical narrative, Francois Peron explains:

“Swept away three times by the waves as he tried to reboard, he disappeared among them, without it being possible to give him any assistance, or even to confirm his death, so great was the violence of the sea and so deep the darkness of the night.

Although all the circumstances pointed to his inevitable death, no member of the expedition had the slightest doubt on the matter, when an article, reproduced in all the French newspapers, drew public attention to the unfortunate Vasse and rekindled hope in the hearts of his companions.

The article claimed that, having miraculously escaped the fury of the waves, Vasse, after the departure of the two ships, had joined the Indigenous peoples of that part of Leuwin Land, adopted their customs, learned their language, and spent two or three years among them. Then, without any explanation, he was said to have been found three or four hundred leagues south of the place of his shipwreck by an American vessel, which took him on board, before he was later arrested by an English cruiser. It was even added that he had recently arrived in England, where he was being detained in violation of international law.

However implausible such an adventure might be, Messrs Freycinet, Lesueur and I did not think that we should ignore this public rumour. We hastened to draw the Ministry's attention to an event which, in every respect, would have been of such great interest had it proved true.

Unfortunately, this comforting error was dispelled by the results of the investigations ordered in this regard by His Excellency the Minister of the Navy: all the details of the article concerning our unfortunate companion were entirely fabricated.

It was to commemorate his misfortune and our regret that we named this singular inlet, which I have described, Vasse River - a discovery that became for us a bitter source of loss and sorrow...”
