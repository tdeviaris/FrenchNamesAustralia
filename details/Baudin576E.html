Île Molière – Moliere Island

Cap Molière – Whyalla-Hummock Hill

During the Napoleonic period, Molière remained highly prominent in the repertory of the Comédie-Française. He was also strongly represented in the free performances organised in Paris during Napoleonic celebrations: fourteen of his plays were staged, amounting to approximately 18% of the Théâtre-Français programme. These official programmes included, among others, Tartuffe, L’Avare, L’École des femmes, Le Médecin malgré lui, Le Misanthrope, Sganarelle and Les Fourberies de Scapin.

Tartuffe, one of Molière’s most emblematic plays, denounces religious hypocrisy. The character of Tartuffe attempts to seduce Elmire — shown here as performed by Mlle Mars — the wife of Orgon, a credulous bourgeois who has welcomed him into his household. By the early nineteenth century, the very name “Tartuffe” had already entered everyday language to designate a false devotee or a hypocrite.

The members of the Baudin expedition did not hesitate, on occasion, to use this reference in relation to their commander. In his journal, the officer Breton described the episode of his transfer aboard the Naturaliste:

“On the 7th [Brumaire, 29 October 1801], the commander gave me the order to pass aboard the Naturaliste: as it was not an order, but rather a letter full of foolishness and absurdities, I brought it back to him. We had a very long explanation, during which the Tartuffe told me that it was for my own good that he was placing me aboard the Naturaliste, and that he had not given me advancement. He played his part so well that he made me shed tears — and shed some himself — and made me believe that he was attached to me, and that I had been at fault towards him. But scarcely had I left him when, reflecting on the way he had behaved towards me and many others since the beginning of the campaign, I saw his hypocrisy and his wickedness. He changed my order and gave me a very laconic one, dated 9 Brumaire [31 October 1801].”

This allusion shows how deeply Molière, and Tartuffe in particular, belonged to the literary and moral culture of French officers at the beginning of the nineteenth century.
