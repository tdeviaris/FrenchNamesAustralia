Cap Lafontaine – Point Lowly

Île Lafontaine – Lafontaine Island

The study of La Fontaine’s fables and tales had formed part of official education in France since the Revolution. La Fontaine reworked Aesop’s stories, classical narratives, and the Latin verse fables of Phaedrus, transforming them into French verse fables built around concise dialogue, capable at once of pleasing, moving, and instructing their readers. These texts were particularly well suited to the classroom, where they helped to link moral instruction, language, translation, and literary imitation. Despite Louis XIV’s occasional disapproval of some of his works and the censorship that affected certain of his publications, La Fontaine was elected to the Académie française in 1684.

In 1802, Pierre Didot published a new edition of La Fontaine’s Fables, illustrated with engravings designed under the direction of Charles Percier, one of the principal architects in Bonaparte’s service. This edition fully belongs to the visual and political culture of the Consulate. The artists gathered around Percier were among the most prominent designers and engravers of the new regime, and Bonaparte himself kept a copy of the Fables in his library. The 1802 illustrations, marked by the neoclassical taste that would flourish under the Empire, offered a visual interpretation of La Fontaine aligned with the values of authority, order, unity, and grandeur that Napoleonic power sought to promote.
