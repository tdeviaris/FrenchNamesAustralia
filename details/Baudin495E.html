Île Depuch - Depuch Island

Anse Depuch - Eagle Bay

Entrée Depuch - Depuch Entrance

Louis Depuch’s role should be understood in the context of the new importance given to geology in voyages of discovery. Together with Joseph Charles Bailly, he was among the first professionally trained geologists to observe the Australian coasts. Their task was not merely to collect stones, but to interpret the nature of the terrain, identify the main types of rocks, record their distribution along the coastline and the islands, and draw from these observations hypotheses about the structure of the continent. Their interpretation remained marked by Neptunist theories, which explained many formations through the ancient action of water and the retreat of the sea. Yet they also recognised basalt as a volcanic rock, even though some identifications, particularly between basalt and dolerite, remained uncertain. From this perspective, the Depuch toponyms, from Anse Depuch to Île Depuch, do not merely commemorate a scholar who died prematurely, but also reflect the central place given by the Baudin expedition to the mineralogical and geological study of the coasts of New Holland.

Depuch Island was in fact explored by Ronsard, whom Baudin had entrusted with the task of reconnoitring it in order to determine whether it was an island or part of the mainland. Depuch himself therefore did not take part in this reconnaissance. However, Ronsard brought back on board the Géographe samples of reddish-brown rocks coated with iron oxide, together with observations on the island’s prism-like basaltic formations. These rocks made a strong impression on Ronsard and, later, on Depuch himself.

In his journal of 27 July 1801, Ronsard described the soil of the northern part of the island where he had landed. He observed basaltic prisms, sometimes pentagonal, piled one upon another and often resting on their angles. These lava blocks, fairly regular in form, seemed in places to resemble large building stones. Elsewhere, he encountered large stones, often lying at an angle, which made walking difficult and even dangerous. Some stones appeared to have been recently broken, although there was no indication that their fractures had been caused by any external force. According to him, the breaks had exactly the appearance that would result from the extraction of a specimen. They retained the colour of fresh basalt, unlike the outer surfaces, which were coated with iron oxide and gave this part of the island its reddish-brown hue.

This connection with mineralogy probably explains why Depuch’s name was given to this island, even though the actual reconnaissance had been carried out by Ronsard. The island offered a remarkable example of volcanic terrain, or at least of terrain interpreted as such by the expedition’s scientists.

The vegetation of Depuch Island is sparse, but locally fragrant and varied in sheltered areas where soil has accumulated between the basalt blocks and in the upper valleys. Ronsard described these associations of rocks and vegetation as true little natural “gardens”. He explained the fertility of certain soils by the presence of basalt, iron oxide and sandy deposits. According to him, the iron oxide coating the rocks, washed down by water into the small valleys, nourished the vegetation and, when mixed with a small quantity of sand, formed fertile deposits. These areas thus appeared as genuine “little gardens”, offering “greenery very pleasing to the eye” and exhaling “a sweet fragrance”. He noted the presence of plants and shrubs growing among the rocks, including mimosas, several legumes, solanums and a species of everlasting remarkable for the brilliance of its colours. Modern regional descriptions suggest a flora typical of the Pilbara offshore islands, composed of plants resistant to arid, saline and rocky conditions, notably coastal grasses and acacias.

Ronsard also recorded several observations on the island’s fauna: large grasshoppers, an abundance of very troublesome small flies, several black butterflies speckled with white, and ants of different sizes. Finally, he reported sailors’ accounts of having seen a large snake, “as thick as an arm” and at least five feet long, as well as a kangaroo of considerable size. This last faunal observation is particularly important, since it probably refers to the Depuch Island rock-wallaby. Later research has shown that the island once supported a population of rock-wallabies, identified through ancient DNA as very probably belonging to Petrogale lateralis lateralis, the black-flanked rock-wallaby. This population is now considered extinct, probably because of predation by introduced foxes.
