Cap du Mont-Tabor - Pickering Point

The Battle of Mount Tabor holds an important place in Napoleonic history, as it is often regarded as the most remarkable military achievement of the Syrian campaign. The engagement effectively prevented a major Ottoman attempt to strike the rear of Napoleon’s forces. Despite the overall failure of the Syrian campaign, this victory, won under the command of General Kléber, is commemorated on the Arc de Triomphe.

Freycinet mentions, between Île Latreille and Cap du Mont-Tabor:

“an enormous detached rock, situated halfway between Île Latreille and Cap du Mont-Tabor, at longitude 140°30', has the most singular shape; it forms a natural bridge with two arches, having from 130 to 150 feet of opening and 60 to 80 feet of height. A projecting and smaller rock in front of the first forms a kind of spur, while the top of the main rock, slightly convex in its middle part and flat throughout its whole extent, bears a striking resemblance to a blacksmith’s anvil.

All the space between Île Latreille and Cap du Mont-Tabor, except perhaps for a few very small sandy coves, is inaccessible.”

It may refer to the Bay of Islands. However, the region is characterised by intense coastal erosion: marine processes actively wear down the limestone cliffs and islands, leading to the frequent collapse and re-formation of landforms. Freycinet also warns that the coasts are mostly “inaccessible”, with reefs. The Miocene limestone cliffs of this shoreline have been weathered and eroded into caves, arches and stacks, which in the past caused numerous shipwrecks.
