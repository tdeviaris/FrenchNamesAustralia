Île Leschenault - Salutation Island

At Shark Bay, although he was trained as a botanist, Leschenault collected a lizard, probably a blue-tongued skink, judging from its size, the robustness of its body, its thick tail, and the viscous secretion he describes. The episode is revealing of the field practices of natural history that characterised voyages of discovery in the early nineteenth century, in which those on board operated in the field as naturalists as much as through their particular specialisations: botany in Leschenault’s case, zoology in Péron and Levillain’s etc. The animal was handled cautiously, though without any particular gentleness, and the observer seems chiefly relieved that the substance discharged by the reptile did not damage his clothing. He thus reported:

“One day, while botanising, I came across a lizard on a plant, four to five inches long including the tail. Its colour was a dirty grey; its head was thick; its eye protruding, large, yellow, sinister, and without liveliness. Its tail, covered with rounded protuberances and only slightly tapered, was about an inch and a half long; its toes were similarly rounded at their tips. I approached; it looked at me without fleeing. Knowing that venomous lizards exist, I wrapped my hand in the skirt of my coat, which was made of woollen cloth, and seized the animal. It made no attempt to escape; but, raising its tail, it discharged from all the protuberances that entirely covered it an almost black and viscous liquid. I then congratulated myself on my prudence. In seizing it, I broke its tail, which, like that of all lizards, continued to move a great deal, although separated from the body. I preserved the animal in brandy; the colour of my coat, which was dark brown, was not altered.”

This apparently anecdotal episode illustrates both the descriptive precision of shipboard naturalists and the concrete—at times brutal—gestures through which the nature of the southern lands was observed, grasped, and incorporated into European collections.
