The toponyms associated with Leschenault refer to several distinct moments of the Baudin expedition and to several places in Western Australia. They recall the role of Théodore Leschenault de La Tour as a botanist and shipboard naturalist, as well as his field observations and the ambiguities of his outlook: attentive to landscapes, plants, and animals, he nevertheless remained shaped by the European categories of his time when describing Aboriginal peoples.

Port Leschenault - Leschenault Estuary

On 4 June 1801, Baudin ordered the officer Le Bas to take the launch and reconnoitre the river that officers from the Naturaliste said they had sighted the previous day. He also authorised Péron and Leschenault to join the reconnaissance.

Leschenault noted in his journal:

"We reached the head of a salt pond situated immediately behind the cliff which, as it were, encloses the whole country. Behind the hillock that holds back the waters of the pond, the river can be seen at a distance of about two hundred yards. In this part, its banks are marshy and covered with samphire. We walked for an hour parallel to its course; then we were stopped, because at this point the pond communicates with the river. While waiting for the boat, we ate our lunch with the cheerfulness inspired by this little expedition. The opposite bank offered us a pleasant country, and swans, together with many other aquatic birds, flew in abundance over the waters."

This first passage reveals an observer sensitive to the physical configuration of the place: the salt pond, the river, the marshy banks, the samphire, and the abundance of aquatic birds together form an estuarine landscape that Leschenault describes with precision. His account allows us to recognize a low, wet, brackish coastal environment where fresh and salt water meet.

Leschenault then refers to traces of Aboriginal occupation observed by other members of the expedition:

"Other people who explored the country found several huts, but all were abandoned. They left there glass beads, knives, and mirrors. These huts are as poorly made as possible; they are at most four feet high. I was later told that larger ones had been seen, but they were of scarcely more perfect construction, and two men could hardly shelter in them. They consist of pieces of wood stuck shallowly into the ground and covered with the bark of the Melaleuca of which I spoke above. The same bark is also spread over the ground inside the huts; no doubt it serves as a bed."

The tone here becomes more pejorative. Leschenault describes the dwellings according to European criteria of solidity, permanence, and comfort, without being able to fully grasp their functions within a way of life adapted to the territory, seasonal mobility, and local resources. What he perceived as rudimentary may also be understood as a light, functional architecture suited to the environment.

The judgement he then passed on the inhabitants of the region was even more severe:

"These peoples seemed to me to be as far removed as possible from all civilisation. They do not even have the curiosity that marks the desire to learn; their fear resembles that of the wildest beasts; and one may, without fear of error, pronounce on the industry and advancement of the ideas of a people who have neither clothing nor canoes, not even the bow, a weapon that has so far been encountered among almost all peoples."

These lines must be read with caution. They do not constitute a neutral observation, but rather the testimony of a dated European gaze, based on material criteria - clothing, weapons, canoes, forms of dwelling - that Leschenault too quickly interpreted as signs of "advancement" or "civilisation." The brevity of the encounter, the absence of direct exchange, and the evident fear of the inhabitants when faced with the arrival of strangers greatly limited his understanding of the societies he observed. What he interpreted as a lack of curiosity may also be understood as a strategy of distance, caution, or protection.

This judgement should therefore be presented for what it is: a valuable document on the encounter, but also a text revealing the limits of the European gaze at the beginning of the nineteenth century. In the intellectual context of the Baudin expedition, the societies encountered were often assessed according to a scale of "civilisation" inherited from European debates on progress, nature, and human history. As Jean-Luc Chappey has shown with regard to François Péron, this period saw the strengthening, in certain scholarly circles, of a tendency to classify human societies according to supposedly objective criteria, yet ones deeply shaped by European values. This development should not be too simply conflated with the more systematic racial doctrines of the second half of the nineteenth century; nevertheless, it helped reduce the complexity of Aboriginal societies by judging them according to norms that were not their own.

Leschenault's text therefore does not allow us to conclude that the Aboriginal peoples of the region lacked culture, social organization, or knowledge. Rather, it reveals the difficulty French voyagers had in fully recognizing forms of dwelling, mobility, relationship to land, and material culture different from those with which they were familiar in Europe.

Île Leschenault - Salutation Island, Shark Bay

Île Leschenault, now Salutation Island, located in Shark Bay, must be distinguished from Port Leschenault or the Leschenault Estuary, situated farther south in the Geographe Bay region. The same name thus refers to two different places, associated with distinct episodes of the Baudin expedition.

Here, Leschenault appears in another light. In contrast to the severity of some of his judgements on Aboriginal societies, his writings sometimes reveal a remarkable sensitivity towards Australian animals and their vulnerability in the face of human beings. This sensitivity formed part of a broader movement, visible in certain scholarly circles in eighteenth-century France, in which a new concern for animal suffering and a critique of unnecessary violence began to emerge. See also, in this respect, the entries Cap Deshoulière and Canal Péron.

The passage devoted to Dorre Island, or Barren Island, belongs directly to the Shark Bay context. It shows Leschenault attentive to the vulnerability of animals living in an island space that had, until then, been relatively protected from human presence. He observed:

"The name Barren Island suits the appearance of this island perfectly. An immense number of kangaroos live there. Far from men, they no doubt lived there in peace. We brought discord and terror into their refuge and killed a great many of them."

This remark finds an echo in a later episode, no longer in Shark Bay but in Tasmania, during the Naturaliste's unsuccessful search for the Géographe in March-April 1802. The geographical context is different, but the motif is comparable: Leschenault again emphasizes the trust of island animals which, having not learned to fear human beings, become particularly exposed to their violence. Concerning the penguins of Waterhouse and Preservation Islands, he wrote:

"Having no enemies, they know no fear and seemed perfectly at ease among us. On Preservation Island especially, where I stayed for three days, these birds pecked at us in an attempt to dislodge us from the place we occupied beside the fires we had lit to dry our clothes, continually soaked by heavy rains. The sailors, who often behaved cruelly towards these creatures, without purpose or reason, abused their trust and slaughtered a great many of them."

Placed side by side, these two episodes therefore describe neither the same place nor the same moment of the voyage, but they reveal the same inflection in Leschenault's gaze: the perception of a vulnerable island nature whose tranquillity was brutally disrupted by the arrival of Europeans. They also help nuance his figure: Leschenault took part in the collecting and predatory practices characteristic of natural-history voyages of his time, yet he was also capable, on several occasions, of explicitly criticizing the gratuitous violence exercised by humans against animals that did not distrust them.

This sensitivity nevertheless had its limits. It did not prevent the ordinary collecting practices of early nineteenth-century natural history, which often involved capturing, killing, preserving, and transporting specimens. Nor did it prevent personal tensions: in February 1803, Leschenault's relations with Baudin deteriorated when the commander requisitioned his cabin to house captured kangaroos.

At Shark Bay, although trained as a botanist, Leschenault thus collected a lizard, probably a blue-tongued skink, judging by its size, the robustness of its body, its thick tail, and the viscous secretion he described - see the drawing made of it by Lesueur. This episode is revealing of the field practices that characterized voyages of discovery in the early nineteenth century: the savants on board acted as general naturalists as much as through their own particular specializations - botany in Leschenault's case, zoology in the case of Péron or Levillain.

The animal was seized cautiously, but without any particular gentleness, and the observer seems above all relieved that the substance discharged by the reptile did not damage his clothing. He reported:

"One day, while botanising, I encountered on a plant a lizard four to five inches long, including the tail; its colour was a dirty grey, its head thick, its eye protruding, large, yellow, sinister, and without liveliness; its tail, covered in rounded protuberances and only slightly tapered, was about an inch and a half long; its toes were similarly rounded at their tips. I approached; it looked at me without fleeing. Knowing that venomous lizards exist, I wrapped my hand in the skirt of my coat, which was made of woollen cloth, and seized the animal. It made no movement to flee; but, raising its tail, it discharged, from all the protuberances that entirely covered it, an almost black and viscous liquid. I then congratulated myself on my prudence. In seizing it, I broke its tail, which, like that of all lizards, continued to move a great deal although separated from the body. I preserved the animal in brandy; the colour of my coat, which was dark brown, was not altered."

This apparently anecdotal episode illustrates both the descriptive precision of the shipboard naturalists and the concrete - sometimes brutal - gestures through which the nature of the southern lands was observed, seized, and incorporated into European collections. It also invites us to grasp Leschenault in all his ambivalence: an observer capable of emotion before animal vulnerability, but also an actor in the collecting practices of his time.

Leschenault did not return to France aboard the Géographe with the rest of the crew. Having fallen ill during the stopover at Timor, he remained there for some time and then in the Spice Islands during his convalescence. He returned to Paris only in 1807.
