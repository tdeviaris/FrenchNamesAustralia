Pointe Moreau - Eagle Bluff

Charles Moreau, like Charles-Pierre Boullanger, belonged to the first generation of Polytechniciens involved in the voyage of discovery to the Southern Lands. Both were members of the 1794 / Year III intake of the Ecole polytechnique, but Moreau belonged to the cohort received on 4 Frimaire Year III, that is, 24 November 1794, whereas Boullanger was received later, on 28 Ventose Year III, that is, 18 March 1795.

Originally from Saint-Domingue, Moreau embarked as a midshipman on the Naturaliste, commanded by Jacques-Felix-Emmanuel Hamelin. His mathematical and nautical abilities were quickly noticed. In his journal, Hamelin notes that, on 18 Messidor Year IX [7 July 1801], he assigned Moreau to stand watch with Ensign Saint-Cricq, in order to train this young officer, already full of “good will” and “promise”, in shipboard command. Moreau was entrusted with a hydrographic mission at Shark Bay in 1801 and was promoted to ensign at Timor later that same year. The first page of his report, dated 4 Fructidor Year IX, states that its purpose was “to make the chart of the eastern and northern part of the point we occupied in Shark Bay.” This report was inserted in Hamelin's journal.

When the Naturaliste and the Geographe, separated after a violent gale in Geographe Bay, were reunited on 21 September 1801 in Kupang Bay, Timor, Moreau came into contact with Charles Baudin, then a midshipman on board the Geographe. He should be distinguished from Nicolas Baudin, the commander of the expedition, to whom he was not related. Born in 1784, Charles Baudin had entered the Navy at a very young age and served on the Geographe as a second-class midshipman; he would later become a vice-admiral, and then an admiral of France at the end of his life.

In his Souvenirs de jeunesse, Charles Baudin located the beginnings of his friendship with Moreau and Etienne-Henri Mangin Duvaldailly, or Duval d'Ailly, both midshipmen on the Naturaliste, precisely at Timor. Until then, he wrote, they had “scarcely seen” one another, even during the calls at Tenerife and the Ile de France. This relationship grew stronger at Port Jackson, where the two ships remained for several months at anchor in a part of the harbour far from the town. Baudin recalled the daily intimacy that developed among the three young men and confessed that his admiration for Moreau's “great qualities” had become “a true cult.” This friendship was at once maritime, intellectual, and scientific: it belonged to a milieu of officers trained in mathematics, hydrography, and the observational practices characteristic of voyages of discovery.

After the voyage to the Southern Lands, Moreau was promoted to lieutenant in 1805. At the Ile de France, he encountered Charles Baudin again, and both men also associated with Matthew Flinders, who was then being held under house arrest by the French authorities. Flinders already knew Moreau from Port Jackson, where French and British officers had met in the context of the provisioning, repairs, and scientific exchanges that accompanied the stopovers of the Baudin and Flinders expeditions. At the Ile de France, despite the war, learned forms of exchange continued. On 2 April 1808, Flinders noted in his private journal that, through M. Cap-Martin, he had received “a new method of finding the latitude by two altitudes” sent to him by Lieutenant Moreau, then serving on the Piemontaise. This gesture testifies to the persistence of nautical and scientific sociability between officers of enemy nations.

Moreau and Charles Baudin subsequently both served on the frigate Piemontaise, active in the Indian Ocean. It was on board this ship that Moreau met his death, on 8 March 1808, during the action against the British frigate San Fiorenzo, in tragic circumstances. In his report, the Minister of the Navy, Decres, emphasised that Moreau's talents, courage, and noble ambition had inspired “the greatest hopes.”
