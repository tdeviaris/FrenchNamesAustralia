Dolomieu Bay - Fortescue Bay

In this letter of 24 June 1790, devoted to “the question of the origin of basalt”, Dolomieu addressed one of the principal geological controversies of the late eighteenth century. On the basis of his field observations, particularly in the volcanic regions of Italy, he attributed a volcanic origin to many basaltic formations and interpreted them as ancient solidified lavas. He thus opposed the Neptunists associated with Abraham Gottlob Werner, who generally explained basalt as a deposit or precipitate formed within a primordial ocean.

In this particular debate, Dolomieu may therefore be associated with the Vulcanists. His position brought him close to Plutonism insofar as it assigned an important role to the Earth's internal heat in the formation of rocks. He should not, however, be presented as a disciple of James Hutton's geological system. Dolomieu relied primarily on field observation, the comparison of rock formations and the study of volcanoes rather than on the construction of a comprehensive theory of the Earth's interior.

In particular, he rejected the view that all volcanic phenomena could be explained by the superficial combustion of layers of coal, sulphur or bituminous matter, an explanation that remained widespread during the eighteenth century. The extent of lava flows and the scale of the transformations visible in volcanic regions appeared to require deeper subterranean processes. Nevertheless, he remained cautious about the precise nature of this “subterranean fire” and the mechanisms that supplied volcanic activity.

In 1791, while the Société d'histoire naturelle was contributing to the preparation of instructions for the scientists accompanying the d'Entrecasteaux expedition, Dolomieu drafted recommendations for the mineralogical and geological study of the lands they would visit. He emphasised the importance of observing landforms, the arrangement of rock strata, the nature of soils and minerals, and any possible evidence of volcanic activity. The d'Entrecasteaux expedition was subsequently to explore Tasmania and several parts of the Australian coastline.

The mineralogists of the Baudin expedition, Louis Depuch and Joseph Charles Bailly, worked within this methodological tradition when examining the rocks, soils and coastal formations of New Holland. Their work was not restricted to collecting specimens: they also sought to record the geographical setting of the materials and their position within the visible succession of strata. The artist Charles-Alexandre Lesueur was accordingly asked to draw some of the coastal profiles observed from the ships, preserving a visual record of the structure and morphology of the shoreline.

Dolomieu had also been one of the scientists who accompanied Bonaparte's expedition to Egypt in 1798. Forced to leave Egypt because of ill health, he was arrested in 1799 after the vessel on which he was travelling entered territory controlled by the Kingdom of Naples. His imprisonment in Sicily, under particularly severe conditions, caused widespread concern among European scientific circles. His release was eventually included among the conditions imposed upon the Kingdom of Naples, and he was freed on 15 March 1801, several months after the departure of the Baudin expedition.

After his release, Dolomieu returned to Paris, where he was received with honours by the scientific community. He died in November 1801, approximately thirteen months after the Baudin expedition had departed and while it was already exploring the coasts of New Holland.

The naming of a cape and a bay after Dolomieu therefore commemorated not only one of the most distinguished geologists of his time, but also a tradition of field observation and geological interpretation directly inherited by the mineralogists of the Baudin expedition.
