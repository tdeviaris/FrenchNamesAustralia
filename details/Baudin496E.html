Forestier belonged to a generation of naval administrators shaped by the study of the great naval ordinances of the Ancien Régime. He possessed, in particular, a correspondence between Colbert and Seignelay, which strengthened his admiration for the maritime organisation of the French “Grand Siècle”. His contemporaries stressed the precision of his geographical and naval knowledge, acquired not through travel, but through an attentive reading of voyages, reports, and naval documents.

Before the Revolution, thanks to the confidence placed in him by Fleurieu, who had been appointed governor to the Dauphin, Forestier entered the young prince’s household as secrétaire des commandements. This was a trusted administrative position involving the preparation and transmission of the prince’s orders and correspondence. After 10 August 1792, he was forced to flee and go into hiding. Once the Terror had passed, he returned to public service in the administrations that emerged from the Revolution, notably in the liquidation service and then in the commission for supplies.

His role became particularly significant under the Consulate and the Empire. Recalled to the naval administration when the ministries were re-established, he helped rebuild an administrative apparatus whose archives, documents, and correspondence had largely disappeared. Under the Empire, he retained his position as head of the First Division of the Ministry of the Navy. He was said to have constantly defended the interests of the French navy, even in government councils, against a policy later described as dominated by despotism and imperial ambition.

After the fall of the Empire, Forestier was rewarded under the Restoration. He became a councillor of State, intendant of the naval armies, and later intendant of the expenses of the King’s household. He also presided for several years over the naval committee and over a sanitary commission responsible for establishing lazarettos in French ports. He was made a knight of Saint-Louis and, in 1821, received the cross of Commander of the Legion of Honour.

In the context of the place names bestowed by the Baudin expedition, the name Forestier therefore refers to a senior administrator of the French Navy, associated with institutional continuity from the Ancien Régime through the Revolution, the Consulate, the Empire, and the Restoration. Under Napoleon, he held a central position in the offices of the Ministry of the Navy, at the very moment when the hydrographic results of the voyage to the Southern Lands were being prepared, published, and incorporated into official cartography.
