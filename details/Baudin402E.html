About Cape Hamelin

Cape Hamelin’ s geographical coordinates were considered sufficiently reliable by Freycinet to serve as a reference point for checking and adjusting the positions of other places. In the nautical and geographical account of the voyage, he wrote that “very close to Cape Hamelin, a large patch of white sand can be seen on the coast [perhaps today’s Cosy Corner Beach]; it is a very good landmark”. He gave its position as 34° 11′40″south latitude and 112° 41′ east longitude from Paris.

As with several positions recorded during the voyage, this latitude does not correspond exactly to modern mapping and appears to require a slight north– south adjustment. On modern maps, the latitude appears to be offset by roughly ten minutes, a discrepancy that should be taken into account when comparing Freycinet’s coordinates with present-day locations.

About Hamelin

In his narrative, Louis de Freycinet gives careful attention to the nautical and meteorological observations of Jacques Félix Emmanuel Hamelin, under whom he served aboard the Naturaliste. He records, for example, Hamelin’ s remarks from June 1801 on the western coasts of New Holland: during stormy nights, the sky there remained surprisingly bright, even when the moon was not visible — a phenomenon Hamelin compared with what he knew from t he English Channel and the coasts of Brittany. Freycinet also reported several of Hamelin’s observations on tides, showing the attention Hamelin paid to natural phenomena essential to navigation.

This favourable image is also echoed in Hamelin’s journal during the Naturaliste’ s first stay at Port Jackson, from 26 April to 18 May 1802. Separated from the Géographe since 7 March, Hamelin justified his decision to sail to Sydney on very practical grounds: the shortage of water and provisions, the presumed weakness of Baudin’s crew, and the fact that he had on board the Géographe’ s longboat, several of its best sailors, the geographical engineer Boullanger, and the midshipman Maurouard. He presented Port Jackson as the most reasonable place to find Baudin, receive his orders, or obtain assistance before resuming the work of explo ration after the winter.

The journal also reveals an officer concerned with order, discipline, and practical management. In Sydney, Hamelin organised repairs, secured supplies of water, wheat, fresh vegetables, potatoes, and pumpkins, arranged care for the sick, set up tents for the observations needed to regulate the chronometers, and maintained regular relations with Governor Philip Gidley King. King, who had been alerted by Joseph Banks to the possible arrival of the French ships, gave the Naturaliste an excellent reception. Hamelin repeatedly noted the attentions shown by the governor and the colonial administration, while also recording the material limits of the colony, especially the lack of salted meat, rice, and dried vegetables.

This image should not, however, be idealised. Jangoux’s article shows that Hamelin’ s decision to leave Port Jackson on 18 May 1802 remains difficult to explain. Although he had just learned from Matthew Flinders that Baudin was alive and was expected to c all at Sydney soon, Hamelin nevertheless announced his intention to leave, officially in order to search for the commander on the south-west coast and then proceed to the Isle de France if he failed to find him. Neither Baudin nor several of the Naturaliste’ s officers seem to have fully understood this decision. Saint-Cricq and Heirisson even reported that Hamelin in fact intended to sail directly to the Isle de France. Jangoux does not necessarily interpret this as a desire to break away, but rather as the possible result of discouragement: the repeated separations of the two corvettes, Baudin’s absence from the agreed rendezvous points, and the reduction of the Naturaliste ’s scientific personnel made the mission increasingly difficult to pursue alone.

Two aspects of Hamelin’ s command therefore need to be held together. On the one hand, he appears as an experienced and cautious seaman, attentive to the condition of his ship, the health of his men, and the practical demands of navigation. On the other ha nd, his conduct at Port Jackson reveals hesitations, occasionally fragile justifications, and a certain weariness in the face of the expedition’s constraints. Heirisson’ s criticisms belong to this context and remind us that not all officers shared the same view of their commander; see also Cape Heirisson.

Nevertheless, by comparison with Nicolas Baudin, whose authority was more frequently challenged and whose relations with several officers deteriorated during the voyage, Hamelin appears to have exercised a more measured and more positive form of command. This difference is visible in the assessments that the commanders were required to write on each midshipman and submit to the Ministry of the Navy in the É tat particulier des aspirants. Whereas Baudin wrote of the officer Giraud that he “ would have requested for him, on his return, the rank of ensign, had esprit de corps not made him on board almost as useless as the others”, Hamelin wrote instead: “He has commanded his watch well; he has much zeal and love for the service. Fit to be an ensign.”

These contrasting assessments shed light on two different ways of commanding and evaluating men. In Baudin’s case, judgement of individual ability was often overshadowed by his denunciation of collective tensions and of the “esprit de corps” he attributed to his officers. Hamelin’s evaluations, by contrast, seem more closely focused on service, watchkeeping, zeal, and professional aptitude. This more encouraging attitude helped make the Naturaliste a relatively more favourable environment for the recognition of young officers, among them Louis de Freycinet.

Freycinet’ s expressions of loyalty and esteem towards Hamelin therefore combined personal gratitude, the memory of a generally appreciated commander and, perhaps, a degree of professional prudence. Remaining on good terms with an officer whose career was clearly advancing could benefit the prospects of a young naval officer. After his return to France, Hamelin rose rapidly through the naval hierarchy. In June 1803 he was promoted to capitaine de vaisseau — equivalent to captain in the Royal Navy — and entrusted with important missions against British forces. In 1811, Napoleon made him a baron of the Empire, appointed him Grand Officer of the Legion of Honour, and promoted him to the rank of rear-admiral.
