Cap Degérando - Cape Degerando

Canal Degérando – West Basin

Van Diemen’s Land, present-day Tasmania, was the region in which Péron was able to apply de Gérando’s method for observing Indigenous peoples with the greatest degree of continuity. In the D’Entrecasteaux Channel and on Bruny Island, members of the expedition encountered Palawa groups on several occasions, including Nuenonne people. Péron observed their shelters, burial place, weapons, means of subsistence and certain forms of social interaction. He also recorded elements of their vocabulary and sought to interpret gestures, exchanges and responses to European objects. The portraits produced by Nicolas-Martin Petit and the drawings of Charles-Alexandre Lesueur complemented these observations by recording the appearance and, in some cases, the names of several individuals. Although these encounters remained brief and were affected by serious difficulties of communication, they generated the most substantial body of documentation produced by the expedition on Indigenous communities still living largely beyond direct European colonial control.

Péron also attempted to collect comparable information along the coasts of New Holland. On the western coast, particularly in the regions of Geographe Bay and Shark Bay, the encounters were generally too brief, uncertain or tense to permit sustained investigation. The absence of a shared language, mutual distrust, the mobility of the groups encountered and the constraints of the voyage prevented the French from remaining with communities long enough to apply de Gérando’s programme fully. Objects, traces of occupation and certain forms of behaviour were observed, but the exchanges did not produce information as detailed as that gathered in Tasmania.

The situation was different at Port Jackson, where Péron encountered several Indigenous people within the setting of the British colony and conducted, among other activities, experiments using a dynamometer. The expedition’s artists also produced a number of individualised portraits. These observations were nevertheless less focused on the autonomous social life of Indigenous communities than those undertaken in Tasmania, since they took place in an environment already profoundly transformed by colonisation.

At the time when Péron and Freycinet were developing the voyage’s geographical nomenclature, de Gérando was serving as secretary to Jean-Baptiste de Nompère de Champagny. They therefore belonged to closely connected administrative and scholarly circles, although any direct meeting between them would need to be established through more specific evidence.
