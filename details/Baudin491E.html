Jean-Baptiste Delambre was one of the major figures of French astronomy and geodesy during the Revolution, the Consulate and the Empire. His name, given by the Baudin expedition to a bay, a cape and an island on the Australian coast, refers directly to a scientific culture founded on observation, exact measurement and calculation. These practices were essential to the hydrographic and cartographic reconnaissance carried out by the officers of the Géographe, the Naturaliste and the Casuarina.

Together with Pierre Méchain, Delambre took part in one of the great scientific enterprises of the Revolution: the measurement of the arc of the Paris meridian between Dunkirk and Barcelona. Begun in 1792, this operation was intended to provide a scientific basis for the definition of the metre, conceived as a fraction of the terrestrial meridian quadrant. Delambre was responsible for the northern section of the measurement, while Méchain worked on the southern section, as far as Barcelona. The enterprise brought together astronomy, geodesy, precision instruments and trigonometric calculation — the same categories of knowledge that enabled navigators to determine positions, verify longitudes and transform observations into maps.

After Méchain’s death, Delambre gathered together the results of this vast operation. Drawing on Méchain’s observations, his own measurements and the legacy of French astronomy represented in particular by Lalande, he wrote the three volumes of the Base du système métrique décimal, published between 1806 and 1810. This work set out the measurement of the arc of the meridian between the parallels of Dunkirk and Barcelona, and thus gave the metric reform its geodetic foundation.

Delambre was also a member of the Bureau des longitudes and of the Institut, two central institutions in the organisation of science under the Directory, the Consulate and the Empire. Founded in 1795, the Bureau des longitudes was charged in particular with improving the determination of longitude at sea, publishing the Connaissance des temps, preparing an annual yearbook and perfecting astronomical tables. Delambre was among its first members, alongside savants and navigators such as Lagrange, Laplace, Lalande, Méchain, Cassini, Bougainville, Borda and Buache.

This connection with navigation also appears in the publications used by hydrographers. Freycinet cites tables published under the authority of the Bureau des longitudes, notably the Tables astronomiques publiées par le Bureau des longitudes de France, whose first part included the Tables du Soleil, par M. Delambre, together with the Tables de la Lune, par M. Bürg, published in 1806. These tables made it possible to calculate the positions of celestial bodies and were therefore directly useful for the astronomical observations required to determine longitudes.

The connection with Baudin’s voyage is especially significant. In January 1803, the Géographe’s observatory was set up near Cape Delambre, which, according to Péron, “forms the eastern end of Baie Bougainville”. There, numerous lunar distances from the Sun were observed in order to verify the longitudes and correct errors in positioning. The presence of Delambre’s name in this area was therefore not merely honorific: it echoed the practical work of the expedition’s astronomers, hydrographers and officers, who had to determine with precision the positions of the capes, bays and islands they surveyed.

In the nomenclature of the Baudin expedition, Delambre’s name thus recalls that geographical discovery rested on a science of measurement. Reconnoitring a coast, determining the position of a cape, drawing a map or correcting a longitude all belonged to the same ideal of precision as the measurement of the terrestrial meridian and the definition of the metric system. By giving Delambre’s name to places observed, measured and mapped in the southern seas, the expedition’s hydrographers placed their own work within the continuity of the great geodetic and astronomical enterprises of learned France.
