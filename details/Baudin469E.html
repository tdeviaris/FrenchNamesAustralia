At the time when preparations were being made for Baudin’s voyage to the Southern Lands, Jean-Léopold-Nicolas-Frédéric Cuvier, known as Georges Cuvier (1769-1832), was a rising naturalist. Assistant to the professor of comparative anatomy at the Muséum national d’histoire naturelle in Paris, he held, from 1799, the chair of natural history at the Collège de France. An influential member of the Institut, he was also associated with the commission responsible for planning the scientific programme of the expedition.

Cuvier played a decisive role in the recruitment of François Péron. Tasked with finding a candidate capable of working in the field of comparative anatomy, he recommended the young medical student after Péron’s professors had praised his “ardent zeal”. In order to advance knowledge in human comparative anatomy, Cuvier instructed Péron to collect specimens of Indigenous human remains. He also encouraged him to gather invertebrates, especially molluscs, jellyfish and pelagic zoophytes, which were then regarded as lower animals but were thought to offer valuable insights into the functioning of more complex organisms.

This orientation did not make Cuvier an evolutionist. He understood organisms as integrated functional wholes, whose parts were closely interdependent: any major anatomical change, in his view, risked disrupting the equilibrium of the living body. It is within this intellectual framework that his interest in the marine animals collected by Péron should be understood.

Cuvier described, classified and named several new species of medusae and molluscs discovered by Péron. He honoured him by attaching his name to several species, including the naked pelagic gastropod Pneumoderma peronii (now commonly known as the “sea angel”), as well as Pleurobranchus peronii and Onchidium peronii - now associated with the genus Peronia. In 1804, he published a memoir on Onchidium, a genus of naked molluscs related to slugs, and on a new species that he named Onchidium peronii.

Péron, however, did not always follow Cuvier in all his theoretical views. In his narrative, he distanced himself from certain Cuvierian ideas, particularly catastrophism and the extinction of species. In observing the zoophytes of the coasts of New Holland and Timor, as well as the giant clams (tridacnas) that he found both living and fossilised, he placed their development within “the long succession of time”.

The proximity, in the expedition’s nomenclature, of the names Cuvier and Lavoisier was probably not accidental. It may be related to the way Cuvier conceived the organisation of the natural sciences, incorporating chemistry into them as a field situated between the mathematical and the moral sciences. In his report to the emperor on the progress of the natural sciences since 1789, Cuvier described this field as a space “as vast as it is fertile”.

In the case of Cuvier Island, now Olive Island, the name published by Péron and Freycinet replaced the one used by Baudin, who had called it Le Postillon, probably in reference to its position off the coast, like an advanced post or relay station set before the shoreline.
