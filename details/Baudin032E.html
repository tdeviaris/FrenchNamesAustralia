Cap Lannes - Cape Buffon

Jean Lannes (1769-1809), 1st Duke of Montebello, was one of the most celebrated Marshals of the Empire. Born into modest circumstances, he distinguished himself during the Revolutionary Wars and later in the Napoleonic campaigns, where his bravery and military skill earned him Napoleon’s particular esteem.

Lannes appears to have had a reputation as a more humane commander than several other marshals. Although he faithfully carried out the Emperor’s orders, he did not hesitate to voice his aversion to unnecessary cruelty and to the violence that accompanied the Napoleonic wars. During the first Italian campaign, he obeyed orders to burn the village of Binasco and to take part in the repression of the revolt at Arquata, but he expressed disgust at such actions. In the aftermath of Austerlitz, he wrote to his wife: “We swept everything away — that is to say, killed or captured everything: never has such carnage been seen.” The Fondation Napoléon likewise stresses this tension between Lannes’s military obedience and his personal revulsion at certain forms of wartime violence.

Another often-cited example is that of Weimar, after the battle of Jena in 1806. Goethe sought protection from French soldiers; Lannes is said to have been the only French commander to respond favourably, ordering guards to be placed around the writer’s house and library.

Napoleon, for his part, held Lannes in deep affection, regarding him as one of his finest generals and as a close personal friend. This proximity probably gave Lannes an exceptional freedom of speech with the Emperor. Yet this does not mean that Napoleon turned Lannes’s moral sensitivity into a general principle of military government. He could condemn looting when it harmed discipline, supply, or the army’s reputation, but he also broadly accepted the system of requisitions, war contributions and seizures imposed on occupied territories. The Revolutionary and Napoleonic wars were therefore accompanied by many acts of violence, destruction and plunder, including in the artistic sphere.

Today, Cape Lannes refers to a promontory located in Little Dip Conservation Park, south of the township of Robe.

Further information

Baudin called the cape “Pointe Marbrée” — “Marbled Point” — probably because of the alternation of lighter and darker sedimentary sequences that form the promontory, visible in the coastal profile above.
