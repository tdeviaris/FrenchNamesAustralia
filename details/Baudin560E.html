Îles Maret – Maret Islands

Baie Maret – Louth Bay

Hugues-Bernard Maret (1763–1839) was one of the loyal servants of the Napoleonic state. A supporter of the Revolution, he was appointed ambassador to Naples in 1793. While crossing Piedmont on his way to take up this post, he was captured by Austrian forces and remained in captivity for thirty months. He was eventually released, together with other French deputies, in exchange for Marie-Thérèse, daughter of Louis XVI.

After Bonaparte’s coup d’état of 18 Brumaire, Maret was appointed Secretary of State in December 1799. In this capacity, he occupied a central position within the new regime: he oversaw the promulgation of laws, the registration and countersigning of decrees, and the circulation of official acts, while also organising Napoleon’s cabinet and reporting to him on the weekly business of the ministers. The decree reproduced here, concerning the feast of Saint Napoleon and the commemoration of the re-establishment of Catholic worship in France, illustrates the administrative and symbolic functions attached to his office. In 1809, he was created Duke of Bassano.

Maret’s political influence, however, did not earn him universal esteem among his contemporaries. Talleyrand, who detested him, reportedly remarked: “There is only one man more stupid than Monsieur Maret, and that is the Duke of Bassano.” Yet Maret played a significant part in the construction of Napoleonic government and public opinion. Associated with Charles-Joseph Panckoucke, he helped found La Gazette nationale ou le Moniteur universel, the official journal that became one of the Empire’s principal instruments of political communication.

The naming of the Maret Islands and Maret Bay in Freycinet’s atlas reflects the broader imperial geography of the voyage: a geography in which the coastal spaces of New Holland were inscribed within the political and administrative world of Napoleonic France. Through such toponyms, the map extended into the southern seas the memory of those who served the regime not only on the battlefield, but also in the machinery of state.
