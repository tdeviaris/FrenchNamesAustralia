Statue of the Joan of Arc by the sculptor Edme-Etienne Francois Gois, at Orleans, 1804. The statue was first approved but soon criticized for its poor dimensions and clothing, and a new statue from Foyatier was inaugurated in 1855. In the margins of the newspaper Le Moniteur of the 30th January 1803, next to the minutes of the council of the city of Orléans, regarding the project to build a monument dedicated to Joan of Arc, Bonaparte wrote words that illustrates this purposeful return of Joan of Arc into the French collective memory: ‘The famous Joan of Arc proved that there is no miracle that French spirit cannot achieve in circumstances when national independence is at risk…’ The commission for the statue was awarded to the sculptor Edme-Etienne Francois Gois. It was unveiled on 8 May 1804 as part of the restoration of the Joan of Arc celebrations, which had been suppressed during the French Revolution, because of the religious nature of Joan of Arc's mission. Additionally, several theater plays featured Joan of Arc and the engraver Cathelin, in 1804, exhibited a medal which showed Napoléon’s portrait on one side and that of Joan of Arc on the other. Also, painters like Henri Buguet, who gave drawing lessons at Mme Campan*’s boarding school, started to exhibit paintings of episodes in Joan’s life, which Joséphine valued.

Further information: According to Alphonse de Fleurieu*, the name Jeanne d’Arc was ‘highly valued’ by some Australian authorities, at least at the turn of the 20th century, when he visited the Geographical Societies of Australia to restore the Baudin expedition’s names. Hence, the National Gallery of Victoria commissioned the French sculptor Emmanuel Fremiet who had created the statue of Jeanne d’Arc in Paris, place des Pyramides, to make another version of it for the gallery, which he completed in 1906.
