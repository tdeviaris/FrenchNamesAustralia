Île Bernier - Bernier Island

For further information on Pierre-François Bernier, the expedition’s astronomer, after whom Bernier Island was named, and who had the observatory of the Géographe set up there, see the entries devoted to Cap Bernier / Cape Bernier.

Baudin refers to the island and the observatory in his ship’s journal, under the date 8 July 1801:

“Citizens Bernier, astronomer, Depuch, mineralogist, and I also set out to go [to the observatory on Bernier Island] to take hour angles and solar altitudes on the northern island, in order to fix its position in a certain and precise manner, for the position given to it by the Dutch chart I am using is very defective and differs, according to our previous observations, by nearly 20’ in latitude. We had very fine weather during this excursion, which took only two hours to reach the northernmost point of this island. After resting a little, we took, at half past ten, an hour angle, and several angles from the point where we were to the ship. Before and after the sun’s passage across the meridian, we obtained good altitudes, so that our journey was successful and we shall be able to determine, with exactness and precision, the entrance to the bay [Shark Bay]…”

While these data were of crucial importance for the construction of the chart of Shark Bay, Péron, for his part, reported another fact that still contributes to the island’s reputation today: the presence of the “banded kangaroo” (Kangurus fasciatus N.), “the smallest and most elegant species of this extraordinary genus of animals of New Holland, which is characterised in particular by the conical shape of its body, by the disproportion of its feet, and by the pouch in which the young are carried and nourished…” Péron gives a detailed description of it, in which natural-history observation is combined with a reflection on the insular distribution of species and, more broadly, with a utilitarian perspective characteristic of the period.

In this passage, Péron indeed places the banded kangaroo within a reflection on insularity. The animal, he writes, inhabits the islands of Bernier, Dorre and Dirk Hartog, but was observed neither on the neighbouring mainland nor on the other islands successively surveyed by the expedition. This limited distribution is not presented as mere chance: it becomes a principle of natural-historical explanation. Islands appear as spaces of separation, where certain species are fixed within precise geographical limits.

However, this scientific observation is immediately accompanied by a utilitarian perspective. The explorers also viewed these animals as a food resource. The description of the hunt is revealing: at first difficult because of the impenetrable thickets in which the kangaroos took refuge, it became systematic once the hunters discovered their hidden tracks. The text leaves little room for concern for the animal: it is above all pursued, captured, consumed, and then judged according to the quality of its flesh.

This logic of exploitation is all the more striking because Péron nevertheless gives the kangaroo a genuine emotional and moral depth. In a style reminiscent of Buffon, he humanises the animal, especially when he describes wounded females trying to save their young. The scene gives the narrative a strongly pathetic dimension: the mothers appear courageous, devoted, almost heroic.

Yet this sensitivity never calls into question the utilitarian framework of the passage. Emotion in the face of the suffering animal coexists with an interest in its flesh, which Péron judged excellent. His conclusion is explicit: the species could become a valuable acquisition for Europe and for farmyards. The banded kangaroo thus appears at once as an object of science, a pathetic figure, and a potential resource to be acclimatised in France.

Banded Kangaroo described by Péron in Voyage de découvertes aux Terres australes, 1807, vol. I, pp. 114-118

“Only one species of mammal is found there: the banded kangaroo (Kangurus fasciatus N.), the smallest and most elegant species of this extraordinary genus of animals of New Holland, characterised in particular by the conical shape of its body, by the disproportion of its feet, by the pouch in which the young are carried and nourished, etc.”

“The species in question is distinguished at first sight from all those known to date by twelve or fifteen transverse bands arranged across the back. These are narrow, of a slightly brownish russet colour, less regular and less clearly defined at shoulder height, where they begin to appear, but soon becoming more distinct and darker as they descend towards the tail, at the base of which they end. These stripes fade away on the sides, with no trace of them visible on the belly; the face and feet have a slightly yellow colour, while the abdomen is light grey and somewhat whitish; the rest of the coat is a hare-grey, darker or lighter according to the individual. In this species, the ears are proportionally shorter than in any other of the genus; the same is true of the tail, which is also weaker and, being hairless, bears a strong resemblance to that of a very large rat. In other respects, it has the same conical body shape, the same disproportion between the forefeet and hindfeet, the same arrangement of toes, claws, etc., as all other kangaroos. But all these details, which will be presented in the zoological part of our work, cease to belong to this narrative. It is enough for me to have indicated the principal characteristics of the pretty little animal with which we are concerned, and M. Lesueur’s drawing will sufficiently make up for the details that I must pass over here in silence.”

“The banded kangaroo inhabits in swarms the three islands of Bernier, Dorre and Dirk Hartog, without our ever having been able to find it on any part of the mainland or on the other islands we successively surveyed. Later on, the same phenomenon will be seen to recur for all species of kangaroos: that is to say, each of them will be seen to have been fixed by nature on particular islands or particular lands, with no individual appearing beyond the limits specific to its species.”

“Deprived of any means of attack or defence, the kangaroos in question display, like all weak creatures, and particularly like the hares of our climates, an extremely gentle and timid character. The slightest noise alarms them; the breath of the wind is sometimes enough to put them to flight. Thus, despite their great number on Bernier Island, hunting them was at first very difficult and uncertain. In the impenetrable bushes of which I have spoken, these animals could safely defy the skill and activity of our hunters. When forced to leave one of these refuges, they emerged by unknown routes and darted quickly under some neighbouring bush, without it being possible to understand how they could so easily plunge into and disappear among these inextricable thickets. But it was soon noticed that, for each bush, they had several small covered paths which, from various points around the circumference, led to the centre, and which could, when necessary, provide them with different exits according to the point from which they felt most threatened. From that moment, their ruin was assured. Our hunters gathered together, and while some of them beat the undergrowth with long sticks, others lay in wait at the exits of the little paths; and the animal, deceived by its own experience, inevitably came to offer itself to almost unavoidable shots. The flesh of this animal seemed to us, as it had to Dampier, fairly similar to that of the wild rabbit, but more aromatic, perhaps owing to the particular nature of the plants on which it feeds, almost all of which are fragrant. It is, moreover, the best kangaroo flesh we have since found; and, in this respect, the acquisition of this species would be a benefit to Europe.”

“At the time when we were on these shores, all the adult females carried in their pouch a fairly large young one, which they tried to save with truly admirable courage. Wounded, they fled, carrying their young in their pouch, and never abandoned them except when, too exhausted by fatigue and too weakened by loss of blood, they could no longer carry them. Then they stopped, crouching on their hind legs, helped the young with their forefeet to leave the maternal pouch, and sought, as it were, to show it the places of refuge where it might more easily hope to save itself. They themselves then continued their flight as fast as their strength allowed. But if the hunter’s pursuit ceased, or merely slackened, they could be seen returning to the protective bush of their nursling; they called it with a kind of grunt peculiar to them; they caressed it affectionately, as if to calm its fears, made it enter their pouch once more, and sought, with this gentle burden, some new thicket where the hunter could neither discover nor force them out. The same proofs of intelligence and affection appeared still more touchingly in these poor mothers when they felt themselves mortally wounded: all their care was directed towards the safety of their nursling. Far from trying to save themselves, they stopped beneath the hunter’s blows, and their final efforts were devoted to the preservation of their young… A generous devotion of which the history of animals offers so many examples, and which we are often reduced to envying them!”

“During our stay on Bernier Island, we captured several of these young kangaroos; but most of them, no doubt too weak, did not survive captivity for long. Only one resisted and became tame. This animal ate bread with pleasure and, above all, savoured sugared water with delight when it was offered. This last taste must seem all the more extraordinary because, on the wild islands where these animals live, all kinds of fresh water are usually absent. This young kangaroo died by accident at Timor. Its loss was less painful to us because, having only a single individual, we could not hope to naturalise it in Europe. But this first attempt was enough to prove, in a certain manner, that this species would adapt very well to human care; and, I repeat, it would be a precious acquisition for our farmyards.”
