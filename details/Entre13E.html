Partridge Island lies off the tip of the Labillardiere Peninsula on Bruny Island, separated from it by a narrow straight. The name Perdrix, meaning Partridge, was given to the island after a party led by officer Cretin recorded the sighting of « two flocks of partridges that appeared to be from the same species as in Europe’, in early May 1792. When, some two weeks after its discovery (20 May), Labillardière* and his colleague Riche* visited the island, they did not find any partridges. Labillardière recorded in his journal: ‘A small island … had been denominated Partridge Island by some of our crew who discovered it. Citizen Riche and I spent the following day upon the island; but instead of partridges we found a great number of quails there. Whether those who had first visited it had taken the one fowl for the other, or whether the partridges had since left the island, I must leave undecided’. There is some irony and bitterness in Labillardière’s sentence, that probably reflected his underlying thinking that d’Entrecasteaux accepted the name perdrix given by his officer without consulting his naturalists who had greater expertise than the officers in assessing the natural history of places.
