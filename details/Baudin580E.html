Monge formalised descriptive geometry, a method for representing three-dimensional objects on a two-dimensional plane, and is widely regarded as its founder. In his time, this discipline had very practical applications in the arts, architecture, construction, manufacturing and engineering. Monge also contributed to the further development of the analytic geometry inherited from Descartes and Fermat.

During the French Revolution, he was appointed Minister of the Navy, serving from April 1792 to April 1793, shortly before Freycinet received his first naval training. His time in office, however, was marked by difficulties: the dockyards and naval ports were affected by disorder, lack of discipline and the weakening of established authority.

Monge was more successful in the field of scientific education. He helped create the École polytechnique, whose aim was to strengthen scientific training and broaden access to the engineering profession. Deeply attached to his students, whom he called his “adoptive sons”, he was a much-admired and popular teacher. Several members of the Baudin expedition had been trained at the École polytechnique before they embarked, including the engineer-geographers Boullanger* and Faure*, the mineralogist Bailly*, and the officers Maurouard* and Hyacinthe de Bougainville*.

In 1798, Monge joined Bonaparte's expedition to Egypt. Together with Berthollet*, he supervised the scientific and archaeological work of the expedition. He also helped administer the Institute of Egypt, drew maps of the country, studied its monuments and proposed a scientific explanation for the phenomenon of mirages.

Monge was a close friend of Napoleon Bonaparte, but he also remained attached to the republican spirit that had shaped the early years of the École polytechnique. He defended his students when some of them expressed hostility towards the imperial regime. A famous exchange between the two men illustrates this tension. Napoleon is said to have told him: “Well, Monge, your students are almost all rebelling against me; they claim to be my enemies.” Monge reportedly replied: “Sire, it was not easy for us to make them republicans; give them time to become imperialists. And allow me to say that you have taken a rather abrupt shortcut.”
