In nautical terms, the vessel was adapted to the conditions of oceanic navigation. Its wooden sheathing, vulnerable to the common shipworm, a small bivalve mollusc capable of attacking hulls, was removed and replaced with copper sheathing, intended to better protect the hull during long passages. Its size, however, limited its ability to approach low, poorly known, or hazardous coasts. Detailed coastal reconnaissance was therefore often carried out by the ship’s boats, launches, longboats, or other small craft sent out from the vessel, and, after the stop at Port Jackson, by Le Casuarina, a smaller vessel specifically acquired to facilitate the exploration of inshore waters.

The vessel had also been fitted out with the sanitary concerns of long-distance navigation in mind. A new type of galley, inspired by the so-called “Kersaint” model, was equipped with a ventilation system intended to “remedy the perpetual corruption of the air inside ships.” Special filters were also meant to purify water altered by prolonged storage at sea. These devices, however, did not fully meet the expectations placed in them. As early as the first stop of the voyage, at Tenerife, the officer Ronsard observed that “in the roadstead of Tenerife, it had been necessary to make some changes to the galley. Its use at sea during the crossing had not met the expectations one might have had of it.”

Other arrangements on board also drew reservations from the very preparation of the voyage. After describing Le Géographe as “a very fine corvette,” Lieutenant de vaisseau Gicquel nevertheless pointed out several practical limitations. His reservations concerned, in particular, the insufficiency of the ballast, the absence of certain partitions in the hold and between-decks, the lack of air and light in several spaces, and the small size of the raised forecastle accommodation intended for Baudin. He also considered the masting too high and insufficiently supported; he recommended strengthening the shrouds and backstays, while noting that, although his observations were acknowledged as well founded, no immediate modifications seemed likely to follow. These reservations probably contributed to his decision to leave the expedition during the stop at Île de France in March–April 1801.

On board Le Géographe, daily life combined blue-water navigation, coastal reconnaissance, scientific work, and collective life. For the officers and savants, the ship constituted a space where the results of the expedition were processed, ordered, and preserved. Surveys were revised, compiled, and copied into fair manuscript charts. Mission reports were written there; scientific drawings were made from the collected specimens; and the specimens themselves were prepared, classified, and preserved.

Le Géographe thus functioned as a true floating laboratory: a material space in which the scientific survival of the collections was at stake. The naturalists had to transform specimens brought back from reconnaissance missions into objects that could be preserved, transported, and studied upon their return to Europe. The hold, crates, parchment, alcoholic liquors, and atmospheric conditions on board all formed part of this chain of preservation. Péron emphasized in particular the combined effects of heat and humidity, which caused containers to become mouldy and directly threatened the integrity of the zoological collections. The preservation of animals therefore required constant technical choices, especially concerning the use of alcoholic liquors intended to prevent the decay of specimens.

Life on board also depended on a strict material organisation, made more difficult by problems of provisioning. At Île de France, Baudin obtained supplies only with difficulty; they were limited in quantity and often of poor quality. He therefore arranged with the ship’s purser a daily distribution system designed to make the scarcest items last longer: two rations of arrack per day and one ration of beer at noon; on meat days, one meal of rice; on vegetable days, peas at noon and rice in the evening; coffee twice per ten-day period, on the fifth and tenth days; fresh bread once per ten-day period, for both officers and crew; and a limited number of bottles of case wine reserved for the officers on the fifth and tenth days of each decade.

Thus, Le Géographe should be understood not only as the principal vessel of the Baudin expedition, but also as a complex maritime workplace: a command vessel, a mobile base for coastal reconnaissance, a cartographic workshop, a space for scientific preservation, and a shipboard microcosm organised around the sanitary, dietary, and material constraints of long-distance navigation.

When Le Géographe sailed from Le Havre, it had 118 men on board. Most were members of the crew, but there were also a few passengers and individuals who had embarked without authorisation. The ship’s complement then changed over the course of the voyage, as a result of illness, deaths, desertions, disembarkations, and transfers between the expedition’s vessels.
