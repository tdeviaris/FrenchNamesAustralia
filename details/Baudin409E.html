In nautical terms, the vessel was adapted to the conditions of oceanic navigation. Its wooden sheathing, vulnerable to the common shipworm, a small bivalve mollusc capable of attacking hulls, was removed and replaced with copper sheathing, intended to protect the vessel's bottom during prolonged crossings. Yet the very dimensions that made her suitable for oceanic navigation also limited her ability to approach low-lying, poorly charted, or hazardous shores. Detailed coastal reconnaissance therefore had to be carried out from the ship's boats — canoes, longboats, and other small craft sent out from the vessel — and, after the expedition's stopover at Port Jackson, by Le Casuarina, a smaller vessel specially acquired to facilitate the exploration of shallow coastal waters.

The ship had also been fitted out with the sanitary challenges of long voyages in mind. A new type of galley, inspired by the so-called “Kersaint” model, was equipped with a ventilation system intended to “remedy the perpetual corruption of the air inside ships”. Special filters were also installed to purify water that had deteriorated through prolonged storage at sea. These innovations, however, did not fully live up to expectations. As early as the first stopover of the voyage, at Tenerife, the officer Ronsard observed that “in the roadstead of Tenerife, it had been necessary to make some changes to the galley. Its use at sea during the crossing had not answered what had been expected of it.”

Other arrangements on board had already attracted criticism during the fitting-out of the expedition. After describing Le Géographe as “a very fine corvette”, Lieutenant Gicquel nevertheless pointed out a number of practical shortcomings. He noted in particular the insufficiency of ballast, the absence of certain partitions in the hold and between-decks, the lack of ventilation and light in several parts of the ship, and the cramped size of the quarter-gallery allocated to Baudin. The masting, in his view, was too high and insufficiently supported; he recommended strengthening the shrouds and backstays, while also observing that, although his remarks were acknowledged as well founded, they did not appear likely to produce any immediate changes. These reservations probably contributed to his decision to leave the expedition during the stopover at Île de France in March-April 1801.

Life on board the Géographe brought together oceanic navigation, coastal reconnaissance, scientific labour, and the routines of a tightly ordered collective existence. For the officers and scientists, the ship was not merely a means of transport, but a working space in which the results of the expedition were processed, organized, and preserved. Surveys were checked, compiled, and copied out as finished manuscript charts. Mission reports were drafted; scientific drawings were made from the collections brought back on board; and specimens were prepared, classified, and conserved.

In this sense, the Géographe functioned as a floating laboratory whose role was central to the scientific fate of the voyage. The survival of the expedition's collections was constantly at stake. The naturalists had to transform the specimens gathered during coastal excursions into objects capable of surviving transport, storage, and later study in Europe. The hold, the crates, the parchment, the alcoholic preservatives, and the atmospheric conditions on board all formed part of this fragile chain of conservation. Péron repeatedly emphasized the combined effects of heat and humidity, which caused containers to mould and directly threatened the integrity of the zoological collections. Preserving animals therefore required continual technical decisions, especially concerning the use of alcoholic liquids intended to prevent the corruption and decay of specimens.

Life on board also depended on a strict material organization, made more difficult by recurrent supply problems. At Île de France, Baudin obtained provisions only with difficulty, and in quantities both limited and often of poor quality. He therefore arranged with the ship's purser a daily system of distribution intended to husband the scarcest resources: two rations of arrack per day and one ration of beer at noon; on meat days, one meal of rice; on vegetable days, peas at noon and rice in the evening; coffee twice every ten-day period, on the fifth and tenth day; fresh bread once each decade for officers and crew alike; and a few bottles of case wine reserved for the officers on the fifth and tenth days of each decade.

Le Géographe should therefore be understood not simply as the principal vessel of the Baudin expedition, but as one of its central sites of work and knowledge. She was at once a command ship, a mobile base for coastal reconnaissance, a cartographic workshop, a space of scientific preservation, and a social microcosm shaped by the sanitary, dietary, spatial, and material constraints of long-distance navigation.

When she left Le Havre, Le Géographe carried 118 men on board. Most were members of the crew, joined by a small number of passengers and persons embarked without authorization. The ship's complement changed throughout the voyage, affected by illness, death, desertion, disembarkation, and transfers between the vessels of the expedition.
