Île Fontanes - Fontanes Island

Baie Fontanes - Dutton Bay

Louis de Fontanes was one of the major literary and institutional figures of France under the Consulate and the Empire. A poet shaped by the intellectual climate of the Enlightenment, initially receptive to the ideals of 1789 and later marked by the violence of the Revolution, he gradually drew closer to Bonaparte, who made him one of the most prominent orators of the regime. Deputy, President of the Corps législatif, and later Grand Master of the Imperial University, Fontanes came to embody a synthesis of classical culture, political moderation, tempered Catholicism, and service to the state.

Born into a noble family, he had made a name for himself before the Revolution through several poetic and literary works. In his Poem on Astronomy (1789), he already combined scientific inspiration with an exaltation of divine infinity. He met Chateaubriand (see the introduction) in 1797, when both had taken refuge in London because of their royalist sympathies. Returning to France in 1798, Fontanes entered Bonaparte’s good graces when the latter entrusted him with delivering the eulogy for George Washington. The First Consul regarded the American president as “the only man who is on my line.” Fontanes then helped revive the literary journal Mercure de France, accepted a position in the Ministry of the Interior, and became President of the legislative assembly in 1804. He also worked to promote a religious revival. Napoléon is said to have told Fontanes: “There are only two powers in the world, the sword and the spirit. By spirit, I mean civil and religious institutions. In the long run, the sword is always defeated by the spirit.”

When Péron and Freycinet were working on the nomenclature, Fontanes thus appeared as an exemplary figure of discernment, culture, and moral authority of the kind the regime wished to promote. In 1808, he was appointed Grand Master of the University, much to Fourcroy’s disappointment (see Île Fourcroy* in Victoria).
