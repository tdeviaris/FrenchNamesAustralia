Port Esperance is a southeastern Tasmanian bay where, today, the small town of Dover is located. In his journal, d’Entrecasteaux expressed his admiration for the beauty of the southeast Tasmanian coast, with its many deep bays providing shelter from the winds. As in May 1792 the expedition’s two ships sailed north on their way to chart the narrow D’Entrecasteaux Channel, a large bay appeared to port. The commander ordered small parties to survey the bay and the coves at its western end. One of the boats dispatched on the 20 May from the vessel l’Espérance was commanded by lieutenant Jean Baptiste Garreau de la Seinie, with on board the hydrographer Miroir Jouvency. They discovered in the west of the big bay a narrow but deep passage which seemed to them to be the mouth of a river. Rowing upstream, they found it to provide an anchorage sheltered from the winds. As d’Entrecasteaux noted in his journal, ‘they discovered an excellent harbour protected from the seas of the large bay’, where ships can be refitted and where fresh water is found in abundance. This sheltered inlet was given the name of Port de l’Espérance, to honour the second vessel of the expedition with the members of its crew who discovered it. The safe harbour charted by de la Seine and Jouvency is now recognised as the mouth of the Esperance River.
