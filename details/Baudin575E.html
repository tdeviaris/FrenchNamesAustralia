Cap Montesquieu – Cape Montesquieu

Îles Montesquieu – Montesquieu Islands

In The Spirit of Laws, Montesquieu set out the different forms of government — monarchy, republic, and despotism — together with the legal and political principles proper to each. It was there that he developed, among other ideas, the principle that power must be arranged so that “power checks power,” a formula that was to enjoy a very long posterity. He also stressed the importance of education, which he understood as a means of forming citizens and cultivating attachment to the nation. The work counted among Bonaparte’s youthful readings and was studied by him from his years of military training onward.

Although Napoléon did not in practice apply any strict separation of powers, The Spirit of Laws nonetheless remained an important point of reference within the intellectual horizon in which the Civil Code was elaborated. The jurists of the Consulate and the Empire found in it, among other things, a reflection on the distinction between the public and the private spheres, a distinction that would play a major role in the ordering of modern law.

Montesquieu’s work nevertheless adopted a more ambiguous position on certain questions, especially slavery and climate. On the one hand, in the famous chapter “On the Slavery of Negroes,” he used irony to ridicule the arguments of slaveholders and to denounce European brutality, the racism that justified the slave trade, and the Christian hypocrisy of its defenders. On the other hand, in the chapter dealing with the relation between the laws of civil slavery and the nature of climate, he argued that in certain hot countries heat weakened body and courage, so that slavery there “offended reason less.” This line of thought did not amount to a biological theory of race in the nineteenth-century sense, but its climatic determinism could provide an intellectual language compatible with certain orientations of Napoleonic policy, especially in the context of restoring colonial order, reassuring planters, reviving the sugar economy, and maintaining the French empire in rivalry with Britain.

The name of Montesquieu, assigned both to a cape and to a group of islands, thus testifies to the eminent place still occupied, under the Consulate and the Empire, by the author of The Spirit of Laws in the learned and political world to which Péron and Freycinet paid tribute.
