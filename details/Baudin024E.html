In this work, Montesquieu describes the different forms of government (monarchy, republican, despotic), with their respective laws, and developed theories such as the separation of the executive, legislative and judiciary powers ‘in order that the power stops the power’ (‘il faut que, par la disposition des choses, le pouvoir arrête le pouvoir’). He advocated the importance of education to instill in children a love for their nation. This was one of Bonaparte’s earlier favorite readings, that he studied first at his military school when he was an officer cadet. Although Napoléon did not separate the three powers, the Esprit des lois remains nonetheless a major benchmark reference in the making of the ‘code Napoléon’ (Civil Code). For example, Napoleon and the codifiers of the Civil Code took from L'Esprit des lois the theory of the distinction between the spheres of private and public conduct, regulating only the latter and organising legal procedures to protect the former, and thus separating private rights from public rights.
