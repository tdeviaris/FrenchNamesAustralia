Cap Ronsard - Cape Ronsard

Île Ronsard - Ronsard Island

Although embarked as a naval engineer, Ronsard performed much broader naval functions during the voyage. His initial technical responsibility was reflected in the construction of replacement longboats: first at Timor, after the loss of the Géographe’s longboat in Geographe Bay, and then a second time after the Timor-built longboat was lost in Bass Strait on 15 December 1802, swallowed by the sea as the Géographe was getting under way from King Island.

His activity, however, was not confined to naval construction. He took part in reconnaissance missions, carried out soundings, drew charts and kept journals particularly rich in information. In July 1801, he was notably entrusted with the exploration of Depuch Island. There he described the basalt formations with great precision, collected natural history specimens and drew a chart. Yet it was not this island that received his name, but a smaller island nearby, close to Depuch Island, which he had in fact not had time to reconnoitre.

Ronsard also took an interest in the very practical problems of life on board, in particular the poor design of the galley stoves installed on the ship. He observed that the models modified by Citizen Garreau, derived from the iron galley stoves designed by Kersaint, functioned badly at sea. The trials carried out on land, in the open air, had proved misleading: on board, the fire went out rapidly when the furnace doors were closed, for lack of air suitable for combustion; but when the doors were left open, the smoke spread through the gun deck instead of being drawn out through the vents provided for that purpose. The problem was serious: during bad weather in the English Channel, the cooks were so severely affected by the smoke that they spat blood.

Ronsard identified further defects: the impossibility of properly using the devices intended for roasting or grilling meat; the risk of fire, caused by the poor insulation of a galley stove set directly on the deck; and the infiltration of water from the deck into the between-decks through the draught pipes, causing rust and damaging the crates and sails stored nearby.

His analysis was therefore not merely a technical complaint. Ronsard sought to identify the physical causes of the malfunction and to outline a path towards improvement. He attributed the failures of Garreau’s galley stove to a misunderstanding of air circulation, combustion and the role of oxygen in the different parts of the vessel. In his view, Garreau’s modifications even represented a step backwards from Kersaint’s system. Ronsard concluded that the idea of iron galley stoves belonged properly to Kersaint, and that future improvements could only come from an inquiry grounded simultaneously in maritime experience, chemistry and the precise observation of real conditions on board.

During the stopover at Timor, in August–September 1801, Ronsard’s situation still appeared favourable from the point of view of advancement: he then received the functions of sub-lieutenant and subsequently lieutenant. Yet, still at Timor, but this time in May 1803, he became involved in a serious dispute with Henri Freycinet over seniority and command aboard the Géographe. Ronsard argued that, as a naval engineer benefiting from seniority by commission, he should take precedence over Freycinet and retain the position of first officer during Baudin’s absences, with the right to command the vessel in the event of the commander’s death.

Freycinet contested this claim, refusing to be placed under the authority of an engineer. Baudin, whose health was deteriorating, settled the matter by secretly consulting the crew and the naturalists. Freycinet was chosen by sixty votes to twelve and designated to take command of the vessel if necessary. Ronsard regarded this decision as a dishonourable demotion and asked to leave the expedition, but Baudin refused to put him ashore. He therefore continued the voyage solely in his original capacity as naval engineer, independent of the senior officers on board and reporting directly to Baudin.
