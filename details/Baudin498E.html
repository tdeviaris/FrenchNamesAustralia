Cap Ronsard - Cape Ronsard

Île Ronsard - Ronsard Island

Although embarked as a naval engineer, Ronsard fulfilled much broader naval functions during the voyage. His original technical role appears especially in the construction of replacement longboats: one at Timor, after the loss of the Géographe’s longboat in Geographe Bay, and another after the boat built at Timor was lost in Bass Strait on 15 December 1802, swallowed by the sea as the Géographe departed from King Island. But his activity extended far beyond shipbuilding. He kept journals, took part in reconnaissance, made soundings, drew charts, and exercised command during expeditions ashore.

His name is particularly associated with the exploration of Depuch Island in July 1801, where he described basaltic formations, collected natural-history specimens, and prepared a chart. Ronsard Island, located nearby, was later named in his honour. During the Timor stopover in August-September 1801, he received the functions of sub-lieutenant and then lieutenant.

Still at Timor, but this time in May 1803, Ronsard became involved in a serious dispute with Henri Freycinet over seniority and command aboard the Géographe. Ronsard argued that, as a naval engineer with long-standing brevet seniority, he had precedence over Freycinet and should remain first officer during Baudin’s absences, with a claim to command the ship should Baudin die. Freycinet contested this, refusing to be placed under the authority of an engineer. Baudin, whose health was deteriorating, resolved the matter by consulting the crew and the naturalists in a secret vote. Freycinet was chosen by 60 votes to 12 and designated to assume command of the ship if necessary. Ronsard regarded this decision as a dishonourable demotion and asked to leave the expedition, but Baudin refused to disembark him. He therefore continued the voyage solely in his original role as naval engineer, independent of the ship’s senior officers and answerable directly to Baudin.
