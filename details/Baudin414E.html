Cap Bouvard - Cape Bouvard

Alexis Bouvard (1767-1843) belonged to the circle of astronomers and mathematical calculators who, at the turn of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, gave French astronomy a central role in scientific voyaging, navigation and cartography. Born at Les Houches, in the Chamonix valley, he moved to Paris as a young man and followed a remarkable path of social and scientific advancement. Often described as self-taught, he entered the Paris Observatory in 1793 as an astronomer of the Observatory of the Republic, where he remained until his death.

In the context of the nomenclature derived from the Voyage de découvertes aux Terres australes, however, Bouvard should be placed within the precise period during which Péron and Freycinet, very probably Freycinet in the case of this toponym, selected and stabilised the names printed on the maps of the Australian coast. This work took shape after the return of the Géographe, between 1805-1806 and 1807, and continued through the editorial preparation of the voyage narrative and atlas into the years 1811-1812, before the effective publication of the nautical and geographical atlas.

By that time, Bouvard was already firmly established within French scientific institutions. He was an astronomer at the Paris Observatory, assistant astronomer at the Bureau des longitudes from 1795, a member of the Bureau from 1805, and a member of the Institut de France from 1803. His name therefore evokes the institutional astronomy mobilised by voyages of discovery: observations, positional calculations, ephemerides, astronomical tables and mathematical geography.

Bouvard was above all an observer and calculator. He discovered several comets and worked on the calculation of their orbits, a practice that brought him close to the major problems of celestial mechanics. In the circle of Pierre-Simon de Laplace, he contributed to the numerical calculations required to apply the laws of gravitation to the motions of celestial bodies. The inscription on his tomb records this intellectual and personal proximity: “He was the collaborator and friend of Laplace.”

The choice of his name belongs to a broader series of toponyms honouring the astronomers, mathematicians and geographers who formed the scientific environment of the expedition. Bouvard collaborated with, or belonged to the same institutional milieu as, several astronomers and mathematicians whose names were also commemorated in the nomenclature of the Voyage de découvertes aux Terres australes: Pierre-Simon de Laplace, Jean-Baptiste Joseph Delambre, Jérôme Lalande and Pierre Méchain. These names recall the role of the Bureau des longitudes, the Paris Observatory and the Institut in the scientific framing of the expedition, as well as in the transformation of observations made at sea into published maps.

Seen in this light, Cape Bouvard refers to the scientific infrastructure without which voyages of discovery could not produce reliable geography: astronomical observation, positional calculation, the reduction of data, the construction of tables and the making of maps. Bouvard’s observations at the Imperial Observatory in Paris in 1803 were used by Boullanger and Freycinet in preparing the maps for the atlas of the voyage. The toponym thus recalls the continuity between work carried out on board, the cabinet work undertaken after the expedition’s return, and the Parisian scientific institutions that supported the cartographic publication of the voyage.

Bouvard’s later career belongs to a second moment in the history of science, after the immediate context of the Baudin nomenclature. In 1808 he published new tables for Jupiter and Saturn, followed in 1821 by tables for Uranus. The latter became famous because Bouvard identified irregularities that could not be fully explained by the perturbations of the planets then known. He therefore considered the possibility of an unknown perturbing body, an hypothesis that found its full confirmation only after his death, when Urbain Le Verrier’s calculations led to the discovery of Neptune in 1846. This later history illuminates Bouvard’s enduring importance in astronomy, but it postdates the moment when his name was incorporated into the commemorative geography of the Baudin expedition.
