Golfe Joseph Bonaparte – Joseph Bonaparte Gulf

When Bonaparte became Consul, Joseph was entrusted with important diplomatic missions. He negotiated several decisive treaties: with the United States, the Treaty of Mortefontaine in 1800, which restored Franco-American relations and indirectly helped pave the way for the Louisiana Purchase in 1803; with Austria, the Peace of Lunéville, which ended Austrian participation in the War of the Second Coalition against France; and with Great Britain, the Treaty of Amiens in 1802.

Joseph then became Lieutenant General Commander-in-Chief of the Army of Naples and, from 31 March 1806, King of Naples. In 1808, Napoleon transferred him from Naples to Madrid, against his brother’s wishes, placing him on the Spanish throne in a deeply hostile context. There he was soon perceived as el rey intruso, the “intrusive king” — a foreign ruler imposed from outside — and became the object of intense political and popular hostility. He abdicated in 1813.

By the time the atlas of Baudin’s voyage was finally published in 1815, Joseph had gone into exile in Philadelphia, in the United States, where he lived under the name of the Comte de Survilliers. He returned to Europe only in the 1830s.
