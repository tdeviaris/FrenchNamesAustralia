Cap Murat - Point Murat

Baie Murat - Murat Bay

The son of an innkeeper, Murat experienced one of the most spectacular social ascents of his time. A daring cavalryman, an energetic and often theatrical commander, he came to embody the offensive spirit of Napoleonic cavalry. This character fully explains his place in a nomenclature that also includes Rivoli, Arcole, Muiron, Bessières, Sérurier and Victor: Murat belongs to the same memorial universe, that of the Italian campaign and of Revolutionary and Consular military glory.

As Bonaparte’s aide-de-camp during the first Italian campaign in 1796, Murat distinguished himself notably at Dego and Mondovì, and later played an important role during the second Italian campaign, especially at Marengo. Having become one of Napoleon’s closest military servants, he regularly fought at the head of the cavalry throughout the Consulate and the Empire. Péron and Freycinet thus remembered Murat as one of the great soldiers produced by the Revolution, associated with the men who surrounded the emperor at his coronation in Notre-Dame.

It is important, however, to place the toponym in its precise historical moment: the Murat honoured in the nomenclature of the Baudin expedition was not yet the King of Naples. He was, above all, a close associate of Bonaparte, through both arms and family. His marriage to Caroline Bonaparte in 1800 brought him into the dynastic circle of the future emperor. He was raised to the dignity of Marshal of the Empire in 1804, and was later appointed Grand Admiral, although he had never held a naval command. He then became a prince, Grand Duke of Berg and Cleves from 1806 to 1808, and finally King of Naples from 1808 to 1815. His Italian ambitions and his ill-judged attempt to conquer Italy ultimately helped estrange him from Napoleon, leading to their rupture and to Murat’s downfall.

The Baudin expedition anchored in Murat Bay in February 1803. From the anchorage, Baudin sent the longboat and the rowboat ashore to examine the surrounding area. Péron took part in one of these reconnaissance trips along the eastern shore of the bay, under the command of M. de Mont-Bazin according to his own account, but under Bonnefoy according to Baudin’s journal. In his own nomenclature, Baudin had initially named the place Baie des Saints, in reference to Saint Francis and Saint Peter islands, and the neighbouring cape Cap Bon Fond, in reference to the quality of the seabed. The name Murat, later retained in the nomenclature published by Péron and Freycinet, therefore inscribed this site within the military and political memory of the Napoleonic circle.
