Baie Mollien – Encounter Bay

Cap Mollien – no known English name (South Australia)

Cap Mollien – Cape Mollien (Western Australia)

Having entered the financial administration at a very young age under the Ancien Régime, Nicolas-François Mollien was recalled to public service under the Consulate. Appointed director of the Caisse d’amortissement, he became Minister of the Public Treasury in January 1806, in the aftermath of a severe financial crisis. He was among the most discreet, yet also among the most effective, ministers of the Napoleonic government. His work contributed to the reorganisation of the Treasury, the rationalisation of public cash flows, the stabilisation of relations between the State, public accountants and financial institutions, and the introduction of more rigorous accounting methods into the administration. Through his role in consolidating the Banque de France, establishing a durable system of public finance, and creating the Cour des comptes, Mollien took a full part in the material construction of the imperial State. The recurrent presence of his name in the nomenclature of the expedition — in both South Australia and Western Australia — further suggests the minister’s importance within the networks of support, funding and recognition that surrounded the publication of the Voyage de découvertes aux Terres australes.

Baie Mollien, now Encounter Bay, occupies a singular place in Australia’s maritime history. It corresponds to the area where, on 9 April 1802, Nicolas Baudin, commander of the Géographe, met Matthew Flinders, commander of the Investigator, at a time when France and Great Britain were engaged in an imperial, maritime and scientific rivalry. Flinders preserved the memory of this episode in the English name Encounter Bay. The French documents, however, reveal a more complex evolution: Baudin first used the names Baie des Invalides and Cap de la Rencontre, while the maps published by Freycinet introduced Baie Mollien and Cap Mollien. The manuscript maps preserved in the Archives nationales make it possible to trace this sequence of designations and revisions. Initially associated with the very event of the encounter between Baudin and Flinders, the cape was subsequently renamed Cap Fénelon, and then Cap Mollien. These substitutions testify to a genuine process of toponymic rewriting, through which a field nomenclature, tied to the immediate circumstances of the expedition, was gradually incorporated into the symbolic and honorific order of imperial cartography.

The figure of Adèle-Rosalie Collart-Dutilleul, wife of Nicolas-François Mollien, also belongs to this geography of dedication. Her first name was associated with Île Adèle, in Western Australia, and this designation hints at the role of family and social networks within imperial sociability. Adèle Mollien belonged to the circles of high administration and political connection; she later served as lady-in-waiting to Queen Marie-Amélie under the July Monarchy. Her importance derived less from any official function than from personal intercession, elite sociability and the networks of recommendation that surrounded Péron and the publication of the voyage. From this perspective, the ensemble formed by Île Adèle and Cap Mollien may be understood as the cartographic inscription of a couple situated at the heart of the administrative and social worlds of imperial France.

Finally, the superimposition of French and British place names should not obscure the prior and continuing presence of Indigenous geographies and systems of knowledge. Long before Baudin, Flinders and their crews entered these waters of Encounter Bay, this bay and its surrounding country were known, occupied, traversed and named by the Ramindjeri, a clan of the Ngarrindjeri people. According to information recorded in the twentieth century from Reuben Walker and synthesised by Norman B. Tindale, the Bluff was known as Longkuwar, and the bay as Ramong. European toponyms — whether French or English — were therefore not applied to an unnamed landscape, but were layered upon an already meaningful Indigenous geography, structured by ancestral connections, local knowledge, stories, movements, resource use and enduring relationships to Sea Country.

A similar caution is required for the Kimberley coast. Cape Mollien, on Adele Island, belongs not only to the history of French imperial cartography, but also to the Sea Country of Indigenous peoples of the north-west Australian coast, including the Mayala, Dambimangari, Wunambal Gaambera and Nyul Nyul peoples. In this context, the name Cape Mollien should be understood as one cartographic layer among others: a Napoleonic dedication imposed within a much older maritime cultural landscape, where islands, reefs, channels and coastal waters are embedded in systems of Traditional Ownership, law, memory and custodianship.
