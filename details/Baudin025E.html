Cap du Mont St Bernard - Cap du Mont St Bernard

The image of Napoleon Bonaparte crossing the Alps derives from one of Jacques-Louis David's celebrated representations of Bonaparte crossing the Alps at the Great Saint Bernard Pass. David produced five versions of this composition, which rapidly became one of the most famous images of Napoleon. Although the painting presents Bonaparte as a heroic leader mastering a spirited horse in a dramatic alpine landscape, this was a deliberately idealised image. In reality, Bonaparte crossed the snowy pass on a mule, a far more suitable animal for such difficult mountain terrain.

The crossing took place in May 1800, during the War of the Second Coalition. Bonaparte, then First Consul, led the Armee de Reserve across the Great Saint Bernard Pass in order to enter northern Italy, support Massena, who was under pressure at Genoa, and regain the strategic initiative against the Austrians. This audacious movement was followed by the French victory at Marengo on 14 June 1800.

David's painting transformed this military operation into a political and visual legend. At Napoleon's own request, the First Consul was shown "calm on a fiery horse", an image that emphasised command, courage and destiny rather than documentary accuracy. The names of Hannibal and Charlemagne, inscribed on the rocks in the painting, place Bonaparte in a lineage of great commanders who had crossed the Alps before him. The work thus formed part of Napoleon's wider propaganda campaign, helping to establish his public image as the new heroic leader of Europe.

The association with Mont St Bernard in the Baudin nomenclature therefore evokes not only the Alpine pass itself, but also the powerful Napoleonic imagery attached to it at the beginning of the nineteenth century. The name would have carried strong contemporary resonance: by 1801-1803, David's portrait had already begun to circulate as an emblem of Bonaparte's military audacity and political authority.
