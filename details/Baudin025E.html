In fact, the fiery horse, not very suitable for the crossing of a high snowy pass, was a mule with more sure-footedness, toughness, endurance, and natural cautiousness.

The painting by Jacques-Louis David*, which at Napoléon’s request portraits him as remaining ‘calm on a fiery horse’, contributed to this epic feat gaining legendary status. This was part of Napoléon’s propaganda campaign to establish his image as the indisputable leader of Europe.

Further information: Baudin named this cape ‘Cap Mont Blanc’ possibly because of its white appearance in the landscape, which Péron also noted in his description of this locality. Freycinet cites some rocky pitons, ‘of which he named the highest Mont-Saint Bernard’, as well as the ‘Cap du Mont-Saint-Bernard’.
