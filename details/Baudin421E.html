As François de Coursin has shown, the life of Louis Bonaparte (1778–1846) unfolded in three distinct phases, each tied to specific geopolitical and institutional challenges. Initially, as the younger brother of Napoleon I, he was drawn into the emerging imperial system. He took part in the Italian and Egyptian campaigns, was promoted to general in 1803, and became Grand Constable in 1804. This first period reflects his integration into Napoleonic power structures, where his role owed much to kinship with Bonaparte.

The second phase began in June 1806, when he became Lodewijk Napoleon I, King of Holland, ruling over a French satellite state. His arrival in Amsterdam was marked by a triumphal welcome, as the city became the political heart of his new kingdom. Anxious to integrate himself into his adopted nation, he sought to understand its customs and to master its language. A famous anecdote relates, however, that his unfortunate pronunciation of “King of Holland” was heard as “Rabbit of Holland”, giving rise to a phrase that long remained in popular memory.

Beyond this often-repeated episode, Louis-Napoleon showed genuine affection for the Dutch people. He tried to take account of their interests, traditions and aspirations, gradually distancing himself from the demands of French imperial policy. Tensions with Napoleon, who sought to impose economic and political dominance over the Kingdom of Holland, forced his abdication in July 1810. This episode reveals the limited sovereignty granted to vassal states in Napoleonic Europe.

Finally, from 1810 onward, Louis Bonaparte settled in Italy under the title Count of Saint-Leu. He led a more discreet life, partly devoted to memoir-writing and marked by bitter reflections on his past. Until his death in 1846, his trajectory embodied the decline of a historical figure whose influence, once tied to the Empire, gradually faded.
