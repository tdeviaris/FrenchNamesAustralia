Bonaparte valued Volney’s account of his voyage, Voyage en Syrie et en Egypte (1787), that made the author’s reputation. In this work, Volney provided geographical, social, economic and cultural information that Bonaparte’s armies found useful on subjects ranging from crops to military organisation, and social resentments within Egyptian society. He was a close advisor to Bonaparte until their relationship deteriorated, as Bonaparte rose to power as Emperor Napoleon and his authoritarian ambitions became clear. After being imprisoned during the Terror to defend the freedom of thought and barely saved from the guillotine, Volney spent some time in America (1795-1798) but did not find the opportunity to visit and live with the native Indian communities for any length of time, as he had done in Syria. Back in Paris, he published Tableau du climat et sol des États-Unis (1803), in which he expressed his disenchantment with the Indians cultures. This adverse change of perspective in the assessment of ‘savage’ societies, no more ‘noble’ (Rousseau), but ‘primitive’, influenced Péron’s narrative on aboriginal peoples and communities. Further information: Baudin called the cape Cap Lodi after the Battle of Lodi in Italy on 10 May 1796 between French forces under Bonaparte and Austrian forces. An island in South Australia also bears the name Volney.
