Île Volney - Franklin Harbor

Bonaparte valued Volney’s account of his voyage, Voyage en Syrie et en Egypte (1787), the work that established his reputation. In this book, Volney provided geographical, social, economic and cultural information that Bonaparte’s armies found useful on subjects ranging from agricultural production to military organisation, as well as the social tensions within Egyptian society. Volney was a close adviser to Bonaparte until their relationship deteriorated, as Bonaparte rose to power as Emperor Napoleon and his authoritarian ambitions became increasingly clear.

After being imprisoned during the Terror for defending freedom of thought, and narrowly escaping the guillotine, Volney spent some time in America between 1795 and 1798. There, however, he did not find the opportunity to visit Indigenous communities and live among them for any length of time, as he had done in Syria. Back in Paris, he published Tableau du climat et sol des États-Unis (1803), in which he expressed his disenchantment with Indigenous cultures. This shift toward a more negative appraisal of so-called “savage” societies, no longer seen as “noble” in the Rousseauian sense but as “primitive”, influenced Péron’s account of Aboriginal peoples and communities.

Further information: Baudin named Cape Volney “Cap Lodi”, after the Battle of Lodi fought in Italy on 10 May 1796 between the French forces led by Bonaparte and the Austrian army. A coastal barrier in South Australia also bears the name Volney.
