Gabriel Bonnot de Mably (1709-1785) was known for his critique of monarchical absolutism and enjoyed considerable moral authority. He published works on law, politics, and history. In his later years, he also developed a strong critique of Voltaire* and of his historical interpretations, especially those relating to Charlemagne. Whereas Mably, in his Observations sur l’histoire de France, presented Charlemagne as an enlightened and magnanimous ruler who had restored certain democratic forms, Voltaire, in his Histoire générale, portrayed him as cruel and despotic.

Mably was an idealist who promoted the model of the virtuous citizen: poor, sober, and devoted to the common good in a manner often compared to the Spartan ideal. These themes later inspired several leaders of the French Revolution. As a philosopher, however, he proved less influential than Voltaire*, whose more pragmatic reformism resonated more strongly with the upper bourgeoisie and the commercial classes.

Mably’s admiration for Sparta also influenced the young Bonaparte, who identified Greece with his native Corsica and sought in the ancient Greek cities a political model for its government. Yet, as he rose to power, Napoléon Bonaparte increasingly distanced himself from several of Mably’s central ideas, insofar as they conflicted with the more authoritarian direction of his rule, even though part of his own political formation had been shaped by the intellectual legacy of Mably.
