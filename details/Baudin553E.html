Cap Kéraudren - Cape Keraudren (Tasmania)

Pierre-François Kéraudren (1769-1858) was a Navy medical officer who, according to Péron, wrote “health instructions of the greatest interest” for the voyage, signed by the Navy medical inspector Augustin Coulomb. Péron praises the work on scurvy that Kéraudren published in 1804 (Brief Thoughts on Scurvy). In his thesis, Kéraudren differentiates between scurvy on land and scurvy at sea and, according to Péron, “rightly concludes that scurvy is an atonic disease of the vascular system”. He believed that “scurvy was caused by removal of warmth from the body and onset of sadness which in turn reduced the tone of blood vessels and allowed escape of blood from vessels via porosities” and “wondered if it was citric acid or some other common component of the fluids in the vegetables, which were inactive when desiccated but in fluid form could ironically in some circumstances provoke scurvy” (Tibballs, 2019). In this instance, Kéraudren follows Poissonnier’s ideas (see the entry Île Poissonnier* in SA) according to which the human body is divided into fluids that allow for the circulation of the scurvy, and solids.

However, twenties years later, knowledge of scurvy and how to protect against it had improved, and Kéraudren advised appropriately the commander of the voyage around the world on His Majesty's corvette La Coquille, Louis Isidore Duperrey, that oranges and lemons should be consumed as often as possible.
