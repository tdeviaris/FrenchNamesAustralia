Jean-Baptiste de Nompère de Champagny, duc de Cadore (1756-1834), was one of the senior administrative and diplomatic figures of the Napoleonic Empire. A former naval officer and diplomat, he served as Minister of the Interior from 1804 to 1807, before succeeding Talleyrand as Minister of Foreign Affairs from 1807 to 1811.

As Minister of the Interior, Champagny was involved in the consolidation of imperial administration, public works and cultural policy. As Foreign Minister, he took part in some of the major diplomatic affairs of the Empire, including the Continental System, the Spanish and Portuguese questions, the Treaty of Schönbrunn, and the negotiations surrounding Napoleon's marriage to Marie-Louise of Austria.

His influence, however, was that of a loyal and efficient servant of Napoleon rather than that of an independent political strategist. Thierry Lentz has underlined that Napoleon valued such ministers precisely for their obedience and administrative reliability. It was Champagny who entrusted Péron, together with his friend Lesueur, with the task of preparing the historical narrative of the voyage of discovery and the description of the natural-history collections.
