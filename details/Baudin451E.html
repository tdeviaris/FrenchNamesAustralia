Îlot Lefebvre - Lefebre Island

In Hamelin's journal, in the entry for 15-18 Prairial, Year IX (4-7 June 1801), p. 194, a passage concerning Geographe Bay explicitly commends Lefèvre, together with Bourgeois and Mounier, for his courage and composure in difficult circumstances.

Hamelin writes:

"As the swell had greatly increased and the wind had not yet completely abated, the large sailing cutter returned on board, and I ordered mine to proceed under tow as close as possible to the stretch of coast nearest the vessels, while I myself continued for several leagues along the shoreline on foot, over the sand. At about eight o'clock it rejoined me roughly three leagues to windward of the river's mouth, and the coxswain informed me that the very heavy surf along the coast had caused the boat to strand there, and that without the courage and goodwill of Bourgeois, Lefèvre, and Mounier-his three boatmen-it would have been lost; but that they had rendered him powerful assistance in getting it afloat again. He added that the Géographe's boat was stranded one league to leeward of his position, in a place where the sea was too heavy for him to refloat it during the night. Mr Le Bas immediately made his way there, and shortly afterwards, as did my own boat, I joined him to offer what little assistance I could..."

This testimony shows that Lefèvre was one of the seamen whose skill and determination were recognized by the officers of the expedition. These qualities are also evident in an episode recounted by Péron at the Josephine Islands, where Lefebvre's presence and resolve proved decisive in saving the naturalist's life. Believing the passage to be shallow, Péron explains:

"We believed that there was little water everywhere, and, without even suspecting any kind of difficulty in this passage, we undertook it. Scarcely had we completed a quarter of the distance when the water was already up to our waists, and the tide was rising visibly. It was impossible to turn back; we were compelled to continue our perilous undertaking. Soon the sea rose to the level of our chests. Unskilled in the art of swimming and exhausted by my efforts, I found myself in imminent danger. The devoted zeal of the good Lefèvre saved me: he alone guided me amid the ever-rising waves; in the deepest places he lent me a helping arm; in short, this courageous man omitted nothing that might avert my destruction..."

Another example of his bravery occurred in Shark Bay, where Lefebvre was attacked by a shark. In this moment of extreme danger, he showed remarkable composure. Péron reports:

"The western coast of Faure Island is infested with sharks of equally remarkable size and voracity. One of these monsters very nearly devoured this same Lefèvre who had saved my life at the Josephine Islands (vol. II, p. ...); he was already knocked down, and the terrible squale was about to engulf him, when three other sailors, rushing to his cries, succeeded in wrenching him from the animal's jaws. Enraged at being thus deprived of its prey, the shark repeatedly charged at the sailor, managed to tear away part of his clothing, and did not withdraw until it had received five wounds."
