During the Napoleonic period, the works of Charles Rollin retained an important place in French historical and educational culture. His account of Roman history was particularly well suited to the period's interest in civic virtue, military discipline, political exemplarity and the monumental representation of the past.

Napoleon did not merely read Rollin in his youth; he appears to have continued consulting his works throughout his life. The catalogue of his library at Saint Helena included several volumes of Rollin's Histoire romaine. A surviving copy of the fourth volume, dealing in part with the Punic Wars, is also said to contain pencil annotations attributed to Napoleon. These notes would appear to reflect his particular interest in troop numbers, the organisation of military corps and the numerical information recorded by Rollin.

For the officers, administrators and elites of the First Empire, Rollin's detailed accounts of Roman strategy, the Punic Wars and the transition from Republic to Empire could provide a useful historical framework for understanding the political and symbolic references employed by the Napoleonic regime.

Although some Enlightenment thinkers, including Voltaire and Montesquieu, criticised Rollin for his piety, moralising approach or insufficient distance from certain traditional narratives, his works remained widely read. Their success rested in part on two complementary qualities. Rollin was an accomplished storyteller: rather than presenting history as a simple sequence of dates and events, he gave battles, speeches and triumphs a vivid and dramatic form. He also offered a moral interpretation of history, according to which empires rise through discipline and civic virtue before declining through luxury, corruption and the weakening of public morals.

Such a reading may have resonated particularly strongly with a generation that had witnessed the fall of the French monarchy, the upheavals of the Revolution and the establishment of a new imperial order.
