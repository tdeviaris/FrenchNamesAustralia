The Place Vendôme, developed in Paris during the reign of Louis XIV, was named in honor of this lineage. It was designed as a symbol of monarchical and aristocratic prestige. The vast space is surrounded by architecturally imposing buildings in the neo-classical style, constructed in the late 17th and 18th centuries, and has at its centre a tall column, ordered by Napoléon, and built under the supervision of Vivan Denon* with a statue of Napoléon at the top. The bronze used in its manufacture came from captured Austrian and Russian canons, to commemorate the battle of Austerlitz in 1805. The column received the name of ‘Austerlitz’, then ‘Victory’ before becoming ‘column of the Great Army’. The statue was unbolted at the time of the fall of Napoléon 1st in 1814, restored on several occasions, unbolted again in 1871, then rebuilt at the end of the XIX century. It was only after the fall of Napoléon that it gradually took on the name of ‘colonne Vendôme’.
