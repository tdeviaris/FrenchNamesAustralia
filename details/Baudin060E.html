Baie Vendome - Aldinga Bay

Cap Vendome - Point Marsden

Developed in Paris under Louis XIV, Place Vendome took its name from the former House of Vendome. Conceived as a setting of monarchical and aristocratic prestige, it is surrounded by imposing classical townhouses built in the late seventeenth and eighteenth centuries.

At its centre rises the Vendome Column. After several earlier projects, its construction was confirmed by Napoleon in a decree of 1 January 1806, following the victory at Austerlitz. Modelled on Trajan's Column, it was promoted by Dominique-Vivant Denon and designed by the architects Jean-Baptiste Lepere and Jacques Gondouin. Its spiral reliefs commemorate the campaign of 1805, while much of its bronze came from captured Austrian and Russian cannon.

Known successively as the Column of Austerlitz, the Column of Victory and the Column of the Grande Armee, it was originally topped by a statue of Napoleon in Roman dress. Removed in 1814, replaced during the Restoration by a fleur-de-lis flag, and then by an image of Napoleon as the "Little Corporal" under the July Monarchy, it underwent several transformations before the column itself was pulled down during the Commune. The designation "Vendome Column" only gradually became established after the fall of the Empire.

Further information. Baudin named the bay Anse des Curieux ("Cove of the Curious").
