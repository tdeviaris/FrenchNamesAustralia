In this extract from Voyage de découvertes aux terres australes exécuté sur les corvettes le Géographe, le Naturaliste et la goélette le Casuarina pendant les années 1800, 1801, 1802, 1803 et 1804, Freycinet does not invoke this memoir by Lavoisier and his colleagues merely as a scholarly authority. Rather, he sees in it evidence of a line of thought directly shaped by the material constraints of long-distance navigation. Fresh water was needed not only for drinking, but also for washing clothes and hammocks and, more broadly, for combating dampness and insanitary conditions on board. Within the ship’s sanitary economy, the still thus appeared as a valuable technical resource, capable of producing distilled water when watering places were unavailable. Freycinet did not, however, regard it as a complete solution. The water obtained by distillation remained of limited use, both because of the apparatus’s restricted productive capacity and because of the persistent reservations inspired by its taste. The importance of the memoir co-signed by Lavoisier therefore lay less in the promise of complete autonomy than in the prospect of a practical improvement in freshwater supplies on board, at a time when access to coastal watering places remained essential to the safety and duration of the voyage.

In France, Lavoisier was long regarded as the founder of chemistry as an autonomous scientific discipline. He helped define matter through the property of mass, introduced the systematic use of the balance, which he took great care to refine, and articulated the principles of the conservation of mass and elements, often summarized in the maxim: “nothing is lost, nothing is created, everything is transformed.”
