Fénélon was appointed tutor to the Duke of Burgundy, grandson of Louis XIV. A theologian, moralist and educator, he wrote numerous works, including the Treatise on the Existence of God and Les Aventures de Télémaque.

His friendship with Bossuet ended during the Quietist controversy, which concerned prayer, pure love of God and forms of spiritual experience. Fénélon defended the possibility of an inward and disinterested relationship with God, while Bossuet regarded such claims as a dangerous deviation, capable of weakening the authority of the Church and its institutions. This spiritual and doctrinal dispute also had political implications, since it touched on questions of authority, influence and power at court. In 1699, several propositions drawn from Fénélon's Explication des maximes des saints were condemned by Rome.

Les Aventures de Télémaque became one of the most widely read French books of the eighteenth century. It was received both as an educational narrative, a literary classic and an implicit criticism of absolutism, luxury, conquest and bad government. For this reason, it appealed strongly to Enlightenment readers and circulated widely in France and abroad.

Fénélon's writings remained influential during the Napoleonic period, though not all in the same way. His religious and spiritual works continued to be respected, but it was above all Les Aventures de Télémaque that continued to be read, reprinted and used in education. The book remained a school classic and a model of moral and political literature. A Latin verse version was even published in Paris in 1808, showing the prestige the work still enjoyed under the Empire.

There is also a revealing Napoleonic connection: Télémaque was reportedly among the books Napoleon, at the end of his life, wished to set aside for his son, Napoleon II. This does not mean that Napoleon adopted Fénélon's anti-war or anti-absolutist values, but it does show that Fénélon's work was still regarded as a formative classic for princely education.
