Ile Fourcroy - Lady Julia Percy Island

Cap Fourcroy - Cape Fourcroy

Peron studied chemistry and pharmacology under Fourcroy after Fourcroy's appointment as professor of chemistry in Paris at the Jardin du Roi, renamed the Museum national d'Histoire naturelle after the French Revolution. Fourcroy was a celebrated teacher and a pioneer in chemical science, laying the groundwork for what we now call biochemistry. Trained as a physician, he submitted a thesis on the use of quinine in the treatment of intermittent fevers for his medical degree but did not practise medicine.

His most important scientific contribution was his collaboration with the acclaimed chemist Antoine Lavoisier on the new standardized chemical nomenclature published in 1787. This reform replaced many older alchemical or descriptive names, often based on appearance, discoverers or places, with terms derived from chemical composition. Thus, for example, "philosopher's wool", also known as "flowers of zinc", became zinc oxide. Fourcroy also carried out extensive research on mineral analysis, demonstrated that aragonite and calcite were two forms of calcium carbonate, and helped initiate the method of proximate analysis, used to determine the general composition of organic substances. He was the author or co-author of numerous scientific publications.

Fourcroy was also a prominent political figure. He held public office under the revolutionary government and was later appointed by Bonaparte to the Council of State, where he served in the Section of the Interior. He played a major role in the creation and organization of a more effective public education system, including primary schools, colleges and lycees, as well as specialized schools in fields such as medicine and chemistry. Although Fourcroy has sometimes been accused of involvement in Lavoisier's arrest and execution, his direct participation has never been proved.

It was therefore in Fourcroy's capacity as Councillor of State that Peron addressed to him his Memoir on the English Settlements in New Holland, in the Land of Diemen and in the Archipelagoes of the Great Pacific Ocean, a text that described these territories from a colonial and strategic perspective. There is no tangible evidence that Fourcroy requested such a report, or even that he received the memoir. Yet Peron's choice of addressee shows that he regarded Fourcroy not only as a great scientist who taught him how to analyze the biology of "new" species using modern chemical methods, but also as an influential political figure who might help promote the voyage and the potential value of the Terres Australes for France.

As Jean Fornasiero and John West-Sooby have written of this memoir:

"As fate would have it, Peron would in a few short years have the opportunity of emulating the political activism of his masters, for it is to this selfsame Fourcroy, in his capacity as Counsellor of State, that Francois Peron, on his return from the Antipodes, would address a report that had nothing to do with chemistry and everything to do with politics."

Further information: To the Gunditjmara and Eastern Maar people, Ile Fourcroy - Lady Julia Percy Island is where the spirits of the dead go before journeying to their next destination in the afterlife.
