Baudin purchased the Casuarina at Port Jackson in October 1802 in order to carry out more detailed surveys in shallow waters. This small schooner was intended to support the hydrographic work of Le Géographe and to allow the expedition to examine more closely the still imperfectly known coasts of New Holland. The undertaking was both scientific and nautical: Baudin, like Matthew Flinders, was seeking to produce the most accurate possible charts of these coastal regions, much of which still remained unknown to Europeans.

In a letter written at Port Jackson on 11 November 1802, or 20 Brumaire Year XI, and addressed to “Citizen Jussieu”, professor at the Muséum national d’Histoire naturelle in Paris, Baudin explained his decision:

“In replacement of the Naturaliste, I decided to purchase a small vessel of thirty tons, which I have named the Casuarina, because it is built largely from the wood that bears that name. This small craft will henceforth accompany me and will be of the greatest use. Had I had it earlier, several places into which I was unable to penetrate would not have remained unexamined. Its shallow draught will enable me to go close inshore almost everywhere; I shall now be able to examine everything and leave nothing for those who may follow us in such work.”

Baudin placed the Casuarina under the command of his young officer Louis de Freycinet. Freycinet accepted the post, but recorded in his journal that, on his first visit aboard, he noticed that the vessel was taking in “five inches of water a day”. He also judged that the Casuarina would undoubtedly have “poor qualities at sea”, making navigation both difficult and uncomfortable. Since Baudin had authorised him to make alterations, Freycinet modified the vessel by adding weight to the stern and setting the mast in a vertical position, in an effort to improve her handling.

Baudin also set out the conditions of Freycinet’s command:

“Citizen Brevedent, midshipman, will be the only officer serving with you on this small vessel. He will dine at your table, but will sleep forward in the cabin that has been made for him. Your crew will consist of fourteen men in all; they will be chosen as well as possible.”

The Casuarina thus became the expedition’s principal instrument for close coastal reconnaissance. Thanks to her shallow draught, she could enter waters that Le Géographe could not safely reach, approach low and intricate shores, sound passages, identify banks and shoals, and complete the survey of several important sections of the Australian coast.
