The name Buache refers to an important lineage of French geographers. Philippe Buache was one of the great geographers of the eighteenth century, close to government circles and well known for his physical geography, which paid particular attention to relief, basins, and the major structures of the globe. His nephew, Jean-Nicolas Buache de La Neuville, followed in his footsteps and was the last geographer to hold the title of First Geographer to the King. After the French Revolution, he became involved in the main scholarly and administrative institutions of the new regime: he taught geography at the École normale, participated in the Bureau des longitudes and the Institut, and held an important position at the Dépôt des cartes et plans de la Marine, where he later became keeper and then conservator.

The three illustrations associated with this entry help explain this double lineage: on the one hand, the intellectual legacy of Philippe Buache; on the other, the more direct role played by Jean-Nicolas Buache in the documentary and geographical preparation of Baudin's voyage.

The first illustration represents the globe according to Philippe Buache. Although it is not precisely dated, this map clearly shows the southern lands, while the Antarctic continent does not yet appear as a recognized space. Philippe Buache marked on it the continental and oceanic mountain ranges, which he conceived as the great frameworks of the planet. In the absence of available information, he left large parts of the southern lands blank.

The second illustration is taken from the notes that Jean-Nicolas Buache de La Neuville compiled and gave to Commander Baudin. These were probably also notes derived from his teaching or preparatory work. The documents summarized the knowledge then available to Europeans about the southern lands and islands. In note no. 10, devoted to New Holland, Buache put forward the hypothesis of a connection between some populations of northern and north-eastern New Holland and those of New Guinea:

“It is likely that part of the peoples of the north and north-east of New Holland come from New Guinea, or at least greatly resemble them in manners, customs, languages, etc. It is therefore important first to obtain somewhat extensive knowledge about New Guinea...”

This remark shows that Buache reasoned from geographical, ethnographic, and linguistic comparisons that remained highly hypothetical. It also reveals the limits of the information available to him when he advised Baudin. He probably had not seen, or had not yet seen in detail, the geographical instructions given to the expedition: its itinerary included Timor as a provisioning stop, but not New Guinea.

The third illustration is taken from the Mémoire sur des découvertes à faire dans le Grand Océan, read on 17 Vendémiaire Year VII by Citizen Buache, that is, Jean-Nicolas Buache de La Neuville. Written in the context of preparations for major French expeditions to the Pacific and the southern lands, this memoir sought to guide future research in the Great Ocean. It offered a synthesis of available knowledge, compared recent discoveries - especially those of James Cook - with older maps and accounts, and identified the regions where coasts, archipelagos, or sea routes still remained uncertain.

The fold-out map that accompanies this memoir is particularly useful for understanding the reconnaissance objectives entrusted to Baudin. Today, it is difficult to read online in its entirety, because the accessible copy has not been fully unfolded: the section where eastern New Holland and Van Diemen's Land should appear lies in the fold and is therefore not visible. Despite this limitation, the map shows the importance Buache attached to spaces that had not yet been fully surveyed. The sections of coast shown with dotted lines marked poorly known or uncertain areas; Baudin stopped precisely at several of these zones, including Geographe Bay, parts of the Land of Witt, and the coasts of present-day South Australia.

Jean-Nicolas Buache also supplied Baudin with documents held at the Dépôt des cartes et plans de la Marine concerning earlier voyages, navigators - notably James Cook and La Pérouse - as well as scientific voyagers such as Joseph Banks and Daniel Solander. He also annotated some captains' and officers' journals, commenting on the discoveries they claimed. Through this work of compilation, comparison, and critique, he helped situate Baudin's voyage within a cumulative history of European exploration in the Pacific.

Like his uncle Philippe, Jean-Nicolas Buache attached fundamental importance to mountain ranges and to the hydrographical basins they structure. In this conception, relief forms the framework of the Earth and organizes the distribution of waters, coasts, and territories. The fact that the Baudin expedition discovered neither major hydrographical basins nor large river mouths in New Holland was therefore both disappointing and difficult for Buache and his colleagues to interpret.

The toponyms Cape Buache, Port Buache, and Île Buache form part of this homage to the scholarly world of French cartography. While they evoke the intellectual legacy of Philippe Buache, they almost certainly refer to Jean-Nicolas Buache de La Neuville, who played a direct role in the documentary preparation of Baudin's voyage. They thus recall the institutional importance of the Dépôt des cartes et plans de la Marine, the geographical culture in which the expedition was prepared, and the enduring influence of a geography attentive to the deep structures of the globe: reliefs, basins, and the organizing lines of the Earth.
