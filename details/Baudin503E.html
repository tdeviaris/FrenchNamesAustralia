Cap Larrey - Larrey Point

Dominique Jean Larrey (1766-1842) is widely regarded as one of the founders of modern military medicine. After serving as ship's doctor on the royal frigate La Vigilante, and later practising at the Royal Hospital of the Invalides and in several other medical institutions, he was appointed surgeon-major of the Army of the Rhine in 1792. François Péron was also sent to that same army at the time. Larrey first served under the authority of Pierre-François Percy.

Although the relationship between the two physicians has sometimes been described as marked by rivalry, both understood the need to reach the wounded during the battle itself, without waiting for the fighting to end. Larrey is especially known for having designed and developed the “flying ambulance”, a mobile medical unit intended to bring rapid assistance to the wounded on the battlefield.

He also devised a casualty transport system that became a model for military medicine and was put into practice during the Italian campaign of 1797-1798. Two- and four-wheeled horse-drawn vehicles carried the wounded: in the first type, patients could lie full length; in the second, they were carried with their legs slightly bent. The vehicles were fitted with windows for ventilation, while side compartments were used to store bottles, medicines, dressings, and other items needed for first aid.

Once the first injuries had been dressed, the wounded were rapidly conveyed to evacuation hospitals behind the lines, where surgeons could operate under better conditions. Larrey's practice came to be associated with what was later called the “twenty-four hour principle”: the idea that a severely shattered limb should be amputated within the day following the injury, before infection or systemic shock drastically reduced the patient's chances of survival. In this respect, Larrey's approach differed from that of Percy, whose ambulances chiefly aimed to bring surgeons and their instruments close to the wounded so that operations could be performed on the spot.

During the Egyptian campaign, Larrey adapted the flying ambulance to local conditions. Horses were replaced by camels, and wheeled vehicles by large cane baskets or beds in which the wounded could be transported on the animals' backs. Back in France, he also founded a school for the training of military surgeons. During the Syrian campaign, his devotion to the wounded earned him the nickname “the soldier's providence”.

After the Egyptian campaign, Larrey was appointed chief surgeon to the Consular Guard in 1802, and then to the Imperial Guard in 1804.
