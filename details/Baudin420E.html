Isle Heirisson - Heirisson Island - Matagarup

Cap Heirisson - Cape Heirisson

The name Heirisson — also spelled Heyrisson — is associated with several hydrographic reconnaissances carried out by the Baudin expedition on the western coast of New Holland. François Antoine Boniface Heirisson (1776-1834), an ensign aboard the Naturaliste, took part in the work conducted in both the Swan River and Shark Bay.

Off Rottnest Island, on 27 Prairial Year IX, Hamelin entrusted him with the task of reconnoitring the Swan River, already recorded by Dutch navigators but still incorrectly positioned on the charts available to the expedition. The mission order specified that Heirisson was to search for the river’s entrance, enter it as far as possible without exposing his boat to the risk of grounding, and then determine its “position, course, widths and depths”. He was also to identify the point at which fresh water first appeared, bring at least one bottle of it back on board, observe the height of the tide and the time of high water, protect the naturalists Bailly and Colas, and avoid any confrontation with the inhabitants of the country.

Leaving the ship on 28 Prairial Year IX (16 June 1801), Heirisson reached the river mouth the same day. He ascended the river over several days, negotiating difficult channels and shallow reaches where the longboat ran aground more than once. His report states that he surveyed about seven leagues of the river on the first day, then continued to around twenty leagues from its mouth. He regularly tasted the river water, which he always found to be salty, before discovering a spring on the bank, where he obtained fresh water and filled the boat’s barrels on the return journey.

This mission produced one of the earliest French surveys of the Swan River. Heirisson’s sketches show, in particular, Entrée Moreau and Île Heirisson, which the French interpreted at the time as one of the islets marking the course of the river. The name Matagarup now recalls the importance of this site in Nyoongar history, while the Heirisson toponym preserves the memory of the hydrographic reconnaissance carried out in June 1801.

Heirisson later took part in another mission in Shark Bay with the engineer-geographer Faure. Supplied with six days’ provisions, he was to follow the inner perimeter of the bay, while Faure carried out its geographical survey. Heirisson was more specifically responsible for observing the tides and ensuring the safety of the boat. The mission took the two men along Dirk Hartog Island as far as Passage Épineux. Contrary winds, however, prevented them from following the planned itinerary in full and forced them to seek shelter under Dirk Hartog, where the crew spent a difficult night. As the fourth day of provisions had already begun, Heirisson and Faure gave up reconnoitring an inlet not shown on their plan and turned instead towards the land lying to the east.

On his return, Heirisson submitted a written report to Hamelin, much more summary than that of his colleague Faure and those of other officers. In it, he explained that east-south-easterly winds had prevented him from reaching the island situated in the middle of the bay, and that he had been forced to follow Dirk Hartog, tack repeatedly, and anchor in difficult conditions. Although Hamelin at first appeared to approve his conduct, the episode soon degenerated into a personal conflict. Heirisson, believing that he had been slandered by his commander, tore from its board the chart of the Swan River on which he was still working. The dispute then centred on an earlier mission report which Hamelin demanded and which Heirisson said he had torn up. Unable to produce it, he was confined to his cabin under arrest for twenty-two days.

These episodes present Heirisson as an important, though sometimes contentious, participant in the hydrographic work of the Naturaliste. His missions combined coastal reconnaissance, the search for fresh water, tidal observation, boat safety and cartographic survey. They also reveal the practical constraints of exploration: contrary winds, uncertain channels, shoals, groundings, shortage of fresh water and hierarchical tensions on board.
