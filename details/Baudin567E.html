Louis-Alexandre Berthier was one of Bonaparte’s, and later Napoléon’s, closest and most constant collaborators. It was with the Army of Italy that he truly entered Bonaparte’s inner circle, when the latter, having chosen him as his chief of staff, praised him as a man of “talent, activity, courage, and character.” For nearly eighteen years, Berthier remained at his side, in war as at court, relaying orders, organising troop movements, and ensuring, with well-known precision, the practical execution of his master’s military designs.

Under the Consulate and then the Empire, he occupied a central place in the functioning of Napoléon’s military apparatus. As Major General of the Grande Armée, he also served as Minister of War from 1800 to 1807, reforming the bureaux, the administration, the organisation of units, recruitment, supply, and logistics. Constantly beside Napoléon in the field, he transmitted his orders, oversaw their execution, and, when necessary, could stand in for him in command. In this capacity, he was one of the principal architects of the Empire’s military effectiveness. This exceptional closeness to Napoléon no doubt explains why, at the very moment when Péron was working on the nomenclature of the voyage, he assigned Berthier three place names, thereby inscribing into the coastal geography of the expedition the memory of one of the regime’s most eminent servants.

This political and military intimacy was also reflected in an accumulation of dignities and honours. Berthier was made Marshal of the Empire in the first promotion, then Grand Huntsman, Grand Eagle of the Légion d’honneur, Prince of Neuchâtel and Valangin, Vice-Constable, Prince of Wagram, and Colonel-General of the Swiss. Napoléon thus integrated him into the highest circle of the regime, making him at once an indispensable servant, a great dignitary of the Empire, and almost a member of his political family.

Their relationship nevertheless became strained in its later years, especially over the Russian campaign and the strategic decisions of 1813–1814. More cautious by temperament, Berthier showed reservations about some of the Emperor’s choices. After the abdication of 1814, he rallied to the new regime, which helped tarnish his image among the most faithful Napoleonic supporters. Napoléon himself, at Saint Helena, pronounced bitter judgments upon him, while still acknowledging his “regularity,” his “accuracy,” his “promptness,” and his “value.”
