Île Borda - Borda Island

Jean-Charles de Borda was commemorated by four Australian place names. Significantly, Baudin himself proposed that Kangaroo Island should be named Île Borda. These names reflect the very high esteem in which Borda was held by French naval officers, owing to his major contributions to nautical science, navigation and precision instruments.

Borda’s name remained closely associated with the reflecting circle, an improved instrument for angular measurement used in navigation and highly valued by officers responsible for coastal surveys and hydrographic work. Thus, at Port Jackson, Sydney, in November 1802, Louis de Freycinet expressly asked Nicolas Baudin, unsuccessfully, for one of Borda’s circles to use aboard the Casuarina. Graduated to 720 degrees, the Borda circle enabled navigators to measure the angular distance between two celestial bodies with greater accuracy than was possible with an octant or sextant. This precision was particularly valuable in determining longitude at sea.

The instrument worked by allowing the same angle to be measured twice: first directly through the telescope, and then by reflection, using a small semi-reflecting mirror placed at the centre of the circle. This repeated measurement helped reduce observational error. For this reason, the instrument was also known as the reflecting circle or the repeating circle.

Borda also played an important role in the establishment of the metric system, working with his colleagues at the Bureau des longitudes. His mathematical and instrumental expertise thus formed part of the wider scientific reforms of the late eighteenth century.
