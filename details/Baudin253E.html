Cap Latouche-Treville - Cape Latouche Treville

Baie Treville - Smoky Bay (southern portion)

Louis-René-Madeleine Le Vassor de La Touche, Count of Tréville, better known as Latouche-Treville (1745-1804), became particularly renowned during the American Revolutionary War. In 1780, he commanded the frigate L'Hermione, which carried La Fayette* to Boston. He later took part in the Battle of Cape Henry, also known as the First Battle of the Chesapeake, in 1781.

After returning to France, he replaced D'Entrecasteaux* in 1785 as associate director of ports and naval dockyards. Imprisoned during the Terror, he returned to active service only in November 1799, after Bonaparte became First Consul. He was then one of the very few naval officers to be praised by Napoleon Bonaparte.

In 1801, Latouche-Treville was given command of the Boulogne flotilla. His main task was to arm and train divisions of gunboats, launches and other light craft, with a view to transporting an invasion force to England. When Nelson attacked the flotilla in August 1801, Latouche-Treville used the tactic known in French as embossage, arranging the vessels in staggered lines so that they could protect one another. On three occasions, he succeeded in holding in check the man he liked to call his “colleague”, Admiral Nelson.

At the end of 1801, he sailed for Saint-Domingue, where he captured Port-au-Prince. In December 1803, he was appointed vice-admiral and commander-in-chief of the Mediterranean squadron, which was intended to play a role in Napoleon's “grand design” for the invasion of England. Latouche-Treville was known as an outstanding leader and trainer of men, able to inspire his crews with enthusiasm, confidence and energy.

Seriously ill, or exhausted by the strain of command, he died during the night of 28 to 29 August 1804, on board his flagship Le Bucentaure. He is said to have uttered these memorable last words: “A naval officer must die under the flag of his ship.”
