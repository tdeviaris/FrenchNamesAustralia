Île Corvisart – Corvisart Island (Western Australia)

Baie Corvisart - Corvisart Bay (South Australia)

Cap Hallé – Cape Radstock

Corvisart was the chief medical officer of the government, a position that included responsibility for the health service of the imperial household, whereas Hallé served as médecin ordinaire de l’Empereur and attended other members of the Bonaparte family. Hallé and Corvisart were close associates and were both deeply involved in promoting vaccination against smallpox (petite vérole, or variole) in France and across the Napoleonic Empire (see also entry Pte Liancourt). On this question, however, Baudin received instructions not from Hallé or Corvisart, but from the German physician Franz Anton Mesmer, then residing in Paris, who supplied him with a memoir entitled Recherches à faire au sujet de la petite vérole. By that time, Mesmer’s theories concerning magnetism, hypnosis, and natural immunisation through substances derived from the umbilical cord were already beginning to attract scepticism.

Although Péron did not adopt Mesmer’s ideas, he nevertheless investigated the diffusion of smallpox in ways that paralleled the concerns of Hallé and Corvisart in their work on vaccination. Thus, during the stopover of Le Géographe at the Isle de France (Mauritius), Péron discussed the distribution of the vaccine on the island with the chief medical officer, Léonard Clair Laborde. When a slave ship arriving from Mozambique brought smallpox to the island, Laborde took six recently vaccinated children on board, whose immunity protected them from the disease. He then exposed them to infection from the sick enslaved people and drew fresh vaccine matter from their pustules, enabling him and his colleagues to extend immunisation on the island.

Hallé and Corvisart both became members of the Institut, although Corvisart was elected only in 1816.

In his narrative, Péron referred implicitly to Hallé’s teachings when he reproached his commander, Baudin, for what he described as a disregard for the hygienic instructions given in Paris to ensure the health of the crew and the safety of the voyage. He wrote (Voyage, vol. I, p. 345): “Finally, our own misfortunes may furnish a great and terrible lesson to navigators: these misfortunes, in fact, had no other cause than the contempt of our chief for all the precautions most essential to the health of the men; he ignored all the generous orders of the government in this regard; he disdained all the instructions which had been given to him in Europe; he imposed, at all times of the journey, the most horrible privations on his crew and his patients.” Baudin was nonetheless concerned with hygiene and the health of his crew, as shown by this entry in his sea journal during the visit to King George Sound on 18 February 1803: “I boarded my canoe and went personally to inspect the watering place, to ensure it was as accessible and convenient as reported. Upon examination, I indeed found it to be so, and I immediately ordered our tents to be set up—one designated for the sick, of whom we then had four, and the other for the naturalists. I also arranged for a large reservoir to be prepared for washing the crew’s linens and hammocks, intending to make the most of the opportunity to have them cleaned.”

Hallé taught that atmospheric physics exerted an influence on the nervous system and on human health, and that the qualities of air and atmosphere should therefore be taken into account in medicine and in the prevention of disease. Armed with this knowledge, Péron made regular measurements and collected data from the thermometers, barometers, and hygrometers on board in order to study the relationship between meteorological conditions and the hygienic state of different parts of Le Géographe—the steerages, holds, powder magazines, and other enclosed spaces—compared with the open air. He concluded that the air within the vessel was unhealthy, owing to excessive heat, humidity, and the presence of “hydrogen sulphide,” and he further believed that washing the hold with seawater contributed significantly to the development of scurvy by creating a “cold and humid atmosphere.” In fact, scurvy results from a deficiency of vitamin C and is not caused by atmospheric conditions. Hallé praised Péron’s research and encouraged him to publish it in La médecine éclairée par les sciences physiques. He also supported Péron’s admission to the Society of the Paris School of Medicine.

During Le Géographe’s stop at the Cape of Good Hope, Péron witnessed the early stages of vaccination there. On his return to France, he prepared an account of these developments for the vaccine committee recently established by the Minister of the Interior, Jean-Antoine Chaptal (Comité central de vaccine établi par S. Exc. le ministre de l’Intérieur). Created in 1804, this body replaced the medical committee of the Society of Subscribers for Vaccine Inoculation (Société des souscripteurs pour l’inoculation de la vaccine), founded in 1800. Hallé and Corvisart, together with La Rochefoucauld-Liancourt, were among its principal members and oversaw the French campaign of vaccination against smallpox. Alongside the other members of the committee, they helped establish a national network linking governmental and departmental authorities in charge of vaccination, coordinating centres of inoculation, regulations, forms, orders, and circulars. In this sense, Cape Hallé and Corvisart Bay honoured two physicians to whom Péron supplied valuable observations drawn from the voyage, observations that intersected directly with their own work on hygiene and vaccination.
