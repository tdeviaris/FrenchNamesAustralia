Péron named Bougainville Bay “in honour of the venerable dean of French navigators.” Louis-Antoine de Bougainville (1729-1811) was celebrated as the first French navigator to have completed a circumnavigation of the globe. After scientific studies pursued under the direction of d’Alembert, followed by a literary education, he began his career as an army officer and took part in the defence of Canada between 1756 and 1760. With the return of peace, he entered the navy, was appointed captain in 1763, and attempted to establish a colony in the Falkland Islands, which he was ultimately obliged to abandon in the face of Spanish protests.

From 1766 to 1769 he carried out the first French circumnavigation officially undertaken as such. His expedition formed part of the broader movement of maritime and scientific discovery in the second half of the eighteenth century, in which the exploration of the Pacific and the search for the southern continent occupied a central place. The voyage also served political and commercial purposes in the aftermath of the Seven Years’ War, with a view to opening new markets and, if possible, new colonial footholds. Leaving Brest on 5 December 1766 aboard La Boudeuse, later joined by the store-ship L’Étoile, Bougainville passed through the Strait of Magellan, visited Tahiti, which he described with admiration in April 1768, and continued on to the New Hebrides. From there he sailed along the dangerous barrier reef off the north-eastern coast of present-day Australia, visited the northern coast of New Guinea and the Moluccas, called at the Isle de France, and, after rounding the Cape of Good Hope, completed his voyage at Saint-Malo on 16 March 1769. The expedition’s botanist, Philibert Commerson, named the species Bougainvillea spectabilis in his honour.

The publication of the Voyage autour du monde in 1771 was an immense success and helped revive European interest in the figure of the “noble savage” and in the state of nature, themes that continued to inform Péron’s earliest reflections at the departure of the Baudin expedition, before giving way, after the return, to more strongly hierarchised and racialised conceptions of human societies.

After his circumnavigation, Bougainville took part in the American War of Independence under the command first of the comte d’Estaing and later of the comte de Grasse, between 1778 and 1782. Under the Revolution, he was promoted vice-admiral, but resigned after refusing in 1792 the post of Minister of the Navy, at a time when discipline among the crews of the fleet was breaking down. Arrested during the Terror, he was released after the fall of Robespierre.

Napoléon bestowed the highest honours upon him: he was made senator in 1799, Grand Officer of the Légion d’honneur in 1804, and Count of the Empire in 1808. His last major official function was to preside, in 1809, over the court of inquiry charged with judging those held responsible for the disaster of Trafalgar. He was also associated with the preparation of the Egyptian expedition and, even more directly, with that of the Baudin expedition to Australia, in which his son Hyacinthe de Bougainville took part as a midshipman.

His son, Hyacinthe Yves Philippe Potentien de Bougainville, also went on to pursue a distinguished naval career. After the apprenticeship he received within the Baudin expedition, he led, from 1824 to 1826, a circumnavigation aboard the frigate La Thétis and the corvette L’Espérance. During his stop at Port Jackson, he expressed admiration for the rapid development of the British colony. In the narrative of this later voyage, he suggested that the French exploration of the eastern coasts of Tasmania in 1802, combined with the fear of being anticipated, may have helped to prompt the British to take possession of them.
