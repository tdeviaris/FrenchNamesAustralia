Cap Poivre - Cape Poivre

Pierre Poivre (1719-1786) was initially a missionary. During one of his voyages to Asia, the French East India Company vessel on which he was travelling was attacked by the British; a cannonball took off his right hand, and his forearm had to be amputated. No longer able to give the blessing, he was forced to abandon the priesthood, as well as drawing, for which he had shown considerable talent. This accident changed the course of his life.

Poivre later became intendant of the Mascarenes, the French possessions in the Indian Ocean, which included Île de France and Île Bonaparte, now La Réunion. Deeply interested in botany, he asked the naturalist Philibert Commerson to study the natural history of the islands' plants. He also taught botany to his nephew, Pierre Sonnerat, who became Commerson's assistant.

In the royal gardens of the French Indian Ocean islands, Poivre promoted the difficult processes of transplanting, acclimatising and propagating spices, especially clove and nutmeg trees from the Moluccas. These experiments had considerable economic and political significance: they aimed to break the Dutch monopoly on spices and to make the Mascarenes a centre of botanical acclimatisation in the service of the French empire. Poivre also became convinced of the economic uselessness of slavery and denounced the immorality of that condition.
