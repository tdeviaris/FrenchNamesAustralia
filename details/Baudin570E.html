Île Laplace – Laplace Island

At the turn of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, the work of Pierre-Simon Laplace played a major role in improving maritime navigation and cartography. A French astronomer, mathematician, and physicist, Laplace worked in the intellectual wake of Newton, but also of scholars such as Leonhard Euler, Alexis Clairaut, and Jean le Rond d’Alembert, whose names also appear in the nomenclature of the voyage. Building on their achievements, he developed a more complete and more precise account of planetary motion.

In his major work, the Mécanique céleste, published in several volumes, Laplace refined the laws governing the movements of celestial bodies. He also extended the work of Joseph-Louis Lagrange, especially in relation to the satellites of Jupiter and the rings of Saturn. These studies greatly improved the accuracy of astronomical prediction.

Such advances had very practical applications for navigation. Mariners could determine latitude with relative confidence, but longitude—that is, east-west position—remained far more difficult to establish. To do so, they used the sky as a natural clock. The Moon, whose position changes rapidly in relation to the stars, provided an essential point of reference.

In practice, the navigator measured, with a sextant or with Borda’s repeating circle, the distance between the Moon and a star or the Sun. He then compared this observation with astronomical tables indicating the precise moment at which that configuration occurred for a reference meridian. These tables, made much more reliable through Laplace’s work, made it possible to establish a reference time. By comparing that time with local time, estimated from the Sun, the navigator could calculate longitude, each hour of difference corresponding to roughly fifteen degrees on the globe.

Laplace’s researches notably enabled the astronomer Jean-Baptiste Joseph Delambre to improve, with great precision, the tables of the Connaissance des temps, published by the Bureau des longitudes. These tables were used by the officers, geographers, and astronomers attached to the great maritime expeditions. During the expedition led by Nicolas Baudin in the early nineteenth century, they thus helped determine the route followed with greater accuracy and contributed to the charting of still imperfectly known stretches of the Australian coast.

Thanks to these advances, the determination of a ship’s position became more reliable, improving both safety at sea and the quality of nautical charts. Even though the development of marine chronometers eventually simplified such calculations, Laplace’s theoretical achievements remained fundamental. They transformed observation of the heavens into a precise scientific instrument, enabling navigators to orient themselves more securely and to contribute to a more accurate mapping of the world.
