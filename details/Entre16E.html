The Huon River is the fifth largest in Tasmania. It flows through the fertile Huon Valley before entering the d’Entrecasteaux Channel. The tiny and sparsely populated Huon Island is mainly covered by grasses and bracken with some scattered trees in its northwestern corner. In keeping with post-revolutionary practice, Labillardière* in his narrative of the voyage deleted the ’de’ from Huon de Kermadec’s name and, more generally, referred to him as ‘Captain Huon’*.

Jean-Michel Huon de Kermadec (1748-1793) was the second in command of the d’Entrecasteaux* expedition and the captain of the Espérance . He was born near the city of Brest, located at the northwestern tip of France, in Britany, where his family had a manor. Huon was an experienced officer and a close friend of d’Entrecasteaux, with whom he had sailed on several occasions, including as his second in command in La Résolution on a voyage to China (see the entry Canal d’Entrecasteaux ). D’Entrecasteaux had made a special request for Huon to accompany him on the voyage in search of Lapérouse* and rewarded his services by naming both the Huon River and Huon Island, located at the river’s entrance, after him. Huon died from tuberculosis during the voyage, in May 1793, in New Caledonia, two months before d’Entrecasteaux.


Further information: In Tasmania, Huon de Kermadec’s name was given to several localities, geographic features, two ships and an establishment. On Tasmanian maps we now find Huonville , Port Huon, the Huon Valley , the village of Kermandie and the Kermandie River, and in northeastern Victoria there is the small settlement of Huon. Two ships and a store base of the Royal Australian Navy were named HMAS Huon. Several plants also bear his name, including the Huon Pine (Lagarostrobos franklinii) of Tasmania, which can live for more than 2,000 years.
