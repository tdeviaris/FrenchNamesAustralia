Île Lamarck - Lamarck Island

Lamarck made fossils one of the foundations of his thinking on the transformations of the globe and of living beings. By describing and classifying fossil invertebrates in continuity with living species, he founded invertebrate palaeontology and gave the word “fossil” a precise meaning. Through the notion of the “analogous fossil”, he showed that certain fossils could be related to living species and interpreted as evidence of gradual transformation under the effects of time, environment, and climate.

Péron seems to have been directly receptive to this way of reasoning. On Bernier Island, he described a shelly calcareous sandstone containing shells that he assigned to Lamarck’s genus Natice, and he explicitly emphasised their affinity with species still living at the foot of the cliffs. Found more than 50 metres above the present sea level, these fossil shells appeared to him as material proof of ancient changes in the Earth’s surface. Fossils thus became, both in Lamarck’s work and in Péron’s gaze, major indicators of a slow, continuous, and transformative history of the Earth and of life.
