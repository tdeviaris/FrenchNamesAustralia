Charles Pierre Boullanger (1772–1813), whose name is sometimes spelled Boulanger in Baudin’s journal, was the chief engineer-geographer of the Baudin expedition aboard the Géographe. His career illustrates the new importance given, in the late eighteenth century, to mathematical, hydrographical and cartographical knowledge in voyages of discovery.

Before joining the expedition, Boullanger had received advanced scientific training at the École Polytechnique, an institution recently created in the context of the reorganisation of scientific knowledge after the French Revolution. He attended lectures by renowned scholars such as Gaspard Monge, Jean-Antoine Chaptal, Joseph-Louis Lagrange, Gaspard de Prony and the chemist Claude-Louis Berthollet — names that would later be incorporated into the geographical nomenclature established by Péron and Freycinet. At the end of this training, Boullanger specialised in the corps of engineer-geographers, whose purpose was to train specialists capable of surveying, triangulating and constructing precise maps.

Boullanger also had some earlier maritime experience. In 1792, he had served as a naval officer cadet in Saint-Domingue, a French colony then engulfed in civil war. He nevertheless abandoned this first naval career after passing the entrance examination to the École Polytechnique. This dual profile — experience at sea combined with advanced scientific training — partly explains his selection for the Baudin expedition. His participation was recommended by one of his teachers, Gaspard de Prony, an important figure in the training of engineer-geographers and director of the applied school established for them.

The mission entrusted to Boullanger and his colleague Pierre Faure was to contribute to the reconnaissance of the still poorly known or unknown coasts of New Holland and Van Diemen’s Land. The expedition’s instructions emphasised the need to determine precisely the geographical position of the principal points along the coast and to draw accurate charts. In this context, Boullanger took part in several major hydrographical operations: the survey of Maria Island on the eastern coast of Van Diemen’s Land; the reconnaissance of that same eastern coast from Cape Tourville; the work carried out between Wilsons Promontory and Western Port; and, aboard the schooner Casuarina commanded by Louis Freycinet, the survey of the Hunter Islands and of the two gulfs of present-day South Australia, Spencer Gulf and Gulf St Vincent.

Boullanger’s method formed part of the renewal of French hydrography associated with Charles-François Beautemps-Beaupré. Like him, Boullanger combined bearings taken with the reflecting circle, astronomical observations, azimuths, soundings and rapid coastal sketches. The principal features of the coastline were fixed trigonometrically, associated with coastal profiles and transferred onto preliminary sketches. These documents were then used to construct more finished charts, linking observations taken on board to the visible forms of the landscape: capes, islands, isthmuses, cliffs, coves, sandbanks, reefs and anchorages.

His work bears witness to a form of field geography that was both technical and descriptive. Boullanger sought to translate into cartographic data landscapes often unknown to Europeans, whose coastal forms and vegetation were not always easy to identify. His registers and sketches thus combined angle measurements, positional calculations, nautical observations and descriptions of the coastal environment. The difficulties were numerous: bad weather, rough seas, agitation of the compass needle, irregularities in the marine chronometers, inaccuracies in the ephemerides, limited material resources and the time constraints imposed by navigation.

Baudin recognised the importance of the geographers’ work, although his relations with Boullanger were sometimes tense. In his journal, he also noted that Boullanger was very short-sighted, to the point, he wrote, that he had to take his bearings and angles “with his nose on the ground”.

After the expedition, Boullanger was assigned to the Dépôt des cartes et plans de la Marine, where he took part in processing the data gathered during the voyage. He worked in particular on verifying observations, comparing longitudes and reducing the errors caused by marine chronometers. The publication of the atlas, however, was ultimately dominated by Louis Freycinet, a naval officer and former commander of the Casuarina, who became the principal editor of the “Navigation and Geography” section of the Voyage de découvertes aux Terres australes.
