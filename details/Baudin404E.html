Cap Mentelle - Cape Mentelle

In 1803, Bonaparte asked Mentelle to make him a terrestrial globe. Globes were then important symbols of political power and domination, and Mentelle had already made a globe for the Dauphin, the eldest son of Louis XVI. Its external face represented the Earth, while its internal face showed the celestial vault. A sphere placed inside the globe displayed the imagined depths of the sea.

The emperor received this globe on 15 March 1811. It is 1.37 metres high and 1.56 metres wide. It consists of two cardboard hemispheres strengthened with metal plates. The cartographic drawing was executed by hand by the engineer-geographer Jean-Baptiste Poirson. Napoleon had it installed in the Galerie de Diane in the Tuileries palace, a few steps from his Grand Cabinet.

It is very likely that Mentelle asked Freycinet to provide the most up-to-date information on the charting of the coasts of the Southern Lands, so that they could be represented as completely as possible on his globe. It notably included the stretch of coast between Cape Hamelin and Cape Naturaliste, mapped for the first time by the Baudin expedition.

Reference

Mentelle, E., and Malte-Brun. Géographie mathématique, physique et politique de toutes les parties du monde. Paris, 1803.
