Thanks to a scholarship that her parents acquired for her, Elisa was educated before the French Revolution in the prestigious boarding school, La Maison Royale de Saint-Cyr, near Versailles, reserved for the daughters of noble families. The institution closed during the French Revolution in 1792. Then, in 1795, Mme Campan (see her entry in the glossary) opened a new boarding school to educate upper-class girls in Saint Germain en Laye, where Napoléon’s youngest sisters were instructed. Her mother married Elisa to a noble man from Corsica, Pascal-Felix Baciocchi, in 1797. With the ascension of Napoléon, Elisa played an important role in advancing the standing of the Napoleonic court, as she was knowledgeable and very interested in literature, comedy, and the arts. From 1805, Napoléon awarded her successive imperial titles and entrusted her with managerial responsibilities in Piombino, Lucca and, eventually, with the entire duchy of Tuscany. These, at the time, these territories were French departments and were quite close to the islands of Corsica and Elba. She governed Piombino, Lucca and, later, the duchy of Tuscany with dexterity, but her attempt to modernize the Tuscan cities through a proactive political programme and restore their glorious artistic past was not warmly welcomed in Italy, as it was seen as a French intrusion. Her executive decisions remained under the control and supervision of Napoléon.
