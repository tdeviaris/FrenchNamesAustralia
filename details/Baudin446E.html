Translations

The following translations are based on the original French transcriptions, whose spelling, abbreviations and punctuation have been preserved in the source document.

Hamelin’s mission order

“Citizen Saint-Cricq is ordered to embark in the large boat, supplied with five meals, and, leaving directly from the ship, to proceed to the southernmost of the Dorre Islands; to take soundings between the ship and that island, in order to verify the position of a reef which I believe to exist there; and to examine the southern coast and part of the south-eastern coast of the said island, to determine whether the corvette Le Géographe may have left there any evidence of her passage through Shark Bay.

He shall examine whether, along the stretch of coast he is to survey and on the neighbouring shores within his view, there is a suitable beach for landing, pitching a tent, and careening or refitting the boat.

He shall take note of the tide, the currents, and the species of birds and fish that chance may bring to his observation.

He shall place, in a conspicuous spot to the south or south-south-east of the island, a national flag and the bottle entrusted to him.

He shall make every effort to return on board this evening.

At anchor off Dirk Hartog, this 29th Messidor, Year IX.”

Saint-Cricq’s mission report

“Citizen,

In accordance with the order you gave me [word crossed out], I left the ship this morning at half past nine and steered North 2° West corrected, towards the middle of Dorre Island. In accordance with my instructions, I took soundings every half-hour, and during the first league and a half the depth remained constantly between 14 and 17 fathoms. But at that distance I found the sea rougher than it had seemed to be nearer the ship, and I rightly judged that the boat was over a shoal. When the lead was cast, it indeed brought back only 8 fathoms of water, with a bottom of sand and fine coral, and soon afterwards I had no more than three and a half fathoms beneath the boat.

I then had the impression that this bank was not of great extent from north to south, which I could moreover confirm when I resumed my course — unless I was mistaken, for about one mile north of the place where I had found eight fathoms of water, the depth was already greater than that. I ran west and then east without being able to find the end of the bank, after having made ten miles on each of these points of sail, and the depth was never less than 3¼ fathoms.

I arrived at Dorre Island at half past two and landed a little to the south-west of the point lying west-south-west of the island. I immediately went to set up the flagstaff on a rock which I found more or less on the summit of the highest place in that part of the island, and at the foot of this rock I deposited the sealed bottle containing the paper with which you had entrusted me for that purpose.

You had wished me to coast, either by land or by sea, part of the south-eastern coast, in order to ascertain whether there was a place suitable for landing and, above all, whether the boat that requires repair could easily be hauled up there. I did not, in this respect, fulfil the order you gave me, and two reasons prevented me from doing so: the first was the certainty I had from the very moment of my approach to the island that it is bordered by a layer of rocks and corals, making access almost impossible at every point; the second arose from the desire you had expressed to see me return on board this evening. I could not have satisfied you on both points, since it was already four o’clock when I had finished raising the flag. I therefore thought it necessary to make my way back to the ship, which was 24 miles away. I dare hope that, in this matter, you will approve my decision.

Dorre Island is simply a rock, from which I brought back a piece for the mineralogist on board, so that he might draw his conclusions from it. No plant, tree or shrub of any kind is to be found there, except for a few wild grasses growing in the cracks of the rocks. I saw no trace of animals, and even seabirds do not appear to visit this place, which is as sterile as it is wild. I saw only birds of prey, which I judged to be vultures.

A great quantity of green oysters and limpets surround the rocks bordering the sea. I saw a few lobsters there and found living giant clams.

The sea appeared to me to have risen 15 to 18 feet above its level at 5:15 in the evening, and from 3:45 until that hour it had fallen by three feet. These data are doubtless very limited from which to obtain results, but I ask you to observe that I remained on the island for only two and a half hours.

The south-eastern coast is arid, and the north-western coast, of which I saw only a small part, appeared to me to be the same. What is quite certain is that, at pistol-shot distance offshore from the place where I landed, the depth was greater than 10 fathoms.

In the prolongation of the west-south-west point of the island, I saw a chain of reefs that may extend 100 toises offshore.

On board, this 29th Messidor in the evening.

St Cricq.

I do not think I need tell you — I would have begun with that had it been otherwise — that I saw no sign of the corvette Le Géographe having passed through Shark Bay.”

Summary

Saint-Cricq was sent by Hamelin to reconnoitre the southernmost of the Dorre Islands. His mission was to verify the existence of a reef, look for possible traces of the passage of Le Géographe, assess landing conditions, and observe the tides, currents and species encountered. Although he was not a naturalist, his report provides a valuable description of the island’s natural history and apparent geological character. He describes it as “simply a rock”, brings back a specimen for the ship’s mineralogist, and depicts a coast that was difficult to approach, being ringed with rocks, corals and reefs.

His perspective remains that of a naval officer: his observations of nature are closely tied to navigation, anchorage, landing and the possible repair of the boat. Yet he records with notable precision the island’s extreme aridity, the absence of trees, shrubs and almost all vegetation, except for a few wild grasses growing in the cracks of the rocks. He also notes the absence of any traces of land animals and the unusually deserted character of the place, which even seabirds seem to avoid. The only abundant forms of life are found along the shore: green oysters, limpets, lobsters and living giant clams. Through this short but concrete report, Saint-Cricq presents Dorre Island as a rocky, coral-fringed, sterile and wild island, whose scientific interest emerges directly from a hydrographic and nautical mission.

Dorre Island was named in 1616 after Peter Dorre, pilot of the Dutch vessel Eendracht, commanded by Dirk Hartog. This island and Bernier Island were known to the French as the “Sterile Islands”.

Today, the names Cape Boullanger and Cape Couture designate, respectively, the northern point of Dorre Island and the southern point of Bernier Island. They recall the survey of these coasts carried out on 8 July 1801 by the geographer Charles-Pierre Boullanger, accompanied by midshipman Victor Couture. These toponyms, however, were not retained by Freycinet in his atlas, nor do they appear in the nomenclature published by Péron and Freycinet.
