Baie Pascal – Wallaroo Bay

Île Pascal – Pascal Island

Blaise Pascal (1623–1662) was one of the great scientific and literary figures of the seventeenth century, later described by Chateaubriand as “a great genius.” He is now regarded as one of the fathers of modern science. Pascal helped shape a new scientific method grounded in precise experimentation and in the use of mathematics to understand physical phenomena, thereby distinguishing scientific inquiry from religious belief. His mathematical work included major contributions to geometry and to probability theory. He also designed the Pascaline, one of the earliest forms of mechanical calculator. He showed that the old doctrine according to which “nature abhors a vacuum” was mistaken, thereby establishing the existence of the vacuum. He also confirmed Torricelli’s theory that atmospheric pressure decreases with altitude, because the mass of air is less at higher elevations than at lower levels.

Pascal is also known as a Christian thinker of remarkable depth. After his early death, his papers were left in disorderly bundles, although each sheet corresponded to a distinct spiritual reflection. The eighteenth-century philosopher Condorcet*, who was honoured with a place name near Pascal Bay, arranged them for a posthumous edition published in 1776, preceded by a eulogy to Pascal.

In 1778, Voltaire, who was also honoured with two place names, published a new edition of Condorcet’s version of Pascal’s Provinciales — a work defending Jansenist beliefs, according to which human beings, left to themselves, are incapable of doing good without divine grace and must refuse to participate in the iniquity of the world — as well as the Pensées, which explore Pascal’s faith. Voltaire added his own commentary and critique to Pascal’s reflections on God and the human condition.
