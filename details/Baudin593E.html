First image

This illustration, taken from Saint-Lambert’s poem Les Saisons, represents autumn. The presence of the tomb reminds the two lovers that all things come to an end.

Second image

This illustration illustrates the virtues of work, courage, and family solidarity, presented as righteous, redemptive, and life-saving qualities. These virtues allow a family whose home and possessions have been destroyed by war to survive and rebuild their lives. Forced to flee, they manage to support themselves by making and selling baskets, a craft the father had learned in his youth.

Third image

Rulhière’s work is an account of the intrigues and manoeuvres that enabled Catherine II to remove her husband, Emperor Peter III of Russia, and seize power. The first two pages of the edition explain the circulation of the manuscript, the attempts made to suppress it, and Rulhière’s resistance to the pressure exerted upon him.

Here is the translation of the first two pages:

EDITOR’S NOTE;

Mr Rulhière was in St Petersburg at the time of the events that placed Catherine II on the throne of Russia; he wrote their history at the request of Countess d’Egmont, daughter of Marshal de Richelieu, whom he had accompanied during his governorship in the capacity of gentleman-in-waiting. Soon after his return to Paris, his manuscript, which he had read aloud in several circles, acquired a certain reputation. People were still speaking of the events that had taken place in Russia, and everyone, as was customary, recounted them in his own way. The court, eager to know in detail and in truth the intrigues that had placed Catherine on the throne of her husband, also wished to hear it read.

The empress was not long in learning of it; she ordered her agents in Paris to use every possible means to make this work disappear. They made the author fairly considerable financial offers, which he refused. Unable to succeed, they wished to use authority. M. le duc d’Aiguillon, then minister, was approached. M. de Sartines, lieutenant-general of police, summoned M. Rulhière to his house; he informed him that he had orders to demand his manuscript and sought to intimidate him with the threat of the Bastille. But he, who had resisted the methods of corruption, was not intimidated by such threats. He replied to the lieutenant of police that they might put him in the Bastille, even tear the manuscript from him, but that it would be a useless punishment, because he carried it written in his memory.
