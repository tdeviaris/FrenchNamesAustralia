In July 1791, in Paris, the Societé d’Histoire Naturelle (the most prominent scientific institution at the time) selected these three experts to join the d’Entrecasteaux expedition as naturalists. That Labillardière was an active member of this society helped in their selection. But their choice was mainly a result of his aptitude, as Labillardière was a highly trained naturalist. Labillardière belonged to the little bourgeoisie of Alençon (Normandie), the Houtou, who own a family estate named La Billardière. After completing a degree in medicine, Labillardière opted to take up a career in the natural sciences. He spent almost two years in Britain studying plants in cultivation and established a firm friendship with Sir Joseph Banks. In later years he visited Mediterranean countries and the Near East to collect plants. When reporting on his excursions, Labillardière expressed his ‘admiration at the sight of these ancient forests, in which the sound of the axe has never been heard … contemplating the prodigious size of these trees … the most luxuriant vigour of vegetation…’. He ‘discovered … a new [to Europeans] species of parsley, which I [Labillardière] named apium prostratum, on account of the position of its stem which always creeps along the ground. Its analogy with the other species of the same genus let me to think it might be good to eat, and it answered my expectations. We carried a large quantity of it on board with us, which was acceptable to mariners who felt the necessity to reduce, by vegetable diet, the bad effects of the salt provisions on which we had lived during the whole of our passage from the Cape of Good Hope to that of Van Diemen’. He also found a species of Eucalypt that he named Eucalyptus globulus (the Tasmanian blue gum) that the carpenters employed for their repairs on the vessels. Labillardière commented that this eucalypt ‘is one of the tallest in nature … the trunk, which is very straight, … might be usefully employed in shipbuilding, especially for masts, although it is neither so light nor so elastic as that of the pine tree. Possibly it might be of advantage to construct masts of different pieces of timber and even perforate the large trunks of trees throughout their whole length, so far to render them lighter, and to give them strength by binding them at equal distances with hoops of iron…’.
