Île Thévenard - Thevenard Island

Cap Thévenard - Cape Thevenard

Antoine-Jean-Marie Thévenard (1733-1815) was one of the major figures of the French navy in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries. During the Revolution, he served as Minister of the Navy and Colonies. On Fleurieu’s advice, he selected Beautemps-Beaupré as ingénieur-géographe for the d’Entrecasteaux expedition.

Under the Consulate and the Empire, his seniority earned him the affectionate nickname “the Ancestor”: he was then the eldest of the French vice-admirals. Thévenard was maritime prefect at Lorient when Le Géographe returned there from the Southern Lands in March 1804. Péron records that “Vice-Admiral Thévenard, the maritime prefect, and Captain Molini, the military commander, welcomed us with extreme kindness and treated us with the most obliging attention.”

Thévenard had a keen interest in the nautical sciences. A member of the Académie de marine, a corresponding member of the Académie royale des sciences, and later a corresponding member of the Institut national in the section of mechanics, he published the Mémoires relatifs à la marine, an important four-volume work devoted to navigational instruments and methods, shipbuilding, hydrography, and many other aspects of maritime science.

Some chapters of the Mémoires also have a historical, geographical, and political significance. This is notably the case with the chapter entitled “On the names of various places on the coasts of Newfoundland and Labrador disfigured by the English in their latest maps, copied and published in France with this transmutation of foreign names.” In this text, Thévenard denounces the alterations made to French place names on English maps and in the French copies derived from them. He argues that place names are essential historical traces: to transform or replace them is to obscure the memory of discovery, erase French precedence, and weaken France’s historical claims to these coasts.

This chapter resonates strongly with Fleurieu’s 1790 memoir, Découvertes des François en 1768 & 1769, dans le sud-est de la Nouvelle-Guinée, et reconnaissances postérieures des mêmes terres par des navigateurs anglois qui leur ont imposé de nouveaux noms. In both works, toponymy is more than a matter of geography: it becomes an issue of memory, scientific priority, and symbolic sovereignty.
