This engraving, chosen as the frontispiece to the second volume of the Voyage de découvertes aux Terres australes, completed by Louis de Freycinet and published in 1816 after Péron’s death, is known in at least two closely related states. At first glance, they appear almost identical. In both, Péron is shown seated in his study, surrounded by the attributes of scholarly activity: jars containing preserved fish, measuring instruments including a thermometer, an unfurled map of New Holland, and his own body, warmly clothed and visibly marked by illness. Everything in the composition places the naturalist within the material world of investigation, collection, and scientific writing. Yet beneath this strong visual continuity, the two portraits differ in the books and papers arranged around him, and it is there that the essential distinction lies.

In one of the portraits, Péron is shown reading the manuscript of one of his memoirs. The text cannot be identified with certainty, but it refers either to one of his political writings or, more probably, to one of his scientific works. The books surrounding him construct the image of a scholar of wide-ranging interests: the two volumes of the voyage narrative are set upright beside a History of Peoples, recalling his anthropological concerns; one also recognises the History of Medusae as well as a volume devoted to physics and meteorology. The whole therefore emphasizes the diversity of his research and the breadth of the fields in which he was engaged.

In the other portrait, the general scenography remains unchanged, but the papers and books have been rearranged in a way that alters the meaning of the image. Péron is no longer reading a memoir: he is correcting the manuscript pages of the second volume of the official account of the expedition. The first volume, already published, lies before him, accompanied by drafts of Chapter III and other still unfinished sections. The History of Medusae is still present, but it is now incorporated into an ensemble dominated by the work of composing the Voyage itself. Three volumes of manuscript notes standing on the table further reinforce this impression of accumulated labour. Here, it is no longer primarily the variety of Péron’s knowledge that is foregrounded, but rather his role as editor and organiser of the expedition’s results.

Thus, although the two portraits rely on the same scholarly scenography, they do not construct quite the same image of Péron: one places greater emphasis on the plurality of his intellectual interests, while the other shows him more explicitly absorbed in the completion of the Voyage, as though the image sought less to celebrate the fertile dispersion of his research than to fix, within the ordered space of the study, the written memory of the expedition.

François Péron (1775–1810) owed his admission to the scientific staff of the Baudin expedition to a combination of medical training, useful connections, and circumstance. A student of medicine in Paris, he had attended lectures in zoology and anatomy at the Muséum national d’Histoire naturelle, notably those of scholars such as Cuvier and Lamarck, whose names also recur in this nomenclature. His first bid for inclusion in the voyage took the form of an ambitious anthropological programme. In a memoir submitted to Cuvier in June 1800 and later presented to the Institut, Péron argued for the importance of studying human societies within the broader field of natural history, with particular attention to the physical and moral causes of the health and longevity he attributed to Indigenous peoples. The proposal was initially rejected by the scientific commission; yet Cuvier, who saw in his student’s work a possible contribution to comparative anatomy, intervened in his favour. Péron was therefore admitted to the expedition, not in the capacity of physician-anthropologist that he had envisaged, but as an assistant zoologist attached to Cuvier and charged with collecting specimens for the Museum. He thus embarked in 1800 as a supernumerary member of the scientific staff, filling one of the vacancies left by those who had declined the voyage.

During the course of the expedition, Péron became its most significant scientist and, for Baudin, one of its most exasperating personalities. Among his major achievements was his work on marine invertebrates, especially jellyfish. As Jacqueline Goy has shown, this research was pioneering. Péron treated these organisms as a coherent zoological group and named several of them with reference to figures from Greek antiquity. A striking example is his designation of Chrysaora pentastoma on Île Decrès: the name alludes to Chrysaor, born from the decapitated Medusa in Greek mythology, an image that Péron associated with the golden colour of the animal’s tentacles, while penta refers to its five-part anatomical structure.

Péron himself, not without a degree of vanity, claimed that no one had collected more animals from the southern hemisphere than he had. He is said to have brought back some 2,500 species previously unknown to European naturalists. His method was notably rigorous. For each specimen, he recorded in Latin the date and place of capture, together with observations on form, colour, distinguishing features, and environmental conditions. Each specimen was also assigned a unique identification number, copied onto the jar or crate in which it was stored. Much of this information was unfortunately lost after the expedition’s return, when Geoffroy Saint-Hilaire reportedly removed specimens from their numbered containers, thereby separating them from the labels and notes that gave them their scientific context.
