Île Jussieu – Jussieu Island

Baie Jussieu – Jussieu Bay

In his Genera plantarum secundum ordines naturales disposita (1789), Antoine-Laurent de Jussieu devised a system of classification based on plant morphology. This system became the precursor of the principal classificatory methods used until the end of the twentieth century, when the search for common ancestry through phylogeny and cladistics, based on molecular biology and genetic sequencing, became the dominant method of classification.

Jussieu’s system relied on a range of characteristics, some regarded as more important than others according to their degree of variability within a species. Unlike Linnaeus’s sexual system, which depended primarily on the number of stamens, and unlike Tournefort’s method, considered inadequate for accounting for the diversity of foreign species, Jussieu’s approach involved weighing characters rather than merely counting them. He placed particular emphasis on cotyledons—acotyledons, monocotyledons, and dicotyledons—as well as on the ovary, rather than focusing solely on stamens.

Jussieu served as director and treasurer of the Muséum national d’Histoire naturelle and was a member of the commission of the Institut when he was charged with drafting the instructions for the Baudin expedition, alongside Fleurieu, Laplace, Bougainville, Lacépède, Lelièvre, Camus, and Langlès. During the voyage, Baudin wrote to Jussieu from the Isle de France, Timor, and Port Jackson in order to keep him informed of the progress of the expedition’s geographical and scientific work.

The expedition’s botanist, Leschenault, drew on both Tournefort’s and Jussieu’s systems of classification in describing the Australian flora. He wrote: “c’est ainsi que beaucoup de légumineuses sont à corolles papilionacées avec des étamines libres; que plusieurs sont à feuilles opposées, et qu’une grande quantité de mimosa offrent le singulier caractère d’avoir dans leur jeune âge des feuilles pennées, mélangées avec des feuilles simples…” (“Thus many legumes have papilionaceous corollas with free stamens; several have opposite leaves; and a large number of mimosas display the singular characteristic of having, in their juvenile stage, pinnate leaves mixed with simple leaves…”).
