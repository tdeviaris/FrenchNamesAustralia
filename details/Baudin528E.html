Îles Lacepede - Lacepede Islands

Baie Lacepede - Lacepede Bay

Détroit de Lacepede - Investigator Strait

Bernard Germain de Lacépède (1756-1825) is best known for continuing the monumental Histoire naturelle, générale et particulière initiated by Georges-Louis Leclerc de Buffon. Encouraged by Buffon, he first published volumes on oviparous quadrupeds and serpents and, following his appointment in 1795 as professor at the Muséum national d’Histoire naturelle, undertook the writing of further volumes on the natural history of fishes and cetaceans. In the same year, he was admitted to the Institut National des Sciences et des Arts.

Lacépède’s career illustrates the close interweaving of science, culture, and politics in the Napoleonic period. A talented musician in his youth - his operas were praised by Christoph Willibald Gluck - he entered politics in 1799. He served successively as President of the Senate and, from 1804, held ministerial functions under Napoléon Bonaparte, who also appointed him Grand Chancellor of the Legion of Honour and made him a count.

Lacépède played a direct role in the institutional framework that supported the Baudin expedition. He was a member of the commission that examined the proposal for a voyage of discovery to the southern lands submitted by Nicolas Baudin to the Institut. As an administrator of the Muséum, he subsequently supported the scientific exploitation of the expedition’s results, backing the editorial work of François Péron after the return of the Géographe and seeking governmental support to promote the collections and findings of the voyage.

Péron explicitly acknowledged Lacépède’s authority, referring to him as his “illustrious master” and a “celebrated naturalist”. He frequently relied on Lacépède’s classifications of fishes and cetaceans to describe newly observed species. Thus, off Terre de Witt, he identified a new species (Balistapodus wittensis), which he distinguished from related forms by the absence of ventral fins and regarded as the type of a new order within Lacépède’s ichthyological system. Although this interpretation was later revised - the French entomologist Étienne Frédéric Girard noting in 1856 that triggerfishes may have ventral fins that are either well developed or rudimentary, which explains why they went unnoticed by Péron and Lesueur - this episode nevertheless illustrates the direct influence of Lacépède’s theoretical frameworks on the interpretation of field observations. It thus highlights the close connections between classificatory knowledge and exploratory practices within the expedition.

On Kangaroo Island (then Île Decrès), Péron described two lizards “under the names of Tridactyle and Tétradactyle”: the former bears only three digits on each limb, while the latter has four - “a combination of digits previously unknown among reptiles, but whose existence my illustrious master M. de Lacépède had anticipated as possible, and even probable.”

Similarly, in Aiguade Bay (now Frenchman Bay), Péron reported having himself killed a large reptile (about 182 cm in length), which, “in the system of M. de Lacépède, ought to serve as the type of a new genus, close to that of the boa”, thereby illustrating the capacity of Lacépède’s system to accommodate and organise newly observed zoological forms.

More broadly, Péron adopted Lacépède’s comparative method, based on the analysis of major physiological relationships and the combinations of external organs.

This scientific and institutional influence is reflected in the nomenclature of the expedition: Péron attributed several place names to Lacépède, including Lacepede Bay and Investigator Strait in South Australia, as well as the Lacepede Islands.
