Lesueur gives the geographical coordinates of the place where the fish shown in this illustration was caught: 31°00’ S, 118°00’ E. The longitude is measured from the Paris meridian, which passes through the centre of the Paris Observatory and lies 2°20’13.82’’ east of the Greenwich meridian. These indications show that the fish was caught slightly south of the Baie des Tétrodons.

Freycinet notes the remarkable abundance of these fish in the area of Dirk Hartog Island and Dampier Reef:

“On the eastern coast of Dirk Hartog Island and to the east of Dampier Reef, an immense quantity of tetrodons is found. These fish, whose skin is of a beautiful silvery colour, are so voracious that they immediately seize upon the bait offered to them; accordingly, we always made an extremely abundant catch of them. The tetrodon usually remains at a short distance from the bottom; it must be caught with large hooks, to prevent it from cutting them with its very strong teeth.”

The toponym “Baie des Tétrodons” therefore refers to a precise zoological observation: the massive presence, in these shallow waters, of fish with powerful teeth and striking voracity, which the members of the expedition caught in abundance. Their behaviour also impressed the travellers. Péron thus reports that a tetrodon suddenly approached the artist Nicolas-Martin Petit, frightening him:

“One of the latter suddenly approached Mr Petit, who, in his fear, fired at the animal.”

The Baie des Tétrodons and the Îlot des Tétrodons are two rare examples of toponyms through which Péron and Freycinet directly inscribed the natural history of the coastal environment into the geographical nomenclature.
