Île Racine – Racine Island

Cap Racine – Two Hummock Point

Napoleon Bonaparte was familiar with Racine’s tragedies and appreciated them, although he seems to have preferred Corneille as a dramatist of political grandeur and statecraft. In 1799, he even advised the leading actor in Racine’s Britannicus on how to interpret the role of the Roman emperor Nero, whom Racine presents as an “emerging monster”.

This actor, François-Joseph Talma, was regarded as the greatest tragic actor of his time and was often said to bear a resemblance to Napoleon. Talma and Napoleon discussed Racine’s plays and theatrical interpretation, and they gradually became friends. Their relationship was both artistic and political: Talma gave dramatic form to the ancient and imperial figures who fascinated Napoleon, while Napoleon in turn advised him on the gestures, restraint and authority appropriate to rulers on stage.

In Britannicus, the subject is deeply political, but it was also potentially uncomfortable for Napoleon. The play depicts Nero at the moment when he begins to become a tyrant. With Talma, the role of Nero became increasingly central, and the political dimension of the play came to dominate its more pathetic or sentimental aspects. Yet Nero was hardly a flattering model for a future emperor. Britannicus could invite reflection on power, but also on its abuses and dangers. This explains why Bonaparte, at the time not yet emperor, was particularly interested in discussing the role with Talma.

Among Racine’s other tragedies seen by Napoleon were Esther, Iphigénie, Andromaque and Bajazet. Some of these productions featured the leading actress Marguerite-Joséphine Weimer, known as Mademoiselle George, who had been Napoleon’s mistress before his coronation in 1804.

Another celebrated actress, Mademoiselle Mars (1779-1847), who performed with the great Talma from 1802, was also highly esteemed by Napoleon. At this time, Mlle Mars was giving lessons in diction to Élisa Bonaparte; see her portrait in the entry Cap Gérard* in SA.
