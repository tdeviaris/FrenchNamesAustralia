Charles-Maurice de Talleyrand-Perigord (1754-1838) was one of the most singular and enduring figures in French diplomacy. A worldly bishop who became an actor of the Revolution, and an aristocrat who served successive regimes, he played a decisive role both in the rise of Napoleon and, later, in the political undoing of Napoleonic power, once imperial ambition and endless war seemed to threaten France's very survival.

Talleyrand Bay, named by the Baudin expedition on the southern coast of New Holland, thus recalls a major figure of power and diplomacy in the Napoleonic age. As Minister of Foreign Affairs under the Directory, the Consulate and the Empire, Talleyrand was involved in the negotiation of many treaties favourable to France. Yet his political art rested less on conquest than on equilibrium: when he came to believe that Napoleon's authority had become personal domination, and that permanent war would exhaust the country, he gradually distanced himself from the Emperor.

At Erfurt in 1808, Talleyrand is said to have remarked to Tsar Alexander I:

"The French people are civilized, their sovereign is not; the sovereign of Russia is civilized, his people are not; it is therefore for the sovereign of Russia to be the ally of the French people."

The phrase captures the way in which Talleyrand sought to distinguish the lasting interests of France from those of Napoleon himself. Another saying attributed to him expressed the same judgement: "Napoleon cannot last; he wears out everything, even glory."

When Napoleon learned of, or suspected, these manoeuvres, he publicly humiliated Talleyrand during the famous scene of 28 January 1809. The Emperor is said to have overwhelmed him with insults, even declaring:

"You are shit in a silk stocking."

Talleyrand, on leaving the room, is said to have replied simply:

"What a pity, gentlemen, that so great a man should be so badly brought up!"

Whether literal or shaped by memoir tradition, the remark expresses the contrast between imperial violence and the diplomat's cold irony.

After Napoleon's fall, Talleyrand once again played a central role at the Congress of Vienna in 1814-1815, presided over by Metternich, which sought to reorganize Europe after the upheavals of the French Revolution and the Napoleonic Wars. Through his diplomatic skill, defeated France avoided political marginalization and was reintegrated into the European concert. This ability to turn military defeat into diplomatic survival explains why Talleyrand is often regarded as one of the fathers of modern diplomacy.
