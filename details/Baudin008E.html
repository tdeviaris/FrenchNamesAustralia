A worldly bishop who in 1791, left his diocese and requested to be relieved of his duties, and an aristocrat-turned-revolutionary, he was instrumental in shaping—and later undoing—the power of Napoleon, when he realised Napoleon’s dictatorship and endless wars threatened France’s survival, and that “Napoleon cannot last; he wears out everything, even glory”, he started secret diplomacy with other European powers. When Napoléon, learned about this betrayal, he told Talleyrand on the 28th of January 1809, from his office in the castle of Saint Cloud: ‘You deserve that I break you like a glass … you are ordure in a silk stocking! ‘(‘ Vous mériteriez que je vous brisasse comme du verre ; j'en ai le pouvoir ; mais je vous méprise trop pour en prendre la peine ! Oh ! tenez, vous êtes de la merde dans un bas de soie ! ’ In his diplomatic work, Talleyrand was involved in negotiations that resulted in the drawing up of many treaties favorable to France. In 1814-15 he was the French chief negotiator at the Congress of Vienna, presided over by Metternich, which aimed to provide a long-term peace plan for Europe and to redraw the boundaries of states, following the upheavals of the French Revolution and the Napoleonic Wars. Talleyrand’s diplomatic skills helped to obtain a good outcome for France. He is often referred to as the ‘father of modern diplomacy’.
