Cap Missiessy - Cape Missiessy

Édouard Thomas de Burgues, lord, then comte de Missiessy, was a French naval officer promoted to the rank of rear admiral in 1793 and later vice-admiral under the Empire. Coming from a family with a long naval tradition, he belonged to that generation of officers trained under the Ancien Régime and later employed, not without difficulty, by the Republic and the Empire. During the Terror he was temporarily removed and obliged to leave France; reinstated afterwards, he continued a career in which technical and administrative functions played an important role.

Back in France, Missiessy was asked by Jean-Charles de Borda, a fellow naval officer and mathematician, to help introduce the metric system in ports and naval arsenals. He was also in charge of standardizing coastal signals to make communication at sea easier and coastal navigation safer.

From September 1804 to June 1805, when the British Navy blockaded the French colonies of Martinique and Guadeloupe in the West Indies, Missiessy was made commander of the Rochefort squadron with the mission to supply the French islands, disembark troops there, and, as far as possible, hinder British action in the region. The squadron carried out the operation successfully and returned to France without losing a ship. Yet this campaign formed part of a much broader strategic design: Napoleon intended to coordinate several squadrons in order to create a diversion in the Atlantic and prepare the conditions for an invasion of England. For security reasons, he did not reveal the whole plan to the commanders concerned. Thus, although Missiessy executed his mission quickly and effectively, the lack of clear strategic coordination prevented him from joining Villeneuve as originally intended.

By selecting Missiessy’s name, Péron and Freycinet were also paying tribute to a learned officer, author of an important treatise on the material organization of ships. Published in 1797, though prepared before the Revolution, Installation des vaisseaux takes as its model a 74-gun ship, then one of the most common types in the French navy. Missiessy proposed a methodical organization of spaces and equipment on board: distribution of materials, precise assignment of the different parts of the vessel, modifications to the yards, preparation for combat, circulation of men and objects, and precise duties for each crew member.

“In order to act methodically, we have established in the most advantageous way, in each place of the vessel, the fixed objects which are necessary for navigation (...) we then matched them, as best we could, with provisions favourable to combat and likely to speed up its preparation, and with the movement of a general manoeuvre.”

Missiessy also advocated in his treatise that iron be used in constructing the kitchen, in accordance with the model conceived by Kersaint.

On the attribution of the name “Missiessy”

The presence of the name Missiessy on two distinct capes — one on the western coast of New Holland, the other in Terre Napoléon — deserves attention. Admittedly, the two capes lay far apart, which greatly limited the risk of confusion for navigators. Even so, hydrographic practice in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries generally sought to avoid assigning the same name to several coastal features of the same kind, especially when they appeared within the same cartographic set.

This duplication should therefore not be interpreted solely as negligence or as a sign of poor control over nomenclature. It also suggests that, for Péron and Freycinet, Terre Napoléon could be conceived as a territorially distinct space from New Holland. In that sense, the existence of two separate geographical frameworks made the duplication of the same toponym possible, or at least less problematic.

This choice must be understood within the particular conditions under which the nomenclature of the Baudin expedition was elaborated, revised, and published after the commander’s death. It shows the room for manoeuvre that Péron and Freycinet possessed to take up, modify, or reinterpret a nomenclature initially established under Baudin’s authority. It also reflects the complex editorial context of the expedition’s return, marked by political change, personal ambitions, and tensions among the actors of the voyage.
