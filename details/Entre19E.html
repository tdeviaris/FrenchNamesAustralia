Île du satellite and L'Arche. Section of the map ‘Carte particulière du Canal Dentrecasteaux…’. By Beautemps-Beaupré, C.-F. In Atlas du voyage de Bruny-Dentrecasteaux, 1807. Op. cit., Plate 4 Source: National Library of Australia,

A surveying party led by lieutenant de Saint-Aignan reported that Satellite Island was surrounded by rocks making it difficult to land. Despite this, they eventually succeeded in getting ashore. They found the island to form an elevated plateau with vertical sides and noted that part of it was covered by trees so tightly intertwined to make it impenetrable. Having almost run out of drinking water, they searched for sources of fresh water on the island but did not find any.

Satellite island may have been named with reference to attempts by the ships’ officers to observe the eclipses ‘of the first satellite’ or moon of the planet Jupiter, in May 1792 and again during the nights of 15 and 16 February 1793. However, as the skies were then mainly overcast, they could observe only one eclipse. The dates and times when such eclipses could be observed were recorded in the ephemeris, a table listing the position of celestial objects at regular intervals of time. The time difference between the observation of an eclipse in Paris, as tabulated in the ephemeris, and in Tasmania, allowed navigators to calculate the longitude of their position on the map.
