Îles de l'Institut – Institut Islands

Members of the Institut, Lamarck and Guyton presented to the Class of Physical and Mathematical Sciences, during the session of 9 Vendémiaire Year XIII, the memoir that Péron had submitted to them on the researches he had carried out during the voyage concerning variations in the temperature of the sea. They approved both his experimental work and the conclusions he drew from it, and judged that “this memoir is worthy of the approval, and even the praise, of the class, and that it should be printed among those of foreign savants [that is, non-members of the Institut].”

Created in 1795, after the suppression of the former royal academies during the Revolution, the Institut national des sciences et des arts, generally referred to more simply as the Institut (see glossary), became, at the turn of the nineteenth century, the principal institution through which French scholarly, literary, and artistic elites were recognised and legitimated. Conceived as an encyclopaedic body uniting knowledge, it brought together the principal scientific, moral, literary, and artistic disciplines, and played an essential role in the evaluation of learned work, the drafting of scholarly instructions, and the public consecration of discoveries. Under the Consulate, it thus became one of the principal instruments through which the state linked science, exploration, national prestige, and public utility.

At once an institution of recognition, advice, and scholarly validation, the Institut conferred upon scientific work a public and symbolic authority. In the context of the Consulate and the early Napoleonic years, it helped to inscribe voyages of exploration, natural-historical enquiries, and cartographic undertakings within a learned culture closely bound to the prestige of the nation.

Napoléon Bonaparte himself appeared at the head of the list of members of the Class of Physical and Mathematical Sciences of the Institut, under the formula: “His Majesty the Emperor and King, appointed member of this class, section of mechanics, on 5 Nivôse, Year VI.” In 1803, Bonaparte reformed the Institut, notably by suppressing the Class of Moral and Political Sciences, where many of his opponents were found. Under the Empire, relations between the Quai Conti and the Emperor — who in 1804 renamed the Institut national the Institut impérial — were at times strained. Napoléon nevertheless attached great importance to this “parliament of learned men,” whose work was expected to contribute to the grandeur of the regime. He also entrusted the academies with investigations and reports.

In 1805, the Institut moved into the Collège des Quatre-Nations, on the Quai Conti, where it still sits today and where its members continue to meet.
