Îles de l'Institut – Institut Islands

Created in 1795, the Institut national des sciences et des arts (usually referred to simply as the Institut; see glossary) replaced the royal academies suppressed during the Revolution. Its members were organised into classes, themselves divided into sections. In 1805, the Institut moved into the Collège des Quatre-Nations on the Quai Conti, where it remains today and where its members still meet. When Péron referred to this “famous society of which our homeland is so proud,” he testified to the Institut’s central and authoritative role in the organisation of scientific and cultural knowledge in the service of the nation. It was the Institut that examined, sanctioned, and confirmed the scientific work of the expedition (see the glossary entry on the Institut national des sciences et des arts).

The principal French intellectual figures in literature, science, and the arts — whether members of the Institut or, before its foundation, of the former royal academies — were commemorated with place names within the group of the Institut Islands, in addition to other toponyms already assigned to them on other Australian coasts.

Under the Empire, Napoléon Bonaparte himself appeared at the head of the list of members of the Class of Physical and Mathematical Sciences of the Institut, under the formula: “His Majesty the Emperor and King, appointed member of this class, section of mechanics, on 5 Nivôse, Year VI.” In 1803, Bonaparte reformed the Institut, notably by suppressing the Class of Moral and Political Sciences, where many of his critics were gathered. During the Empire, relations between the Quai Conti and the Emperor—who renamed the Institut national the Institut impérial in 1804—were at times strained. Napoléon nevertheless attached great importance to this “parliament of learned men,” whose work was intended to contribute to the grandeur of the regime. He also regularly called upon the academies to undertake studies and prepare reports.
