Îles d'Arcole – D'Arcole Islands

Pic d’Arcole - Mount Nicholas

The place names concerned here are located in the Bonaparte Archipelago in Western Australia and on the north-eastern coast of Tasmania.

The name Arcole refers to the locality in northern Italy associated with Bonaparte’s first Italian campaign (1796–1797), during which he commanded the French revolutionary armies fighting the Austrian forces for control of Italy and access to Vienna.

The celebrated representations of Bonaparte at Arcole, brandishing a revolutionary flag under the smoke of battle and urging his troops to cross the bridge, contributed powerfully to the making of his image as an inspiring, audacious, and victorious commander. Circulated in print in France as well as in conquered territories, these images were, however, part of a heroic staging far removed from the actual course of the battle. On 15 November, Augereau advanced on Arcole without being able to cross the bridge; Bonaparte in turn attempted to draw his troops across it, but unsuccessfully. The fighting resumed on 16 and 17 November: Bonaparte then had a bridge built over the Alpone to allow Augereau’s divisions to outflank the Austrian forces, while Masséna, arriving from Ronco, finally succeeded in crossing the bridge at Arcole. It was only after three days of particularly deadly fighting that General Alvinzi’s Austrian troops retreated. Total losses, killed and wounded, are estimated at between 8,000 and 14,000 men, with the French losing around 4,000 to 6,000 soldiers and the Austrians between 5,000 and 8,000.

Thus, the name Arcole in the voyage’s nomenclature refers not merely to a military episode, but to an event that had already, by the beginning of the nineteenth century, been transformed into a site of heroic memory. Its inscription in coastal toponymy shows how the expedition also participated in the geographical dissemination of the Napoleonic narrative.
