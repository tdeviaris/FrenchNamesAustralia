The d’Entrecasteaux expedition surveyed the Archipelago of La Recherche between 9 and 17 December 1792, at a particularly difficult moment in their voyage. Despite it being summer, the crew faced relentless bad weather, heavy seas, and the constant threat of hidden reefs, which made progress slow and dangerous. To add to their hardship, the scarcity of fresh water placed the expedition under further strain.

The toponyms bestowed along this stretch of Western Australia reflect both the geographical challenges encountered and the traditions of maritime exploration. Many names describe the coast’s physical features, useful for navigation: Îles du Sud-Est, Cap Aride, Île du Milieu, Groupe de l’Est, Île du Mondrain (for its hummock), Groupe de l’Ouest, la Chaussée, Cap des Basses, and l’Avant-garde. Others carry a more personal resonance. Cap Le Grand and Île Remarque honoured a crew member who, in the midst of a violent storm, noticed / “remarked” a break in the surf—an observation that allowed l’Espérance, in desperate straits, to reach safe harbour. Île de l’Observatoire recalls the scientific work carried out there, while Archipel de la Recherche and Baie de l’Espérance commemorated the expedition’s two ships.

The expedition’s naturalist, Jacques Labillardière, made landfalls on Observatory Island between 13 and 15 December. On his first landing he was accompanied by the chaplain Ventenat, who gave the party a fright when he slipped into the sea—at a time when large sharks were in sight—but was rescued swiftly by the coxswain. During his explorations, Labillardière identified several new species of plants, remarkable for their resilience to the arid conditions of the archipelago’s coastline. Among them were Chorizema ilicifolium, Anigozanthos rufus, Banksia repens, and Banksia nivea. He also recorded observations of the local fauna, which included shooting Pacific gulls and a seal for study.
