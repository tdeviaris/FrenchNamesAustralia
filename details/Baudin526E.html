Île Berthollet - Carnac Island

Scientific exchanges between French and Dutch scholars long predated Louis-Napoléon's accession to the Dutch throne. In the late eighteenth century, for example, the Dutch chemist Martinus van Marum and the French chemist Claude Louis Berthollet maintained an active correspondence in which they discussed scientific problems, publications, experimental practices, and emerging theories in chemistry and physics.

These intellectual connections were also recognised by learned institutions. The Hollandsche Maatschappij der Wetenschappen elected Berthollet as a foreign member in 1786, while the Institut National des Sciences et des Arts, now the Institut de France, elected Van Marum as a foreign member in 1803.

Berthollet also played an important role in the Egyptian Expedition. One of his assignments was to identify resources of strategic and economic value, including fuel for bread ovens, substitutes for hops in beer production, and raw materials for the manufacture of gunpowder. Under the command of Antoine-François Andréossy, he took part in an expedition to explore the Valley of the Natron Lakes, whose margins were covered with thick deposits of natron, a substance essential to gunpowder production.

Claude Louis Berthollet was also a prominent member of the Institut National des Sciences et des Arts, where he contributed to the development and organisation of scientific research in Napoleonic France.
