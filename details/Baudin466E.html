Nicolas-Martin Petit, portraitist of the Baudin expedition

Born in Paris in June 1777, Nicolas-Martin Petit embarked at Le Havre aboard le Géographe at the age of twenty-three. He was not officially recruited as an artist, but as a fourth-class assistant gunner. Baudin nevertheless employed him privately as a draughtsman for his own journal, recognising very early the graphic abilities of a young man whose original position on board gave little indication of the considerable role he would come to play in the visual memory of the expedition.

The departure, at Île de France, of the three artists officially appointed to the voyage profoundly altered Petit’s position. Together with Charles-Alexandre Lesueur, he became one of the two principal artists of the Baudin expedition. His drawings, later published in the Atlas volumes accompanying the historical account of the voyage, now form one of the most remarkable bodies of work produced by the expedition, particularly in relation to ethnographic subjects.

Petit was above all an exceptional portraitist. His talent may partly be explained by artistic training received in Paris, probably in the circle of Jacques-Louis David, a hypothesis advanced by several scholars. His representations of men and women encountered in New Holland and Van Diemen’s Land are striking for their precision of line, their attention to faces, postures, ornaments and individual expression. Far from reducing his sitters to generic types, Petit sought to capture distinct presences. This ability to individualise the people he portrayed gives his work a rare force within the context of early nineteenth-century voyages of exploration.

The Portrait of Arra-Maïda, woman from South Cape, Tasmania, made in 1802, is one of the most powerful examples of this quality. Executed in watercolour, gouache, ink and pencil on paper, the drawing is now held by the Muséum d’histoire naturelle in Le Havre. A note attributed to François Péron appears on the work: “For a simple outline, carefully retain the general shapes, but retouch the main flaws; as the slender limbs are a characteristic of this race, they must be depicted with great care.” Marked by the classificatory vocabulary of its time, this annotation also reveals the importance attached to bodily precision and to the documentary value of the portrait.

Péron was particularly receptive to this encounter in Van Diemen’s Land, and to the maternal tenderness shown by Arra-Maïda towards her young daughter. In his account, he described the young woman as having “an interesting physiognomy” and eyes in which he perceived “expression and something spirited”. He also observed that she seemed to “cherish her child greatly”, and that her care had “that affectionate and gentle character which recurs among all peoples as the particular attribute of maternal tenderness”. In Péron’s prose, as in Petit’s drawing, Arra-Maïda thus appears not merely as an ethnographic figure, but as an individual presence, observed in her dignity, expression and humanity.

Petit’s work therefore occupies an essential place in the visual history of encounters between Europeans and the Aboriginal peoples of Australia. It reflects both the scientific ambitions of the expedition, the growing importance accorded to ethnographic observation, and the sensitive eye of an artist capable of bringing forth, within the constrained framework of a scientific voyage, human figures of remarkable intensity.

Nicolas-Martin Petit died prematurely in Paris on 21 October 1804, at the age of twenty-seven, from gangrene following a knee injury caused by a fall in a street of the capital. His career was brief, but his legacy was considerable: together with Lesueur, he made a decisive contribution to the artistic and documentary dimension of the Baudin expedition.
