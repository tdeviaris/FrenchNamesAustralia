Cap Montgolfier - Frenchman Bluff

First illustration
This print depicts the experiment conducted at Versailles on 19 September 1783 in the presence of Louis XVI, Marie Antoinette and the royal family. The Montgolfier brothers’ balloon carried a sheep, a duck and a cockerel, the first animals sent aloft in a balloon equipped with a basket. The original title uses the historical spelling “Versaille”.

Second illustration
This print depicts the Battle of Fleurus, fought on 26 June 1794. The tethered balloon L’Entreprenant, visible above the battlefield, was used to observe the positions and movements of the forces of the First Coalition. Designed by Thomas-Charles Naudet and engraved by Pierre-Adrien Le Beau, the image was published between 1798 and 1802. It is therefore a later representation of the battle rather than an eyewitness image produced at the scene.

The balloon companies
Established during the French Revolution in 1794, the balloon companies were specialised military units responsible for operating tethered observation balloons such as L’Entreprenant. Their members produced the hydrogen; inflated, transported and maintained the balloons; and controlled them from the ground by means of cables. Officers or observers took their places in the basket to monitor enemy positions and movements and transmit their findings to the military command.

These companies were the first permanent military units devoted to aerial observation. They notably took part in the campaign in Belgium and the Battle of Fleurus. Their use nevertheless remained difficult: the balloons depended heavily on weather conditions, were cumbersome to move with an army and required heavy equipment to produce hydrogen. Considered expensive and of limited effectiveness, the companies were abolished by the Directory in 1799.

Symbolic and scientific uses
Balloons nevertheless retained considerable symbolic importance. During the celebrations for Napoleon’s coronation on 2 December 1804, the aeronaut Jacques Garnerin launched an immense gas balloon from Paris, decorated with an imperial eagle and approximately three thousand pieces of coloured glass. Carried southwards, it reached the vicinity of Rome the following day. Strictly speaking, it was therefore not a Montgolfier-type hot-air balloon, but a gas-filled balloon.

Balloons were also used to study the atmosphere. In July 1803, Étienne-Gaspard Robertson and Auguste Lhoëst made an ascent from Hamburg during which they measured atmospheric pressure and temperature. In 1804, Joseph-Louis Gay-Lussac undertook two scientific ascents from Paris, including a solo flight to an altitude of nearly 7,000 metres, in order to investigate the physical properties and composition of the atmosphere.

Humboldt and Bonpland are sometimes mistakenly associated with a balloon ascent in June 1802. At that time, they were in the Andes attempting to climb Chimborazo.
