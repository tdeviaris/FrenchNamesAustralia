Île Bedout - Bedout Island

Cap Bedout - Cape Bedout

In this letter, Bédout forcefully expresses his dissatisfaction, his sense of injustice, and even his “disgust,” after having been promoted to the rank of rear admiral only to see the squadron he was supposed to command entrusted to another officer. This personal testimony reveals the deep dysfunctions affecting the French Navy in the aftermath of the French Revolution, characterized by unstable chains of command, the politicization of careers, and the uncertainty of appointments, all of which durably weakened the institution.

Although Bédout tendered his resignation in response to this affront, it was not accepted. In 1799, he was on board the Republican in charge of the 2nd squadron under Admiral Eustache Bruix. Leaving Brest in April 1799, this fleet reached Toulon, then sailed to Genoa to supply the French army of Italy. The following November, Bédout took command of a squadron which sailed from Lorient to Rochefort to protect the region against possible British attacks.

Then, in October 1802, Napoleon Bonaparte entrusted Bédout with the command of a new squadron of five ships, the Argonaute being the flagship. It left Brest in January 1803 for Genoa, where it embarked 5,000 Polish soldiers who were to be transported to Santo Domingo, in present-day Haiti, after the Saint-Domingue civil war.
