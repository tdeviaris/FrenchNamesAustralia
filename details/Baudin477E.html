Île Rosily - Rosily Islands

Cap Rosily - no registered English name for this place

François Étienne de Rosily-Mesros (1748-1832) was at once a naval officer, hydrographer, administrator of charts, and actor in the Revolutionary and Napoleonic wars. His name, given by the Baudin expedition to places on the Australian coast, therefore evokes a figure situated at the intersection of maritime exploration, naval cartography and imperial power.

Rosily had already taken part, in 1772, in Kerguelen's expedition to the Indian Ocean. He was then aboard La Fortune when Kerguelen believed he had discovered the southern land, now the Kerguelen Islands. Rosily was sent to reconnoitre the land sighted, but when his boat returned, La Fortune had disappeared; he was taken aboard the Gros-Ventre and only returned to France after several months at sea. This episode directly linked him to the French history of the search for the Southern Lands.

He later played an important role in hydrographic work. From the 1780s onwards, he undertook several missions in the Red Sea, the Indian Ocean and the China seas. His observations were used in nautical documents such as Le Pilote de Cochinchine, which included, among other material, a plan of Manila Bay based on his observations: http://catalogue.bnf.fr/ark:/12148/cb43615040k and https://gallica.bnf.fr/ark:/12148/btv1b525055501/f43.item. In 1795, he was appointed director and inspector general of the Dépôt des cartes et plans de la Marine, the central institution responsible for preserving, verifying and disseminating French nautical charts.

Under Napoleon, Rosily was also involved in the major naval affairs of the Empire. He supplied Bonaparte with intelligence concerning the naval dimension of the Egyptian expedition, and in 1805 was appointed to replace Villeneuve in command of the combined Franco-Spanish fleet at Cádiz. He arrived, however, only after the defeat at Trafalgar and found no more than the remnants of the combined fleet. Blockaded at Cádiz, he was eventually forced to surrender in 1808, when the Spanish uprising transformed the strategic situation.

The connection with Freycinet is especially suggestive. After returning to France, Rosily resumed his duties at the Dépôt de la Marine, while Freycinet was preparing the atlas and hydrographic section of the Voyage de découvertes aux Terres australes. It is therefore highly likely that Freycinet knew Rosily, or at least worked within an institutional framework placed under his authority or influence. This should nevertheless be stated cautiously, in the absence of direct evidence of a specific meeting. In any case, Rosily embodied the very institution through which observations gathered at sea were transformed into official charts.
