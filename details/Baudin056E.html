On a leaf of d’Alembert left foot is his preliminary discourse in the Encyclopedia

Cap d’Alembert, known today as cape Jervis, forms the tip of Fleurieu Peninsula* as well as the eastern boundary of Gulf St Vincent*. Peron, continuing his comparison of the peninsula with the ‘boot’ shape of the Italian peninsula, notes that ‘the Grand Cap d'Alembert ends the Fleurieu peninsula to the east, forming the toe of the boot’.

Jean Le Rond d'Alembert also published articles on vibrating strings and the sound waves, and on the causes of winds. He was sometimes in conflict with other savants who are also part of Péron and Freycinet’s nomenclature, such as Euler*, Clairaut* and Bernouilli* who did not validate some of d’Alembert demonstrations in physics. For example, he published a major work entitled Recherches sur la précession des équinoxes, et sur la nutation de l'axe de la terre (Research on the Precession of the Equinoxes and on the Nutation of the Earth's Axis), a problem previously addressed by Clairaut. D'Alembert's method was similar to Clairaut's, but he used more terms in his integration of the equation of motion, arriving at a solution that aligned more closely with the observed motion of the Earth, which he studied in conjunction with the measured degree of the Earth's flattening. D’Alembert is also, with Diderot, the thinking head of the Encyclopédie, ou dictionnaire raisonné des sciences, des arts et des métiers (Encyclopedia, or a Systematic Dictionary of the Sciences, Arts, and Crafts) that encouraged the spreading of scientific and rational knowledge and was based on a system of cross-referencing that bridged the different spheres of knowledge. He composed the Encyclopedia’s opening section (‘discours preliminaire’, see the illustration) where he explained that it is important to reflect on and go beyond our assumptions and the preconceptions that underlie our knowledge and opinions. He referred to Descartes*, who opposed the doubt to arbitrary thoughts. Napoléon admired not d’Alembert’s commitment to free thought and critical inquiry, but his mathematical genius.

Further information: Baudin named the cape Cap de la Sécheresse (drought). It was first named by Péron and Freycinet Cap Montesquieu*.
