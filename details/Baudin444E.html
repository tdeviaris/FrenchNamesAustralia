Cap de l’Inscription - Cape Inscription

Hamelin notes in his journal:

…”The master of the watch submitted to me his report on what he had been able to observe concerning the tide, and brought back with him a flattened pewter plate bearing an inscription left on Hartogs Island by the navigator, commander of the Endraght, in 1616, together with that engraved beneath it in 1697 by Vlamingh, commander of the Geelving. He found this kind of medal on the north-eastern point, fallen at the foot of the post (now rotten) to which it had formerly been nailed…

Had this inscription still been fastened to the post, I should have regarded it as a crime on the part of the master of the watch to have laid a sacrilegious hand upon it in order to remove it; but as he found it lying upon the ground, exposed to being buried there by its own weight alone, I commended him for having brought it back on board….

…At ten o’clock in the evening I dispatched my boat, under the command of Midshipman Duval Dailly, to convey to Dirk Hartogs Island the naturalists Bailly and Bellefin, who were to remain there for two days in order to conduct their researches.

At the same time, I charged him with planting, on the eastern coast of the said island, at one of the most remarkable points, a post bearing the following inscription (engraved upon a sheet of lead), a copy of which is here given:

[text arranged in a circle]

“French Republic. Expedition of discovery, under the orders of Captain de Vaisseau Baudin. Corvette Le Naturaliste; Capitaine Hamelin. 27 Messidor, Year IX (16 July 1801).”

Hamelin immediately understood the historical value of the object brought back on board. The pewter plate bore both the inscription left in 1616 by the crew of the Eendracht, commanded by Dirk Hartog, and the one added in 1697 by Willem de Vlamingh, commander of the Geelvink. The captain of Le Naturaliste emphasizes the respect owed to such monuments: he states that he would have considered it a sacrilegious act to remove the plate had it still been fixed to its post. But, since it had been found on the ground at the foot of a rotten post, he believed that it risked disappearing, buried by its own weight.

Hamelin therefore decided to preserve it. He ordered the master carpenter to prepare a new post, to which the plate was to be firmly nailed, before being returned to the very place where the Dutch navigators had set it. He also had a French inscription placed on a sheet of lead, intended to record the passage of the Baudin expedition and of the corvette Le Naturaliste at Dirk Hartog Island. Cape Inscription thus recalls an exceptional moment of historical transmission: the French did not merely visit a place already marked by European maritime history; they recognized, restored and extended that commemorative act by adding their own trace.
