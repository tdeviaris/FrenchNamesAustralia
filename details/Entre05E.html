The original naming of Rocher Blanc (White Rock) reflects the way the French perceived it during their running survey. The white patches noted by the first observers were likely the result of droppings from the seabirds nesting on the rock, which is composed of dark grey dolerite.


The officers’ very first concern was of the dangers and hazards these islets represented for navigation: In the report on his survey of the coasts near Recherche Bay, Lieutenant Alexis-Ignace de Cretin wrote: ‘I had been ordered to visit the coasts situated east and north-east of Port du Nord. I left Recherche Bay on the morning of 30 April and was soon joined by the boat from l’Esperance , commanded by M. de la Grandière, lieutenant de vaisseau. As we were leaving Port du Nord, we followed the coast to larboard. After passing the land for one league, we found ourselves in the middle of rocks which were protruding far offshore; some of these rocks were level with the water, others were rather high … We followed the coast in a northerly direction to pass windward of the Sterile Islands, and the dangers to starboard.’

The rocks which de Cretin and his companions observed form part of the Actaeon Island Group. They consist of a system of small low-lying islands, rocky islets and extensive reefs. Their generally barren nature must have prompted d’Entrecasteaux to give the name of Sterile Island to one of these small specks of land.
