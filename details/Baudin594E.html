Banc des Méduses - Medusa Bank

Writing of the seas to the north and north-west of New Holland, Péron observed:

“Of all the parts of the sea that we were able to see, none was richer in pelagic zoophytes than those washing the coasts of Witt Land and Arnhem Land, to the north and north-west of New Holland. The family of jellyfish includes a great number of remarkable species, among which we must especially distinguish the beautiful Aequorea [...] the animal appears in the midst of darkness as if wrapped in a circle of fire, sparkling with green, purple and azure.”

After the return of Le Géographe, Péron worked with Lesueur on several publications, including the memoir “Sur les Méduses du genre Équorée”, published in 1810 in the Annales du Muséum d’histoire naturelle. In this text, the two authors did not simply describe new species observed during the voyage: they sought to understand how these animals functioned. They regarded the Aequorea as organisms of particularly simple structure, composed almost entirely of a gelatinous, transparent or lightly coloured substance, whose tissue appeared homogeneous and without visible internal vessels. This matter seemed so fragile that it rapidly dissolved when exposed to air or during decomposition, leaving only a very slight membranous residue.

Péron and Lesueur then turned to the question of locomotion. The Aequorea move through the regular contraction and dilation of their umbrella. By contracting, they push away the water beneath their body and propel themselves in the opposite direction; by dilating again, they continue this movement. To rise, they adopt a vertical position; to change direction, they incline their umbrella; to descend, their own weight often suffices, since their substance is denser than seawater. The authors thus describe a precise mechanics of movement, based on the direct observation of living jellyfish.

They also analyse the means by which these animals seize their prey. Although the Aequorea lack the developed arms found in some other jellyfish, they possess long, sensitive, thread-like tentacles, which move around the animal, capture prey and draw it towards the opening of the stomach. Their food probably consists mainly of small gelatinous animalcules abundant in the sea; species with a larger stomach, however, may attack beroes, salps and even small pelagic fish.

Their digestive system particularly intrigued the two naturalists. The stomach, thin, gelatinous and apparently little organised, at first seemed incapable of retaining and digesting prey. Yet Péron and Lesueur observed the presence of a slightly viscous gastric fluid, which produced on the tongue a sharp but fleeting sensation of burning. They concluded that this fluid played an essential role in digestion, penetrating, dissolving and decomposing the substance of the captured animals.

The memoir finally emphasises contractility, which Péron and Lesueur regarded as a fundamental property of jellyfish. The regular contractions of the umbrella do not serve only for locomotion: they continue even when the animal is not moving, when it is placed in a vessel, removed from the water, or even when its umbrella is cut into fragments. These oscillations, compared to movements of systole and diastole, seem to contribute to several vital functions: the circulation of fluids, digestion, nutrition, excretion and perhaps respiration. The authors therefore interpret them not merely as a mechanism of swimming, but as a general principle of organisation and life.

The scientific interest of the Aequorea thus lay in their apparent simplicity. Their almost transparent body, their weakly differentiated tissue and their vital functions centred on contractility seemed to offer naturalists a privileged model for observing life in its most elementary forms. This explains the importance attached to jellyfish by Péron, Lesueur, Cuvier and the scientists of the Muséum: their study appeared capable of illuminating the first degrees of animal organisation and, more broadly, the origins of life.
