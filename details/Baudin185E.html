Pingré contributed to one of the central technical problems of eighteenth-century navigation: the determination of longitude at sea. He worked on methods based on lunar observations, comparing the formulae used by La Caille — see Pointe La Caille — which relied on the measured distances between the Moon and the Sun or fixed stars, with his own approach, based on the hourly declination and right ascension of the Moon. These methods belonged to the broader effort to make astronomical navigation more reliable before the general use of marine chronometers.

Pingré’s work had a direct bearing on voyages of discovery. In long-distance navigation, especially in the Pacific and the Indian Ocean, the ability to determine longitude accurately was essential for fixing a ship’s position, drawing coastal charts, avoiding reefs and shoals, and returning to previously observed points. He published nautical almanacs that supplied astronomers and navigators with the tables needed for these calculations. These almanacs were used by Fleurieu and Borda, who compared Pingré’s astronomical results with longitudes obtained from the marine chronometers made by Le Roy and Berthoud.

This comparison between lunar methods and chronometric methods was crucial for the scientific voyages of the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries. Expeditions such as Baudin’s relied on both astronomical observations and marine timekeepers to establish more accurate maps of distant coasts. Chronometers were valuable instruments, but they did not eliminate the need for verification: their daily rate had to be regularly checked, and any gain or loss had to be taken into account when recalculating longitude. Astronomical observations, lunar distances and nautical tables therefore remained essential, not only as independent methods but also as means of controlling and correcting the performance of the watches. In this sense, Pingré’s work forms part of the scientific background that made such voyages possible: the exploration of new lands was inseparable from the improvement of mathematical astronomy, nautical tables, timekeeping instruments and hydrographic surveying.
