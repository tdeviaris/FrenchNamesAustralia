Cap Jaffa - Cape Martin

Courtyard of a mosque used as a military hospital in Jaffa. Scene from the Syrian Campaign. Bonaparte touches the tumour on the naked chest of one of the patients with his hand, the doctor tries to stop him and an officer holds his nose. In the foreground, the sick are in agony.

It is possible that during the survey conducted under sail (running survey), in weather described as “overcast, very dark and misty, with a limited horizon” (Baudin’s journal, entry for 5 April 1802, p. 75), the officers were unable to clearly distinguish the contours of the coastline. They may have believed that Inner Penguin Island, one of the two rocky outcrops that together form Penguin Island, was connected to Cape Jaffa (Cape Martin). Consequently, only the second outcrop, Outer Penguin Island, appears on the original charts. Penguin Island consists of two rocky outcrops, commonly referred to as Outer Penguin Island and Inner Penguin Island.

The painting of the plague victims of Jaffa by Antoine-Jean Gros, completed in September 1804, ahead of Napoléon’s crowning (2 December 1804), was commissioned by Napoléon Bonaparte to draw a parallel between himself and the assumed miraculous powers possessed by past kings who had touched the sick bodies of their people. Péron and Freycinet likely saw the painting at an exhibition organized by Denon at the Louvre in 1804.

Further information

Baudin called the place ‘Cap Le Noir’, perhaps in reference to ‘citizen Le Noir’ (Lenoir) who had designed the reflecting circles used on board the corvette. Confusedly, on today’s maps, there is another place named Cape Jaffa, about 60 kilometres to the north, and 19 kilometres southwest of the town centre of Kingston.
