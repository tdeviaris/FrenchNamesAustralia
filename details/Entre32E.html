Pilot of La Recherche, Pierre-Guillaume Gicquel Destouches (1770–1824), first enlisted as a volontaire on the expedition. He was promoted to enseigne (sub-lieutenant) in February 1793, in recognition of his surveying skills, which greatly supported the work of Beautemps-Beaupré and Rossel. A committed republican, Destouches found himself at odds with d’Auribeau, who succeeded d’Entrecasteaux after the latter’s death.

Destouches was rigorous in his work, as demonstrated by the five notebooks that form his logbook, complemented by a volume on magnetic variations and a short vocabulary of words from the languages of Indigenous peoples encountered. His perspective on these communities was shaped by the Eurocentric outlook shared by most Europeans of the time, who regarded their own technological civilization as a means of transcending what they perceived as a "natural" state of existence—an attitude that persisted regardless of the friendly disposition of the Tasmanians.
