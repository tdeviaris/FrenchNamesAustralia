Cap Cassini - Cassini Cape

Île Cassini - Cassini Island

Four members of the Cassini family — Jean-Dominique Cassini, Jacques Cassini, César-François Cassini de Thury, and Jean-Dominique Cassini, comte de Cassini — are commonly identified as Cassini I to Cassini IV. They played a major role in French astronomy, geodesy, and cartography during the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, particularly through their association with the Paris Observatory.

Jean-Dominique Cassini first established an important meridian line in Bologna and later contributed, after his arrival in France under Louis XIV, to the astronomical work carried out at the Paris Observatory. Meridian lines, combined with astronomical observations, made it possible to determine positions and longitudes with greater accuracy. Observations of the eclipses of Jupiter's Galilean satellites (Io, Europa, Ganymede and Callisto) also played an important role in longitude determination, especially for land-based cartography.

César-François Cassini de Thury and his son Jean-Dominique Cassini were the principal architects of the first general map of France produced by geodetic triangulation. The surveys and copperplate engraving for this map, known as the Cassini map, began in the mid-eighteenth century and were not fully completed until 1815. The map consists of 180 sheets and is drawn at an approximate scale of 1:86,400.

Although the Cassini map was not fully completed until 1815, Napoléon I had already recognised its limitations for military and administrative purposes. In 1808, he ordered the preparation of a new and more detailed map of France, better suited to the needs of the army and entrusted to military geographical engineers. Cassini IV nevertheless received a pension and was decorated by Napoléon, a sign of official recognition for the role played by the Cassini family in the history of French astronomy and cartography.
