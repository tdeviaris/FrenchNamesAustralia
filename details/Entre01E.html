On 20 April 1792, the southern part of Recherche Bay came into view, a sheltered anchorage named Port du Sud by the French. It was the first Australian harbour the expedition discovered and surveyed. The calm waters, reliable fresh water, and abundant seafood reported by d’Entrecasteaux’s reconnaissance officers brought welcome relief after weeks of hardship at sea. Relentless westerly winds and heavy seas had strained the frigates, broken chains, and allowed seawater to seep into storerooms. The ordeal had even caused d’Entrecasteaux a serious injury, when a violent roll of the ship threw him heavily against the deck, badly damaging his ribs. For both commander and crew, the refuge of Port du Sud marked a much-needed respite. When the expedition returned to Recherche Bay and Port du Sud in early 1793, the astronomers established their observatories (one for each vessel) on the southern shoreline enclosing Port du Sud, from where they took astronomical bearings.
