Morard de Galles, J-B: engraved by Quenedey, who used a process called physionotrace which involved the projection of the shadow of a profile in actual size on a sheet of transparent paper, then reduced its scale and engraved it on copper plate, 1790. Morard de Galles is represented with a serious, concentrated look, as he was at that time in quest for professional advancement. Please note: According to Hennequin (an historian who studied Quenedey’s technic), this portrait represents someone else, but no evidence confirms this. Source : Bibliothèque nationale de France, département Estampes et photographie http://catalogue.bnf.fr/ark:/12148/cb40251882g

The name of Morard-de-Galles does not appear on either of the several lists of names suggested for the atlas or the narrative of the voyage. Freycinet’s superiors or even Hamelin alongside whom Morard fought may have suggested its late inclusion. Initially, Baudin named the cape Cap des Méduses (jellyfishs). Péron said he saw ‘a few jellyfish’ on 2 April, near Cape Boufflers.
