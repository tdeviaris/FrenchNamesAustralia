Île Corneille – Corneille Island

Baie Corneille – False Bay

Pierre Corneille represented, within the Napoleonic imagination, a particularly powerful image of French greatness: that of a classical, heroic, and Roman theatre founded upon honour, duty, reason of state, and self-mastery. In the national narrative that Napoléon sought to construct, Corneille thus came to embody a France at once literary, political, and imperial. Napoléon clearly preferred Corneille to Racine. A frequently repeated saying sums up this admiration: “Corneille is the only one who showed in his tragedies that he understood reason of state”; he is even said to have added that he would have made him his Prime Minister.

Among the plays by Corneille performed or especially valued under Napoléon were Cinna, The Death of Pompey, Le Cid, Horace, Polyeucte, Nicomède, Rodogune, and Héraclius. This repertoire was particularly suited to Napoleonic tastes because it staged the tensions between passion and duty, vengeance and pardon, individual greatness and political reason. In Cinna, or the Clemency of Augustus, the figure of Augustus made it possible to conceive the ideal emperor as a man capable of mastering his passions, rising above vengeance, and governing in the name of a higher interest. Clemency appears there not as weakness, but as a higher form of power.

The Death of Pompey also interested Napoléon because it brought onto the stage Caesar and, with him, the question of how power should be represented. After one performance, Napoléon is said to have reproached Talma, who played Caesar, for moving his arms too much, reminding him that “rulers of empires are less prodigal of movement” and that “a gesture is an order, a glance is death.” The remark clearly shows that Napoléon did not view the play merely as a spectator: he found in it a genuine lesson in power. The theatre thus became a mirror of imperial authority.

With Talma and Mlle George, Corneille’s theatre also acquired a personal and political dimension. Talma, Napoléon’s favourite actor, appeared as a kind of theatrical embodiment of Bonaparte; Mlle George, the great tragedienne of the Comédie-Française, enjoyed Napoléon’s protection and was also his mistress. Under Napoléon, Corneille was therefore not simply a great classical author: he became a political reference, offering the Empire an imaginative language of greatness, order, reason of state, and ancient heroism.
