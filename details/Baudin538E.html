Golfe Bonaparte – Spencer Gulf

About the illustrations:

The first illustration is a print reproducing the first truly emblematic image of Bonaparte at the head of the Army of Italy. It presents the young general in heroic, almost allegorical terms, leading his troops with sabre and flag in hand and seizing victory through courage and audacity. The original painting was executed by Jean-Antoine Gros as early as 1796, at the Palazzo Serbelloni in Milan, from a sketch approved by Bonaparte himself — see this sketch in the entry for Pic d’Arcole, in Tasmania. It was Joséphine who introduced Gros to Bonaparte.

The second illustration offers a darker portrait of Bonaparte, in marked contrast to the heroic image of the bridge at Arcole. Yet the Latin reference to Horace that accompanies it — Odes, II, 1, 1-16 — likewise seeks to place Bonaparte within a glorious and enduring memory. The phrase Cui laurus aeterno honores Italo peperit triumpho associates his name with the eternal honours won through his Italian triumph.

At the time when Péron selected the names that would appear on the map of the atlases of the Voyage de découvertes aux terres australes, Bonaparte had already been proclaimed and then crowned “Emperor of the French” with the name Napoléon I, by the Constitution of an XII (May 1804) and at a coronation ceremony on 2 December 1804 at the cathedral Notre-Dame de Paris. Despite this, Péron chose to give the emperor’s surname of Bonaparte to the gulf, under which he was formerly known. Péron recalled here the republican time when Napoléon was the young general Bonaparte leading the Army of Italy and later the consul who commissioned the voyage of discoveries on behalf of the French Republic.

This choice is not neutral. It refers less to the already established emperor than to the memory of the young general of the Army of Italy, then the First Consul who had supported, in the name of the French Republic, the voyage of discovery to the Southern Lands. The toponym thus preserves the memory of a political moment that predated the Empire: that of the republican Bonaparte, victorious conqueror and patron of the sciences.

Locations along the shores of the gulf were given names that honoured Napoléon Bonaparte’s most favoured public figures whose high standing reflected on his own power and reign. They included the names of his much-loved writers from the “Grand Siècle” of Louis XIV, such as Corneille, Racine and La Fontaine, as well as of scientists, mathematicians and others in whose work Bonaparte had a special interest. Through this geography of names, Bonaparte Gulf became a symbolic space where maritime exploration, republican memory, military glory and the cultural legitimation of Napoleonic power were intertwined.
