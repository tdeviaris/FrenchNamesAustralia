An overall length of 127 feet corresponds to about 41.1 metres; a maximum beam of 27 feet to about 8.77 metres.

Originally built as a storeship under the name La Menaçante, the vessel was acquired for the voyage, renamed Le Naturaliste in 1800, and subsequently refitted and reclassified as a corvette. As with the Géographe, its wooden sheathing, vulnerable to attack from the common shipworm, a small bivalve mollusc capable of destroying hull planking, was removed and replaced with copper sheathing. The Minister of the Navy at the time, Forfait, attentive to technical detail, wrote as follows to the chief naval commissioner at Le Havre on 30 Messidor Year VIII (19 July 1800):

“I have received, Citizen, together with your letter of 19 Messidor [8 July], the observations of Engineer Lafosse regarding the order I transmitted to you to remove the wooden sheathing from the storeship La Menaçante and replace it with copper.

I was not unaware that this vessel was fastened with iron bolts, but as I cannot dispose of any other, and as this inconvenience must nevertheless be remedied as far as possible, you will have all bolt- and nail-heads covered with bottle mastic. I should point out that this mastic, which is used for sealing corks, cannot be replaced by that of glaziers.

This operation must be carried out with the greatest care and scrupulous exactness. You will appreciate its full importance for a vessel intended for a long voyage, during which major repairs would be impracticable.

Forfait

P.S. You will have a small washer of cloth placed over the mastic covering each bolt- and nail-head. This washer adheres very easily to the hot mastic.”

The assistant naval engineer Lefebvre described the internal arrangements of the Naturaliste in these terms:

“The arrangements of this storeship are the same as those of the Géographe, apart from a few slight modifications. There is a wine hold right forward, separated by a longitudinal partition from the lion pit. The former occupies the whole port side and the latter the starboard side. The galley is between the heel of the bowsprit and the bitts. The great cabin has bookcase cupboards on both sides. There is no poop, but only a coach aft of the mizzen mast, fitted on the port side with a folding escritoire disguised as a cupboard between two real cupboards. The starboard side presents the same appearance, but instead of an escritoire there is a bed that folds up. Fore and aft are lockers forming seats. This vessel is steered with a tiller. Surveyed by me, assistant naval engineer, at Le Havre on 14 Vendémiaire Year IX.”

It should be noted, however, that since the fittings of Le Naturaliste were very similar to those of Le Géographe, her galley also proved defective. Before the first stopover at Tenerife, Hamelin reported:

“As the galley produces a great deal of smoke and does not draw air from the hold as expected, I have provisionally had the still removed, pending a thorough examination of its defects and advantages at Santa Cruz.”

At the time of her departure from Le Havre, Le Naturaliste had approximately 120 people on board, a complement that included the crew, a few passengers, and several unauthorised embarkations. Some of these passengers later disembarked at Île de France. As on Le Géographe, the number of men on board subsequently varied over the course of the voyage as a result of illness, deaths, desertions, and transfers between the two vessels.

After the expedition, the Naturaliste was decommissioned, reclassified as a storeship, and finally condemned and sold in 1811.
