Cap Sévigné - Rapid Head

Orphaned at an early age and widowed at twenty-five, Madame de Sévigné enjoyed an unusual degree of autonomy for a woman of her rank in seventeenth-century France. She led an active social life and cultivated a lasting engagement with reading, writing, and conversation.

She moved in Parisian literary circles, notably the Hôtel de Rambouillet, famous for its "Blue Room", and Madeleine de Scudéry's Saturday gatherings. She also maintained a close relationship with her relative and friend Madame de La Fayette, author of La Princesse de Clèves, published anonymously in 1678. In conversation and correspondence alike, Madame de Sévigné distinguished herself through her wit, powers of observation, and lively style.

Her letters to her daughter reveal deep maternal affection, expressed within the social and emotional codes of her milieu. They also reflect the galant ideal associated with the Carte de Tendre, the imaginary geography devised by Madeleine de Scudéry in her novel Clélie, histoire romaine. Madame de Sévigné also commented on contemporary theatre, including Racine's plays, among them Bajazet, which was still performed during the Napoleonic period (see Cape and Isle Racine).

The women and men of letters from the age of Louis XIV who appear in Madame de Sévigné's intellectual world likewise received place-names in the French nomenclature. Taken together, these names may be read as reflecting what would later become French classical culture.

Further information. Baudin did not explicitly name the cape, but gave the nearby headland the name Pointe aux Corbeaux ("Crow Point"). An earlier stage of French mapping uses the name Cape d'Aboukir, while Cape Sévigné was retained in the nomenclature associated with Péron and Freycinet. The modern name, Rapid Head, derives from the brig Rapid, which brought Colonel William Light to South Australia in 1836.
