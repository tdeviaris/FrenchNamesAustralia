Charles-Pierre Claret de Fleurieu was a French naval officer, hydrographer, administrator, and statesman. His career, which began under the Ancien Régime and continued through the Revolution, the Consulate, and the Empire, reflects the scientific, technical, and strategic transformations of the French navy at the end of the eighteenth century. A practitioner of navigation, theorist of hydrography, and organiser of expeditions, he made a major contribution to the development of French maritime knowledge and to the preparation of the great voyages of discovery in the Pacific and the southern lands.

A leading figure in the science of navigation
From an early age, Fleurieu was drawn to mathematics and the problems of navigation. He focused on one of the principal challenges facing eighteenth-century seafarers: the reliable determination of longitude at sea. From 1765 onward, he worked with the clockmaker Ferdinand Berthoud on marine timekeepers designed to compare the time at the port of departure with local time established through astronomical observation.

In 1768-1769, he commanded the corvette Isis during a voyage intended to test Berthoud's marine clocks nos. 6 and 8 at sea, in order to assess their usefulness for determining longitude. These trials demonstrated the practical value of marine timekeepers and helped make astronomical navigation a more precise instrument of cartography. Fleurieu published the results in 1773 in his Voyage made by order of the King in 1768 and 1769 to various parts of the world in order to test at sea the marine clocks invented by Ferdinand Berthoud.

His work extended far beyond longitude. Fleurieu was the author, coordinator, or reviewer of important hydrographic and cartographic publications, including the Neptune américo-septentrional, the Neptune des mers du Nord, and the atlas of the Kattegat and the Baltic. He also contributed to the modernisation of naval signalling systems, which were essential for visual communication between ships of the same squadron. His approach therefore combined instruments, observations, charts, nomenclature, and naval organisation.

Architect of the French expeditions
Appointed Director of Ports and Arsenals in 1776, Fleurieu held a central position in the administration of the French navy for fifteen years. He supervised matériel, shipyard works, and fleet movements, while also contributing to the planning of naval campaigns and voyages of discovery. His role was especially important in the preparation of French expeditions at the end of the eighteenth century.

He helped prepare the instructions issued to Jean-François de Galaup, comte de La Pérouse, for the expedition of 1785. These instructions combined scientific objectives, hydrographic surveys, commercial enquiries, and observations on the European powers active in the Pacific. They illustrate the close relationship between maritime geography and France's strategic and imperial interests.

After La Pérouse disappeared, Fleurieu was also associated with the expedition entrusted to Bruni d'Entrecasteaux in 1791 to search for the missing ships. Under the Consulate, while presiding over the Naval Section of the Council of State, his experience and scientific authority continued to influence preparations for Nicolas Baudin's expedition to the southern lands, undertaken between 1800 and 1804. The instructions issued to Baudin called in particular for a careful examination of Van Diemen's Land and the D'Entrecasteaux Channel, in order to determine whether the British had established settlements there. The mission was therefore not limited to natural-history observations: it also reflected geopolitical concerns about British presence and expansion in the southern seas.

A thinker of historical and oceanic geography
Fleurieu was also a critical historian of navigation. His French Discoveries in 1768 and 1769 in South-Eastern New Guinea, published in 1790, defended the priority of French discoveries against later British renamings. He argued for restoring to France the recognition of its discoveries and its symbolic rights in the regions explored.

After the Revolution, he published the account of Étienne Marchand's circumnavigation of 1790-1792. In its extensive historical introduction, he surveyed discoveries made by different European powers and reflected on how the major oceanic spaces should be divided and named. The fourth volume included observations on hydrographic nomenclature, the application of the decimal metric system to navigation, and the improvement of charts.

This conception of geography rested on the precision of observation, comparison of sources, criticism of earlier accounts, and the stabilisation of names on maps. It brought together the history of discovery, nautical science, and rivalry between maritime powers.

Fleurieu, Péron, and Freycinet
Fleurieu was a major point of reference for François Péron and Louis de Freycinet, the two principal scientific authors associated with the Baudin expedition, although each emphasised a different aspect of his work.

Péron particularly admired Fleurieu's scientific and geographical thought. In his General Considerations on the Formation of Coral Islands and Mountains, he cited Fleurieu's questions about the origin of low islands and coral archipelagos. Fleurieu wondered about these “small plateaux”, isolated or grouped together, which appeared still to be growing in the middle of a deep sea, far from continents and without visible traces of ancient volcanic activity. He suggested that such formations were not the result of sudden catastrophe, but of the gradual and secular work of nature.

This attention to the slow formation of islands resonated with Péron's own research on reefs, corals, and the transformation of marine landforms. It reflects a physical geography attentive to the relationship between sea depth, island structure, living organisms, and geological time.

Péron also drew on Fleurieu's ideas in his reflections on the geographical distribution of marine animals. In his Notice on the Habitation of Marine Animals, he described the zonal variation of shell species between equatorial regions, temperate coasts, and southern seas. He praised the “fine geographical-zoological divisions” of Lacepède and Fleurieu's “valuable hydrographic-zoological work”. For Péron, marine species were not distributed at random: they formed a genuine natural geography of the seas.

Freycinet, by contrast, paid particular tribute to Fleurieu as a hydrographer and cartographer. He emphasised Fleurieu's innovative approach to printing nautical charts, especially the direct transfer of a reduced chart drawing onto copper plates. This method facilitated the drafting and reproduction of charts. Freycinet developed and refined these techniques in his own hydrographic work, particularly in preparing the atlas of the Voyage of Discovery to the Southern Lands.

A legacy inscribed in the southern lands
Péron and Freycinet gave Fleurieu's name to four places, one of the most substantial tributes in their nomenclature. These place names linked the man of science and state to the cartographic and hydrographic work carried out in the southern seas.

In South Australia, Péron and Freycinet named the peninsula south of Adelaide the Fleurieu Peninsula. The name was later replaced by Hindmarsh Peninsula. In the early twentieth century, Alphonse de Fleurieu, great-nephew of Charles-Pierre Claret de Fleurieu, petitioned South Australian authorities to restore his ancestor's name. These efforts led to the official adoption of the name Fleurieu Peninsula in 1911.

In Tasmania, Freycinet's Île Fleurieu corresponds to present-day Hunter Island, while the wider island group to which it belongs, formerly called the Hunter Group in British nomenclature, is now commonly known as the Fleurieu Group. Péron's Rivière Fleurieu and Baie Fleurieu were later designated in British colonial toponymy as Agnes Rivulet and Great Oyster Bay.

These names were not merely personal tributes. They expressed Fleurieu's place within a French geography of the southern lands, founded on coastal observation, positional measurement, chart production, and awareness of imperial rivalries.

A political career across successive regimes
Fleurieu's technical expertise enabled him to retain an important role despite profound political upheaval. He served as Minister of the Navy and Colonies from October 1790 to April 1791, resigning amid tensions between the king, the Assembly, and the naval administration. He was then appointed governor to the Dauphin in 1792.

Arrested during the Terror, he was imprisoned before being released after Thermidor. He soon regained a position in scientific and political institutions: he became a member of the Bureau des longitudes and the Institut in 1795, and a Councillor of State from 1799. Under the Empire, he presided over the Naval Section of the Council of State, acted several times as interim Minister of the Navy, became a senator in 1805, and was made a Count of the Empire in 1808.

Fleurieu's career illustrates the continuity of French maritime knowledge across changing political regimes. His influence is not measured solely by his official roles or his publications, but also by the way in which his methods, ideas and authority served as a reference for the expeditions led by La Pérouse, d'Entrecasteaux and Baudin.
