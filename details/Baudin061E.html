Fleurieu is the author of significant publications that were scientific, informative, but also strategic with a sense of geopolitics, aimed to defend French maritime discoveries and their overseas presence against the British. For example, Fleurieu wrote Découvertes des françois en 1768 et 1769 dans le sud-est de la Nouvelle Guinée with ‘the desire of restoring to the French nation its own discoveries, which an emulous and jealous neighbour [the British] has endeavoured to appropriate to herself…’. Before the Revolution, this naval officer and savant had been director of the French naval ports and arsenals, then intermittent minister of the navy and governor of the dauphin. During the Terror (see this entry in the glossary), he was deprived of resources, arrested, and imprisoned. After the French Revolution, Fleurieu edited and published an account of a voyage around the world (1790–1792) commanded by Étienne Marchand (1755–1793). This was the second circumnavigation of the world by French navigators, following Antoine de Bougainville’s voyage. Compared to the mortality rate among crews on similar voyages, it was remarkable that no lives were lost, except for one due to natural causes In his long introduction to the book, Fleurieu reviewed the geographical knowledge already obtained on previous voyages that itemize each nation’s own discoveries and commented on navigation and its progress. He concluded this work with a memoir proposing a universal nomenclature for the oceans organised around ‘le Grand Océan’ (Pacific Ocean) and its different divisions all named according to their geographical location (for example, ‘Grand Océan Austral’ [Great Southern Ocean] for the body of water separating Australia from Antarctica and ‘Grand Océan Équinoxal’ for the sea off the eastern coast of Australia - a terminology which Beautemps-Beaupré and Freycinet introduced in their atlases). The memoir also advocated for the introduction of the metric system in the Navy, which Freycinet applied in his working charts by converting marine leagues into ‘myriameters’ (10.000 meters). Fleurieu was also a great administrator able to conceive and perform projects efficiently at the Dépôt des Cartes et Plans de la Marine* as director of the naval yards and as a government minister. Backed by his expertise and skills, he took a leading role in the planning of the three French voyages of discovery in the late eighteen century. After the return of the savants and officers of the d’Entrecasteaux and the Baudin expeditions, Fleurieu continued to support the findings of these voyages. In 1803, Decrès* entrusted him with the task of supervising the edition of the narrative of the d’Entrecasteaux voyage. Then, in 1806, Fleurieu was, together with Laplace*, Bougainville*, Lacépède* and Cuvier*, part of the commission of the Institut* who wrote a report in favour of the publication of the narrative detailing the findings from Baudin’s voyage. Delivered to the government, this report concluded encouragingly ‘that the works which he [Péron] is preparing must contribute splendidly to the progress of the natural sciences’.This judgment contrasted with the rather negative opinions expressed by some scientists who had embarked in Le Havre but, faced with the difficult conditions of the voyage, abandoned the expedition in Isle de France before their mission even began.. Péron acknowledges the ‘rigorousness’ of Fleurieu’s thoughts, and Freycinet salutes Fleurieu’s innovative thinking to print with copper plates fair charts. In their nomenclature, they dedicated four place names to Fleurieu, the most given to any one individual. Fleurieu is also thanked for his advice and support in the introduction of the narratives of the two voyages.

Further information: Péron notes: ‘This peninsula, like the one in Italy, has the shape of a boot: the cape Mollien* ends it in the East; then slopes northward, forming a picturesque bay approximately 10 to 12 miles long and nearly as wide. We have named it Bay Cretet*; further on are the small isles Decaen* and the cape of the same name, which forms, so to speak, the spur of the boot; Cape Montalivet* forms the heel … the large cape d'Alembert* ends the Fleurieu peninsula to the east forming the tip of the foot of the boot’. An island (Île Fleurieu*), a river (Rivière Fleurieu*) and a bay (Baie Fleurieu*) were also named after Fleurieu, in Tasmania. Baudin did not name this peninsula as he sailed along its shore in 1802. It was Péron and Freycinet who first named it in honour of Fleurieu. Some years after the founding of South Australia in 1834, the peninsula was given the name of Hindmarsh, after the first governor of the state, Sir John Hindmarsh (1836-38). In 1911, Fleurieu’s grand-nephew, Alphonse de Fleurieu*, made representations to the authorities in South Australia to have the name of his forebear formally reinstated. The portion of an 1878 map of the Gulfs of St Vincent and Spencer shows the name "Presqu’Île de Fleurieu," which had by that time been replaced by "Hindmarsh." It also includes, in red ink, some of the other French place names along the coasts of the Fleurieu Peninsula, which Alphonse de Fleurieu attempted—unsuccessfully—to reinstate.
