The 1798 memoir on nautical astronomy
The first illustration shows the Mémoire sur l’astronomie nautique, et particulièrement sur l’utilité des méthodes graphiques pour le calcul de la longitude à la mer, par les distances de la lune au soleil et aux étoiles. “Citizen Rochon” read this memoir before the Institut national on 1 Ventôse, Year VI, corresponding to 19 February 1798.

In it, Rochon examined the use of graphical methods to facilitate the calculation of longitude at sea from measurements of the angular distances between the Moon and the Sun or selected stars. Such methods were intended to reduce the length and complexity of the calculations required to interpret astronomical observations made aboard ship.

Rochon had been admitted in 1795 to the Institut national’s First Class, devoted to the physical and mathematical sciences. In the digitised copy held by the Bibliothèque nationale de France, the words identifying him as a member of the First Class appear to have been crossed out by hand. This alteration should not be interpreted as evidence that Rochon had been expelled: he remained a member of the Institut and retained his position when it was reorganised in 1803. The date, authorship and purpose of the manuscript correction have not been established.

The publication of Marion Dufresne’s voyage
The second illustration shows a detail from the plate entitled Côtes des Terres de Diémen parcourues par Mr Marion. The chart was published in 1783 with the Nouveau voyage à la mer du Sud, an account prepared by Rochon principally from Julien Crozet’s narrative and from the journals, observations and surveys brought back by members of Marc-Joseph Marion Dufresne’s expedition.

It depicts part of the south-eastern coast of Van Diemen’s Land, near present-day Cape Frederick Hendrick and the waters now known as Marion Bay and North Bay. Following Marion Dufresne’s death in New Zealand in 1772, Rochon thus helped preserve, organise and publish the geographical and scientific results of the voyage. It is nevertheless safer to describe the chart as compiled or published under his direction from the expedition’s surveys, rather than as personally drawn by him.

Maritime missions
Rochon was appointed astronomer to several maritime missions. In 1769, he accompanied Jacques-Raymond de Grenier in the Indian Ocean. The voyage was concerned in particular with a more direct route between Isle de France and India, although Grenier’s conclusions and calculations gave rise to a controversy in which Rochon became involved.

On 1 May 1771, Rochon sailed from Lorient aboard the Berryer, commanded by Yves-Joseph de Kerguelen-Trémarec. One of the mission’s objectives was to examine the route proposed by Grenier. Rochon and Kerguelen nevertheless disagreed over their respective responsibilities, the interpretation of observations and the astronomer’s scientific independence. Rochon did not continue the campaign with Kerguelen after their arrival at Isle de France.

He therefore took part neither in the 1772 voyage of the Fortune and the Gros Ventre towards the southern lands, nor in the continuation of Louis Aleno de Saint-Aloüarn’s mission to the western coast of Australia. After becoming separated from Kerguelen’s vessel in the archipelago that now bears Kerguelen’s name, Saint-Aloüarn continued eastwards and formally claimed the Shark Bay region in the name of Louis XV.

Rochon during the Revolution and the Consulate
Rochon continued to show a strong interest in the practical applications of science. During the Revolutionary Wars, when the British blockade made it difficult to obtain the animal horn used to protect lights aboard French naval vessels, he proposed a substitute made from a very fine fabric of copper wire.

When Péron and Freycinet developed and published the French nomenclature of the Australian coastline, Rochon was a recognised figure at the Institut. He examined memoirs and projects relating in particular to navigation, tides, harbours, the properties of crystals, optics and scientific instruments.
