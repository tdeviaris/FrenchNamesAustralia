Péron’s descriptions of animals are deeply imbued with Buffon’s evocative style. For example, when referring to seals, Buffon alludes to their “docility”, “sweet look”, and their ability to “express feelings of affection and loyalty” {« Le regard de cet animal est doux, et son naturel n'est point farouche; ses yeux sont attentifs et … expriment du moins ses sentimens d'affection et d'attachement … »), while Péron, portraying Sea elephants at King Island, praised their “so sweet and lovely physiognomy” (“Les Éléphans marins sont d'un naturel extrêmement doux et facile … tous ces animaux ont une physionomie si douce et si bonne …”) Péron referred to Buffon as ‘the French Pliny’, in reference to the famous Roman naturalist Pliny the Elder (AD 23/24-AD 79). {« Le regard de cet animal est doux, et son naturel n'est point farouche; ses yeux sont attentifs et … expriment du moins ses sentimens d'affection et d'attachement pour son maître… ») Additional information: Baudin named the cape ‘Cap Bourru’ (Cape Grouchy). In his 1814 atlas, the English navigator Matthew Flinders bestowed the name Buffon on a nearby cape that the French had initially named Cap Lannes. Perhaps Flinders felt that the capes had not been correctly positioned concerning their assumed geographical coordinates and, therefore, used this renaming to question the accuracy of Freycinet’s atlas.Buffon is also honoured with an island in WA.
