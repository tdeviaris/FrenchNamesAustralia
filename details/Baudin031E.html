Cap Buffon – Cape Banks

Île Buffon – Buffon Island

In classifying and interpreting the animal specimens he collected during the Baudin expedition, Péron frequently referred to Buffon’s major work, Histoire naturelle, générale et particulière. Since Buffon had established a new order for only part of the animal kingdom—quadrupeds and birds—Péron set himself the task of extending this enterprise by adding descriptions of various marine species absent from the Histoire naturelle. He thus wrote: “not only then do we hope to prove the accuracy of the laws established by Buffon and his honourable emulators, but we shall also demonstrate that they are applicable to all species of animals, whether of land or sea.”

Péron’s animal descriptions are deeply marked by Buffon’s evocative style. Thus, in relation to seals, Buffon emphasises their “docility”, the gentleness of their gaze, and their capacity to “express sentiments of affection and attachment” (“The look of this animal is gentle, and its nature is not fierce; its eyes are attentive and … express at least its feelings of affection and attachment…”). For his part, Péron, describing the sea elephants of King Island, likewise stresses their “so gentle and kindly physiognomy”: “Sea elephants are of an extremely gentle and docile nature… all these animals have a physiognomy so gentle and so good…”
